INTERNATIONAL ECONOMICS

Posted on 2023-04-12 Edited on 2023-04-20

My notes on the 2023 spring International Economics course taught by Professor Hong Ma and Professor Tuo Chen from School of Economics and Management, Tsinghua University.

Lecture 1: Introduction & The Ricardian Model

Why Study Trade: Important, Interesting, and Relevant

Trade in general is beneficial

  • In almost all cases, trade makes the world better off

But not everyone gains from trade

  • Trade not only creates gains, but also causes a re-distribution of wealth
  • winners and losers from globalization

We want to understand who gains, who loses, and why, thus we can make better policy decisions.

How Levels of Technology Shape the World Trade Pattern

Absolute advantage

A country should specialize in what it can produce most efficiently.

Comparative advantage

A country can engage in trade even when her workers are less efficient at producing every single good than workers in other countries.

Ricardian Model

Basic assumptions: 2-2-1 model

  1. Two countries: Home and Foreign
  2. Two goods: Wheat and Cloth
  3. A single factor of production: labor
    • Ignore land and capital for now
    • Home has 25 workers & Foreign has 100 workers
  4. Technology is country-specific
  5. Perfect competition

The Home Country

Labor productivity: The marginal product of labor (MPL) describes the extra output obtained by using one more unit of labor.

In Home, one worker produces

  • 4 bushels of wheat, so \(MPL_W=4\)
  • 2 yards of cloth, so \(MPL_C=2\)

Given MPLs, we could calculate the opportunity costs, which describes the amount of cloth that must be given up to obtain one more unit of wheat.

The opportunity cost of wheat is \(\frac{MPL_C}{MLP_W}\) and the opportunity cost of cloth is \(\frac{MPL_W}{MLP_C}\). We should note that the higher is MPL of its own, the lower is its opportunity cost.

Home Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)

The set of production possibilities is all the different combinations of wheat and cloth that Home can produce. \[ \frac{Q_W}{MPL_W}+\frac{Q_C}{MPL_C}\leq 25 \] The production possibility frontier (PPF) describes the most cloth that Home can produce for each unit of wheat produced. That is, PPF gives the production capacity of the home country. \[ \frac{Q_W}{MPL_W}+\frac{Q_C}{MPL_C}=25 \] The economic interpretation of the slope: \[ \text{Slope of PPF}=-\frac{MPL_C}{MPL_W} \] It is the ratio of marginal products of the two goods and the slope also equals the opportunity cost of wheat.

Understanding Home PPF

  • What happens if the labor force doubles
  • What happens if there is technological progress in cloth production

Prices of goods & Wages

In competitive markets, firms hire workers up to the point at which the hourly wage equals the value of one more hour of production, which equals MPL times the price of the good. \[ \begin{aligned} \text{Wage}_C&=P_C\cdot MPL \\ \text{Wage}_W&=P_W\cdot MPL \end{aligned} \] Since labor can move freely between industries, it will continue moving until the equalization of wages across industries, which resulted in that labor is hired up to the point where \[ \text{Wage}_C=\text{Wage}_W \] Rearranging terms, we see that \[ \frac{P_W}{P_C}=\frac{MPL_C}{MPL_W}=\frac{1}{2} \] This equation says that the relative price of wheat (on the left) and opportunity cost of wheat (on the right) must be equal in the no-trade equilibrium.

Therefore, in competitive markets, price reflects the opportunity cost of a good.

Deriving the Equilibrium

Adding a relative demand curve will pin down the domestic equilibrium. More generally, to derive demand curve, we need consumers' preferences.

  1. Representative consumer: For simplicity, we assume all workers have the same preference.
  2. Use indifference curves to represent preference.
  3. Each indifference curve shows the combinations of two goods, such as wheat and cloth, that a person or economy can consume and be equally satisfied.
  4. All points on an indifference curve have the same level of utility and points on higher indifference curves have higher utility.

Autarkic (Closed-Economy) Equilibrium

With perfectly competitive markets, the country will produce at its highest level of utility within the limits of the PPF.

Three equilibrium conditions:

  1. Consumers (workers) maximize their utility
  2. Workers maximize their revenue (wage)
  3. Consumption = Production

The Foreign Country

Assume a Foreign worker can produce one bushel of wheat or one yard of cloth, i.e., \(MPL^*_W=1\) and \(MPL^*_C=1\). So Foreign's technology is inferior to Home's in both goods, which means Home has absolute advantage in making both goods.

Assume there are 100 workers available in Foreign.

  • The Foreign PPF is a straight line between 100 yards of cloth and 100 bushels of wheat.
  • The slope of the PPF equals the negative of the opportunity cost of wheat.
  • The opportunity cost is amount of cloth that must be given up (1 yard) to obtain 1 more bushel of wheat.

Comparative Advantage vs. Absolute Advantage

Home has absolute advantage in making both goods. But Foreign has a comparative advantage in making cloth because the opportunity cost of cloth in Foreign is less than that in Home.

Determining the Pattern of International Trade

The no-trade relative price equals its opportunity cost of production:

  • The relative price of wheat in Foreign is \(P_W^*/P_C^*=1\).
  • The relative price of wheat in Home is \(P_W/P_C=1/2\).

Who Exports What

Both countries export the good for which they have the comparative advantage, i.e., Home exports wheat and Foreign exports cloth.

How would Prices Change

The relative price of wheat to cloth will become the same in both countries:

  • First, it depends on world supply and demand.
  • Second, it will be between the no-trade price in the two countries \(P_W^*/P_C^*\) and \(P_W/P_C\).

For now assume the free-trade price of \(P_W/P_C=2/3\), then we can take this price and see how trade changes production and consumption in each country.

Where is the Production Point

Home producers of wheat can earn more than the opportunity cost of wheat by selling it to Foreign. Labor resources will be shifted toward the production of wheat and increase its production. \[ \frac{\text{Wage}_W}{\text{Wage}_C}=\frac{P_W\cdot MPL_W}{P_C\cdot MPL_C}=\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{2}>1 \] Therefore, Home's workers will want to work in wheat and no cloth will be produced.

With trade, Home will be fully specialized in wheat production, and will export wheat for cloth.

Where is the Consumption Point

The world price line \((\text{slope}=-2/3)\) shows the range of consumption possibilities that a country can achieve by specializing in one good and engaging in international trade.

Change in Production and Consumption

Home consumption occurs at the point where utility is maximized, i.e., at the tangent intersection with indifference curve of highest possible utility.

With trade, the optimal consumption bundle does not need to be the same as the optimal production bundle. And Home obtains a higher utility with international trade than in the absence of trade.

Pattern of Trade and Gains from Trade

Foreign consumption occurs at point C*, and total exports are 40 yards of cloth in exchange for imports of 60 bushels of wheat. Relative to its pre-trade wheat and cloth consumption (point A*), Foreign consumes 10 more bushels of wheat and 10 more yards of cloth.

  • trade triangular

Lessons from the Ricardian Model so far

The pattern of trade is determined by comparative advantage

  • Each country is specializing in and exporting the good for which it has the comparative advantage.
  • complete specialization in this case

There are gains from trade for both countries

  • Indeed, every person gains from trade in this model \[\begin{align} \text{Home wage}=4\text{ bushels of wheat}\text{ or }\frac{8}{3}\text{ yards of cloth}\\ \text{Foreign wage}=\frac{3}{2}\text{ bushels of wheat}\text{ or }1\text{ yard of cloth} \end{align}\]

Wages are determined by absolute advantage

  • Foreign workers earn less than Home workers as measured by their ability to purchase either good.

Application: Labor Productivity and Wages

Labor productivity is measured by value-added per hour of work and can be compared with the wages paid in manufacturing in various countries.

The trends in labor productivity and wages can also be graphed over time.

The general upward movement in labor productivity is matched by upward movements in wages, as predicted by the Ricardian model.

Solving for International Prices

Two approaches

In reality world price is determined by a market for exports and imports.

  • We will derive a Home export supply curve which shows the amount of wheat that Home wants to export at various relative prices.
  • Similarly we will derive a Foreign import demand curve which shows the amount of wheat that it will import at various relative prices.
  • We put these together to get the trade equilibrium.

Home Export Supply

When the relative price of wheat is \(\frac{1}{2}\), Home will export any amount of wheat between 0 and 50 bushels.

For relative prices above \(\frac{1}{2}\), Home exports more than 50 bushels. For example, at the relative price of \(\frac{2}{3}\), Home exports 60 bushels of wheat.

Foreign Import Demand

When the relative price of wheat is \(1\), Foreign will import any amount of wheat between 0 and 50 bushels.

For relative prices below \(1\), Foreign imports more than 50 bushels. For example, at the relative price of \(\frac{2}{3}\), Foreign imports 60 bushels of wheat.

International Trade Equilibrium & World Market

Putting together the Home export supply curve and the Foreign import demand curve for wheat, the world equilibrium is established at the point where the relative price of wheat is \(\frac{2}{3}\).

  • Equilibrium price of \(\frac{2}{3}\) and trade of 60 bushels of wheat.
  • This is the amount that clears the world market.
  • Desired sales of Home equal the desired purchases by Foreign

We derive world relative supply and world relative demand, then naturally we get an equilibrium.

Or in a more general case, we could solve for the equilibrium conditions for both wheat and cloth.

Classic Trade Theory: the Ricardian Model

Trade happens between two countries due to their differences in technology/productivity.

  • Two countries (H & F) with 2 goods/industries (1 & 2)

  • One factor: \(L^H\) and \(L^F\) (move across industries freely, but not across border)

  • Technology: unit labor requirement \(a_i^k\) for \(k=H,F\) and \(i=1,2\), which denotes how much labor is required to product a single product

    Note that \(MPL_i^k=(a_i^k)^{-1}\). Let's assume \[ \frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}<\frac{a_1^F}{a_2^F} \]

Defining the PPF for H country

Let \(Q_1^H\) (\(Q_2^H\)) be the number of good 1 (good 2) produced by country H.

Then the set of production possibilities is \[ \{Q_1^H,Q_2^H|a_1^HQ_1^H+a_2^HQ_2^H\leq L^H\} \] Then the production possibility frontier is \[ Q_2^H(Q_1^H)=\frac{L^H}{a_2^H}-\frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}Q_1^H \]

Note that the absolute value of slope \(\frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}\) is the opportunity cost of good 1

Price and Specialization

Now suppose the price of good 1 and good 2 in H are \(p_1^H\) and \(p_2^H\).

Assume perfect competition and labor moves frictionlessly between the two industries, so \[ \frac{p_1^H}{a_1^H}=w_1^H\equiv w_2^H=\frac{p_2^H}{a_2^H} \] Thus, we have \[ \frac{p_1^H}{p_2^H}=\frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H} \] Similarly for country F, we have \[ \frac{p_1^F}{p_2^F}=\frac{a_1^F}{a_2^F} \] Recall that \[ \frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}<\frac{a_1^F}{a_2^F} \] Thus in autarky, we have \[ \frac{p_1^{AH}}{p_2^{AH}}=\frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}<\frac{a_1^F}{a_2^F}=\frac{p_1^{AF}}{p_2^{AF}} \]

where we use superscript A to denote Autarky

Preference

To determine the equilibrium we need to specify the preferences of workers.

For simplicity, we assume a representative consumer with the utility \[ W=U(C_1^H,C_2^H) \]

  • \(C_1^H\) is the quantity of good 1 consumed in H
  • \(C_2^H\) is the quantity of good 2 consumed in H
  • \(U\) is some given function
  • \(W\) is a number that tells us the total utility of the representative consumer given her consumption bundle \(C_1^H\) & \(C_2^H\)

We shoule assume that \(\frac{\partial U(C_1^H,C_2^H)}{\partial C_1^H}\geq0\) and \(\frac{\partial U(C_1^H,C_2^H)}{\partial C_2^H}\geq0\)

Leontief utility

\[ U(C_1^H,C_2^H)=\min\{C_1^H,\beta C_2^H\} \]

Cobb-Douglas utility

\[ U(C_1^H,C_2^H)=(C_1^H)^\beta(C_2^H)^{1-\beta} \]

Autarkic Equilibrium

With information on production (supply) and utility (demand), we are ready to define the equilibrium in autarky.

  1. Demand: consumer preferences or utility function \(U(C_1^H,C_2^H)=(C_1^H)^\beta(C_2^H)^{1-\beta}\)
  2. Supply: production function or technology (workers ULR or MPL) \(a_1^H,a_2^H\)
  3. Endowment: total labor \(L^H\)

Equilibrium Conditions

  1. consumers maximize their utility
  2. workers maximize their income (wage)
  3. market clears: consumption is equal to production (closed-economy)

the market equilibrium price \[ \frac{p_1^H}{p_2^H}=\frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H} \] To pin down the equilibrium quantity of good 1 and good 2, we need to specify the utility function.

Suppose it takes the Cobb-Douglas preference \[ U(C_1^H,C_2^H)=(C_1^H)^\beta(C_2^H)^{1-\beta}\quad\beta\in(0,1) \]

Utility Maximization

Maximize utility subject to the worker's budget constraint \[ \max_{C_1^H,C_2^H}(C_1^H)^\beta(C_2^H)^{1-\beta}\quad \text{s.t.}\quad p_1^HC_1^H+p_2^HC_2^H\leq Y^H \] where \(Y^H=w^HL^H\) is the income of worker

The Lagrangian is \[ (C_1^H)^\beta(C_2^H)^{1-\beta}-\lambda(p_1^HC_1^H+p_2^HC_2^H-Y^H) \] the first order conditions (FOCs) are \[ \beta(C_1^H)^{\beta-1}(C_2^H)^{1-\beta}=\lambda p_1^H\quad \text{and}\quad (1-\beta)(C_1^H)^\beta(C_2^H)^{-\beta}=\lambda p_2^H \] combining both equations to get rid of \(\lambda\) \[ \beta p_2^HC_2^H=(1-\beta)p_1^HC_1^H \] with the budget constraint \(p_1^HC_1^H+p_2^HC_2^H=Y^H=w^HL^H\), we have \[ p_2^HC_2^H=(1-\beta)Y^H\quad \text{and}\quad p_1^HC_1^H=\beta Y^H \]

That is, with Cobb-Douglas preference, consumer always spend a constant fraction of income on each good.

Combining production and consumption conditions (prices & wages determination):

\[ \begin{aligned} p_1^H&=\frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}\cdot p_2^H\\ w^H&=\frac{p_1^H}{a_1^H}=\frac{p_2^H}{a_2^H} \end{aligned} \]

We get the optimal consumption bundel: \[ \begin{aligned} C_2^H&=(1-\beta)\frac{w^HL^H}{p_2^H}=(1-\beta)\frac{L^H}{a_2^H}\\ C_1^H&=\beta\frac{w^HL^H}{p_1^H}=\beta\frac{L^H}{a_1^H} \end{aligned} \] And since market clears, the optimal consumption bundle is also the optimal production bundle.

Introducing Trade

  • Two countries (H & F) with 2 goods/industries (1 & 2)

  • Labor endowment: \(L^H\) and \(L^F\) (move across industries freely, but not across border)

  • Technology: unit labor requirement \(a_i^k\) for \(k=H,F\) and \(i=1,2\)

  • Preference: representative consumer in each country \[ W_k=U_k(C_1^k,C_2^k)\quad\text{for}\quad k=H,F \]

Notice that the above variables are given (predetermined). They are information that are available to us to solve for the equilibrium.

These variables are called exogenous variables, including

  • technology (the labor productivities)
  • preference (the utility function)
  • endowment (in the Ricardian case, it includes only labor)

Trade Equilibrium Conditions

  1. Optimal production: Given relative prices, workers choose what to produce in order to maximize their income. \[ Q_i\ \text{for}\ k=H,F\ \text{and}\ i=1,2 \]

  2. Optimal consumption: Given relative prices and the income earned from production, the representative consumers choose what to consume in order to maximize their utility. \[ C_i\ \text{for}\ k=H,F\ \text{and}\ i=1,2 \]

  3. Market clearing: Total world consumption of each good equals total world production.

Comparative Advantage

Recall that we have assumed \[ \frac{a_1^H}{a_2^H}<\frac{a_1^F}{a_2^F} \] That means the opportunity cost of producing good 1 (in terms of good 2) in country H is lower than country 2.

  • country H has a comparative advantage in producing good 1
  • country F has a comparative advantage in producing good 2
  • note that \(a_1^H>a_1^F\) means that country H has a absolute advantage in producing good 1

Trade Equilibrium: Relative Prices

With trade, the relative price of good 1 to good 2 is the same in both countries. \[ \frac{p_1^H}{p_2^H}=\frac{p_1^F}{p_2^F} \]

Lecture 2: Specific Factors Model

Proposed originally by Jocob Viner, also called Ricardo-Viner model.

Viner used the model to study the welfare of migrant workers from rural to city during the industrial revolution.

Further developed by Samuelson, Jones, and Mussa

Key ideas

  1. Labor is not the only factor of production
  2. Some factors are sector-specific or region-specific, meaning that they are not mobile across sectors / regions.
    • E.g. Machinery producing cars cannot be used to produce garments
  3. Price changes due to trade may generate winners & losers
    • Overall free trade will enhance welfare

Model Setup

  • Two countries: Home & Foreign
  • Two goods/industries
    • M: manufacturing, eg. cloth
    • A: agriculture, eg. wheat
  • Three factors of production
    • Labor \(L\): move across industries freely
    • Capital \(K\): specific to manufacturing
    • Land \(T\): specific to agriculture

So this is a short-run model

Production: Outputs and Inputs

  • Output: let \(Q_M^H\) (\(Q_A^H\)) be the number of good M (good A) produced
  • Input Labor: let \(L_M^H\) (\(L_A^H\)) denotes the number of labor used in production of good M (good A)
  • Input Capital: let \(K^H\) denotes the capital specifically used in production of good M
  • Input Land: let \(T^H\) denotes the land specifically used in production of good A

Then the production functions are \(Q_M^H=Q(L_M^H,K^H)\) for good M and \(Q_A^H=Q_(L_A^H,T^H)\) for good A.

Diminishing marginal product of factors

The marginal product of labor declines as the amount of labor used in the industry increases.

Recall that MPL measures the extra output obtained by using one more unit of labor. \[ MPL_M=\frac{\partial Q_M^H}{\partial L_M^H}>0 \]

Diminishing \(MPL_M\) \[ \frac{\partial^2 Q_M^H}{(\partial L_M^H)^2}<0 \]

As more labor is used, manufacturing output increases, but it does so at a diminishing rate. More precisely, MPL is the slope of the production curve.

Similarly we can derive \(MPL_A\), \(MPK\), and \(MPT\)

We also assume constant return to scale \[ Q(aL_M,aK)=aQ(L_M,K) \]

Defining the PPF

Recall that PPF measures the maximum amount of good M that can be produced given the number of good A.

In the Ricardian model, the MPL is constant in both sectors, so the PPF is a straight line.

In this specific factors model, we need to account for the diminishing MPLs.

The key is to use the labor market clearing condition \[ L^H=L_M^H+L_A^H \]

The Slope of the PPF

Suppose one unit of \(L\) moves from Agriculture to Manufacturing, then agricultural output falls by \(MPL_A\) and manufacturing output rises by \(MPL_M\).

\[ \text{Slope of PPF}=-\frac{MPL_A}{MPL_M}=-\frac{\frac{\partial Q_A(L_A,T)}{\partial L_A}}{\frac{\partial Q_M(L_M,K)}{\partial L_M}} \]

If \(L\) continues to move to manufacturing, \(MPL_A\) rises and \(MPL_M\) falls.

Wages

A worker considers which sector to work, taking as given the prices of both goods.

  • wage in M sector \(W_M=P_M\times MPL_M\)
  • wage in A sector \(W_A=P_A\times MPL_A\)

Wages in Equilibrium

In equilibrium, workers earn the same wage in M & A sectors. \[ P_M\times MPL_M=W_M=W_A=P_A\times MPL_A \] Thus \[ \frac{MPL_A}{MPL_M}=\frac{P_M}{P_A} \] i.e. relative goods price reflects the opportunity cost: labor moves b/w M and A sectors until the relative price is equal to the relative marginal product.

Equilibrium

  1. workers maximize their income (wage)
  2. consumers maximize their utility
  3. market clears: consumption is equal to production

Preferences

To determine the equilibrium, we assume a representative consumer with the utility \[ W=U(C_M,C_A) \] Assume that \[ \frac{\partial U(C_M^H,C_A^H)}{\partial C_M^H}>0\quad \text{and}\quad \frac{\partial U(C_M^H,C_A^H)}{\partial C_A^H}>0 \]

Defining The Autarkic Equilibrium

Exogenous variables are what we already know as parameters or pre-existing conditions

  1. production functions: \(Q_M(L_M,K)\) and \(Q_A(L_A,T)\)
  2. consumer preferences: \(U(C_M,C_A)\)
  3. factor endowments: \(L,T,K\)

Endogenous variables are what need to be solved as model outcomes in equilibrium

  1. Quantity produced: \(Q_M^*\) and \(Q_A^*\)
  2. Quantity consumed: \(C_M^*\) and \(C_A^*\)
  3. Relative prices: \(\frac{P_M}{P_A}\)
  4. Returns to factors: wage & rentals

Returns to Factors of Production

Given the equilibrium, we could calculate the income obtained by each factor of production.

  • Labor: wage per worker \(W=P_M\times MPL_M=P_A\times MPL_A\)

  • Capital: rental rate \(R_K=P_M\times MPK_M\) or \(R_K\times K=P_M\times C_M^*-W\times L_M\)

Lecture 4: The Heckscher-Ohlin Model

A Quick Review

The Ricardian model is very stylized.

  • Labor is the only factor of production
  • Countries differ in their technology (MPL).
  • Each country is exporting the good for which it has the comparative advantage with complete specialization.
  • Everyone gains from trade.

The specific-factors model is powerful.

  • It allows multiple factors of production \(L,K,T\).
  • Overall each country gains from trade, but within country some win and some lose.
  • It is a short-run model: some factors are specific and not mobile b/w sectors.

Introducing the H-O Model

The H-O model allows for

  • Multiple factors (resources) that are mobile across sectors.
    • Thus it is a long-run model
  • Trade can happen between countries with the identical technologies but different resources / endowments.
  • How trade affects different types of factors in this long-run model
    • Trade generates winners and losers (owner of factors)
    • But countries still overall gain from trade

Model Setup

  • Two countries: Home H & Foreign F
    • Assume both countries can have the same level of technologies.
    • They have different endowments: H is relatively more abundant in capital. \[ \frac{K_H}{L_H}>\frac{K_F}{L_F} \]
  • Two goods/industries: a capital intensive good C (e.g. computers) & a labor intensive good S (e.g. shoes)
    • Goods move freely between countries.
  • Two factors of production: Labor L & Capital K
    • Both factors can move between industries freely.
    • In the equilibrium, no factor price difference between sectors.
    • Finally assume factors cannot move between countries (i.e. no immigration / Foreign Direct Investment).

Shoes Production is Labor Intensive

Shoes production is labor-intensive, meaning that it requires more labor per unit of capital to produce shoes than computers. \(\frac{L_S}{K_S}>\frac{L_C}{K_C}\) in both countries H and F.

Production: Outputs and Inputs

  • Input of Labor: let \(L_C^H\) (\(L_S^H\)) denotes the number of labor used in production of good C (good S)
    • Labor market clears: \(L^H=L_C^H+L_S^H\)
  • Input of Capital: let \(K_C^H\) (\(K_S^H\)) denotes the capital used in production of good C (good S)
    • Capital market clears: \(K^H=K_C^H+K_S^H\)
  • Output: let \(Q_C^H\) (\(Q_S^H\)) be the number of good C (good S) produced

Then the production functions are \[ \begin{aligned} Q_C^H=Q_C^H(L_C^H,K_C^H)\quad&\text{for good C}\\ Q_S^H=Q_S^H(L_S^H,K_S^H)\quad&\text{for good S} \end{aligned} \]

Constructing the PPF

Because Home is capital abundant and computers are capital intensive, the Home PPF is skewed toward computers.

Wages Determination

Labor (L) considers which sector to work, taking as given the prices of both goods, in equilibrium, we have

  • wages are equalized between sectors: \(W_C=W_S\)

Similarly capital (K) moves freely between two sectors, in equilibrium, we have

  • capital rental rates are equalized between sectors: \(R_C=R_S\)

Firms maximize their profits (e.g. computer sector) \[ \pi_C=\max_{L,K}(P_C\cdot Q_C(L,K)-W_C\cdot L_C-R_C\cdot L_C) \]

  • FOC on Labor: \(P_C\cdot \frac{\partial Q_C}{\partial L_C}-W_C=0\)
  • FOC on Capital: \(P_C\cdot \frac{\partial Q_C}{\partial K_C}-R_C=0\)

thus we have \[ P_C\cdot MPL_C=W_C\qquad P_C\cdot MPK_C=R_C \] which means \[ \frac{MPL_C}{MPK_C}=\frac{W_C}{R_C} \]

Similarly the profit maximization in the Shoes sector derives \[ \frac{MPL_S}{MPK_S}=\frac{W_S}{R_S} \]

Example

Suppose the production functions are \[ \begin{aligned} Q_C=(L_C)^\delta(K_C)^{1-\delta}\quad&\text{for good C}\\ Q_S=(L_S)^\beta(K_S)^{1-\beta}\quad&\text{for good S} \end{aligned} \] we assume \(\delta<\beta\), which means good S is labor intensive

  • Then the marginal product of labor in Computer \[ MPL_C=\delta(L_C)^{\delta-1}(K_C)^{1-\delta} \]

  • Then the marginal product of capital in Computer \[ MPL_K=(1-\delta)(L_C)^\delta(K_C)^{-\delta} \]

Profit maximization implies the wage determination function \[ \frac{MPL_C}{MPK_C}=\frac{\delta}{1-\delta}(\frac{L_C}{K_C})^{-1}=\frac{W_C}{R_C} \] This explains why the wage/rental curve is downward sloping.

Similarly derive the wage/rental curve for the shoes sector \[ \frac{\beta}{1-\beta}(\frac{L_S}{K_S})^{-1}=\frac{W_S}{R_S} \]

Recall that in equilibrium, it has to be true that \[ W_C=W_S\qquad R_C=R_S \] so we have \[ \frac{\delta}{1-\delta}(\frac{L_C}{K_C})^{-1}=\frac{\beta}{1-\beta}(\frac{L_S}{K_S})^{-1} \] thus \[ (\frac{L_S}{K_S})/(\frac{L_C}{K_C})=\frac{\beta}{1-\beta}/\frac{\delta}{1-\delta}>1 \] which means that Shoes production is labor intensive while Computer Production is capital intensive

Prices of Factors

In equilibrium, labor market clears and capital market clears \[ L=L_C+L_S\qquad K=K_C+K_S \]

Thus the relative supply of labor and capital in Home is \[ \frac{L}{K}=\frac{L_C+L_S}{K_C+K_S}=\frac{L_C}{K_C}\times\frac{K_C}{K}+\frac{L_S}{K_S}\times\frac{K_S}{K} \]

The RHS means that the overall relative labor demand is a weighted average of the labor to capital ratio in both sectors.

In equilibrium, the relative labor supply (LHS) equals the relative labor demand (RHS).

Wage Determination: Factor Prices and Goods Prices

Suppose both Computer and Shoes are produced

Then workers have to be paid equally in both sectors in equilibrium \[ W_C=W_S\iff P_C\cdot MPL_C=P_S\cdot MPL_S\iff\frac{P_C}{P_S}=\frac{MPL_S}{MPL_C} \]

Similarly for the rental rates to be equalized between sectors \[ R_C=R_S\iff P_C\cdot MPK_C=P_S\cdot MPK_S\iff\frac{P_C}{P_S}=\frac{MPK_S}{MPK_C} \]

Effect of Computer Price Increase

Suppose the relative price of Computers \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}\) increases

What happens to the allocation of labor

  • Since \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}=\frac{MPL_S}{MPL_C}\) so \(\frac{MPL_S}{MPL_C}\) must increase.
  • Recall MPL is diminishing when L increases, it must be that some labor moves from Shoes to Computer
  • That means \(L_C\) increases while \(L_S\) decreases.

What happens to the allocation of capital

  • Since \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}=\frac{MPK_S}{MPK_C}\) so \(\frac{MPK_S}{MPK_C}\) must increase.
  • Recall MPK is diminishing when K increases, it must be that some capital moves from Shoes to Computer
  • That means \(K_C\) increases while \(K_S\) decreases.

Thus when \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}\) increases, both labor and capital will move from Shoes to Computer production.

How will this affect the relative returns to factors (wages and rental rates)

Note that in the equation \[ \frac{L}{K}=\frac{L_C+L_S}{K_C+K_S}=\frac{L_C}{K_C}\times\frac{K_C}{K}+\frac{L_S}{K_S}\times\frac{K_S}{K} \] \(\frac{K_C}{K}\) increases while \(\frac{K_S}{K}\) decreases

And given any \(\frac{W}{R}\) we know \(\frac{L_C}{K_C}<\frac{L_S}{K_S}\), which implies that when the weight of the Computer sector increases, the total relative labor demand will drop.

The corespongding result is that an increase in the relative price of a good will increase the relative factor price of the factor that it uses intensively.

Winners and Losers from a Price Change

Who benefits from the increase in the relative price of Computers

  • \(\frac{W}{R}\) decreases as a result
  • both \(\frac{L_C}{K_C}\) and \(\frac{L_S}{K_S}\) increase

What happens to \(MPL_i\) and \(MPK_i\)

  • Recall in equilibrium we have \(\frac{MPL_i}{MPK_i}=\frac{W}{R}\)
  • It must be true that MPL in both sectors has fallen, and MPK in both sectors has risen.

What can a worker buy with income \(W\)

  • If she only buys Computers, she can buy \(\frac{W}{P_C}\) where \(W=P_C\cdot MPL_C\)
  • If she only buys Shoes, she can buy \(\frac{W}{P_S}\) where \(W=P_S\cdot MPL_S\)
  • Since MPL falls in both sectors, a worker is unambiguously worse off.

What can a capital owner buy with income \(R\)

  • If she only buys Computers, she can buy \(\frac{R}{P_C}\) where \(R=P_C\cdot MPK_C\)
  • If she only buys Shoes, she can buy \(\frac{R}{P_S}\) where \(R=P_S\cdot MPK_S\)
  • Since MPK rises in both sectors, a capital owner is unambiguously better off.

Stolper-Samuelson Theorem

In the long run, when all factors are mobile, an increase in the relative price of a good will increase the real earnings of the factor used intensively in the production of that good and decrease the real earnings of the other factor.

What does Stolper-Samuelson Theorem imply in reality

Think of USA and China, the Stolper-Samuelson theorem predicts that when USA opens to trade and faces a higher relative price of computers, the real rental on capital in the US rises and the real wage in the US falls. In China, the changes in real factor prices are just the reverse: workers gain from trade.

Testing the Heckscher-Ohlin Theorem

The first test of the Heckscher-Ohlin theorem was performed by economist Wassily Leontief in 1953.

Leontief supposed correctly that in 1947 the United States was abundant in capital relative to the rest of the world.Thus from the Heckscher-Ohlin theorem, Leontief expected that the United States would export capital-intensive goods and import labor-intensive goods.

What Leontief actually found, however, was just the opposite.

  • the capital-labor ratio for U.S. imports was higher than the capital-labor ratio found for U.S. exports

This finding contradicted the Heckscher-Ohlin theorem and came to be called Leontief's paradox.

Explanations

  • U.S. and foreign technologies are not the same, in contrast to what the H-O theorem and Leontief assumed.
  • By focusing only on labor and capital, Leontief ignored land abundance in the United States.
  • Leontief should have distinguished between skilled and unskilled labor, because it would not be surprising to find that U.S. exports are intensive in skilled labor.
  • The data for 1947 may be unusual because World War Ⅱ had ended just two years earlier.
  • The United States was not engaged in completely free trade, as the Heckscher-Ohlin theorem assumes.

Factor Endowments in the New Millennium

To determine whether a country is abundant in a certain factor, we compare the country's share of that factor with its share of world GDP.

  • If its share of a factor exceeds its share of world GDP, then we conclude that the country is abundant in that factor.
  • If its share in a certain factor is less than its share of world GDP, then we conclude that the country is scarce in that factor.

A good explanation for the Leontief Paradox

  • labor is highly productive in the United States and less productive in the rest of the world
  • If that is the case, then the effective labor force in the United States is much larger than just count people.

Define the effective factor endowment as the actual amount of a factor found in a country times its productivity \[ \text{Effective Factor Endowment}=\text{Actual factor endowment}\cdot\text{Factor productivity} \]

  • \(\text{Effective labor force}=\text{Labor force}\cdot\text{Its productivity}\)
  • \(\text{Effective R&D scientists}=\text{Actual R&D scientists}\cdot\text{R&D spending per scientist}\)

Long-Run Effect of Immigration

In the long run, all factors are free to move between industries. Thus we will use the H-O model to analyze.

Recall the labor market clearing condition \[ \frac{L}{K}=\frac{L_C+L_S}{K_C+K_S}=\frac{L_C}{K_C}\times\frac{K_C}{K}+\frac{L_S}{K_S}\times\frac{K_S}{K} \]

An increase in labor force increases relative labor supply (i.e. the LHS \(\frac{L}{K}\)). And we know that \(\frac{L_C}{K_C}<\frac{L_S}{K_S}\). Hence to make the RHS of the equation increase, we need to increase capital share allocated to the Shoes sector.

Moving capital into the Shoes sector increases the production of shoes, and decreases the production of computers.

The economy can absorb the extra amount of a factor by increasing the output of the industry using that factor intensively and reducing the output of the other industry.

So immigration has no effect on factor prices – the factor price insensitivity

  • Factor prices \((w,r)\) are determined by the goods prices \((P_C,P_S)\).
  • So if goods prices have no change, then factor prices would not change either, and the sectoral level relative demand for labor \((\frac{L_C}{K_C},\frac{L_S}{K_S})\) would have no change.
  • To see this rather counter-intuitive results, we have to use a model.

Factor price determination

The relative demand for labor/capital in C & S \[ \frac{W_C}{R_C}=\frac{\delta}{1-\delta}(\frac{L_C}{K_C})^{-1}\qquad\frac{W_S}{R_S}=\frac{\beta}{1-\beta}(\frac{L_S}{K_S})^{-1} \]

Wage and Rent equalization between two sectors

Workers have to be paid equally in both sectors in equilibrium \[ \frac{P_C}{P_S}=\frac{MPL_S}{MPL_C}=\frac{\beta(\frac{L_S}{K_S})^{\beta-1}}{\delta(\frac{L_C}{K_C})^{\delta-1}} \]

Then substitute \(\frac{L_C}{K_C}\) and \(\frac{L_S}{K_S}\) using \(\frac{W}{R}\) \[ \frac{P_C}{P_S}=\frac{\beta(\frac{1-\beta}{\beta}\frac{W}{R})^{1-\beta}}{\delta(\frac{1-\delta}{\delta}\frac{W}{R})^{1-\delta}}=\frac{\beta^\beta(1-\beta)^{1-\beta}}{\delta^\delta(1-\delta)^{1-\delta}}(\frac{W}{R})^{\delta-\beta} \]

Implications

  • \(\frac{W}{R}\) is uniquely determined by \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}\).
  • A increase in \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}\) will lead to a decrese in \(\frac{W}{R}\) since \(\delta<\beta\).
  • Rental equalization gives the same results.

More General Cases

We could write the cost function of producing C & S as \[ C_C(w,r)=wL_C+rK_C\qquad C_S(w,r)=wL_S+rK_S \]

By firms' cost minimization and by perfect competition, we have \[ C_C(w,r)=P_C\qquad C_S(w,r)=P_S \]

It tells us that under certain conditions, factors prices are not determined by domestic market conditions, rather, they are determined by international goods prices.

Rybczynski Theorem

In the Heckscher-Ohlin model with two goods and two factors, an increase in the amount of a factor (given constant prices and technology) will increase the output of the industry using that factor intensively and decrease the output of the other industry.

We could write production as function of factor and factor prices \[ K_S=(\frac{1-\beta}{\beta}\frac{W}{R})^\beta Q_S\qquad L_S=(\frac{1-\beta}{\beta}\frac{W}{R})^{\beta-1} Q_S\\ K_C=(\frac{1-\delta}{\delta}\frac{W}{R})^\delta Q_C\qquad L_C=(\frac{1-\delta}{\delta}\frac{W}{R})^{\delta-1} Q_C \] Then apply the factor market clearing conditions \[ a_C^LQ_C+a_S^LQ_S=L\qquad a_C^KQ_C+a_S^KQ_S=K \]

Note that \((a_S^L,a_S^K)\) and \((a_C^L,a_C^K)\) are the unit factor requirement for producing shoes / computers, depending on \(\frac{W}{R}\).

And we know that \(\frac{W}{R}\) solely depends on \(\frac{P_C}{P_S}\).

We solve this system of equations and get \[ Q_C=\frac{a_S^LK-a_S^KL}{a_C^Ka_S^L-a_C^La_S^K}\\ Q_S=\frac{a_C^KL-a_C^LK}{a_S^La_C^K-a_S^Ka_C^L} \] And explain the Ryb effect that expansion in \(L\) (immigration) leads to expansion in \(Q_S\) and contraction in \(Q_C\)

To diversify means \(Q_C>0\) and \(Q_S>0\)

  • To make sure \(Q_C>0\), we need \[ \frac{K}{L}>\frac{a_S^K}{a_S^L}=\frac{K_S}{L_S} \]
  • To make sure \(Q_S>0\), we need \[ \frac{K}{L}<\frac{a_C^K}{a_C^L}=\frac{K_C}{L_C} \]

Diversification cone: As long as a country's endowment \(\frac{K}{L}\) lie in between \(\frac{K_S}{L_S}\) and \(\frac{K_C}{L_C}\), the country would optimally choose to diversify (i.e. produce both products).

Now suppose we divide factors between H and F by a point in the Box Diagram and do NOT allow for free move of factors across countries.   • F country now has an excess supply of shoes, so it will export shoes and import computers.

How capital can move from one country to another through foreign direct investment (FDI).

FDI in the Short Run: Specific-Factors Model

Effect of FDI on the Wage

As a result of the shift in \(P_M\cdot MPL_M\), the equilibrium wage increases, from \(W\) to \(W'\).

More workers are drawn into the manufacturing industry, and the labor used there increases.

Effect of FDI on the Industry Output

An FDI inflow and the shift in \(P_M\cdot MPL_M\) will cause workers to be pulled out of agriculture, and since there is no change in the amount of land used there, output of the agriculture industry must fall.

Since both capital and labor used in manufacturing increasing, output in manufacturing also increases.

Effect of FDI on the Rentals

Rental on land

  • With an inflow of FDI, fewer workers are employed in agriculture, and each acre of land is associated with fewer workers.
  • The value of marginal product of land, \(R_T=P_A\cdot MPT_A\), falls.
  • If \(MPT_A\) falls and \(P_A\) remains unchanged, then land rental must fall.

Rental on capital

  • With an inflow of FDI, more workers are employed in manufacturing.
  • Then how does the value of the marginal product of capital, or \(R_K=P_M\cdot MPK_M\), change due to the inflow of FDI
    • Note that both labor and capital increase in manufacturing sector
  • By carefully tracing through how the capital-labor ratio in manufacturing is affected, we conclude that the rental on capital declines when the capital stock increases through FDI.

FDI in the Long Run

To analyze the impact of FDI in the long run, we continue with the same assumptions as before.

  • There are two industries, computers and shoes.
  • There are two factors of production, labor and capital.
  • Computers are capital intensive and shoes are labor intensive, meaning that \(\frac{K_C}{L_C}>\frac{K_S}{L_S}\).

Now we consider an increase in Capital due to FDI.

Effect of FDI on Outputs and Factor Prices

As the Rybczynski Theorem states, the increase in capital has increased the output of the capital-intensive industry and reduced the output of the labor-intensive industry.

This change in output is achieved without a change in the capital-labor ratio in either industry. Because capital-labor ratios are unchanged, the wage and the rental on capital are also unchanged.

The Factor Price Insensitivity Result

In the long run model, an inflow of either factor of production will leave factor prices unchanged.

A Model of Offshoring

To develop the model of offshoring, we need to distinguish all the activities used to produce and market a good or service.

The Value Chain of a Product

  • Any product has many different activities involved in its manufacture. Here are activities ranked by ORDER in production.
  • R&D – Component production – Assembly – Marketing and sales

However, for offshoring, it is more useful to look at the activities according to the ratio of skilled to unskilled labor they use.

  • We start with the less skilled activities and move to more complex components and then onto the more skilled labor activities.
  • Assembly – Component production – Marketing and sales – R&D

Relative Wage of Skilled Workers

Assumption One: Foreign wages are less than those at Home \[ W'_L<W_L\qquad\text{and}\qquad W'_H<W_H \]

Assumption Two: The relative wage of low-skilled labor is lower in Foreign than at Home \[ \frac{W'_L}{W'_H}<\frac{W_L}{W_H} \]

Cost of Capital and International Trade

Labor costs are lower in Foreign, for both H & L, so offshoring all activities seems cost-saving.

However, the firm must also take into account extra costs of doing business there

  • Higher prices to build a factory or costs of production
  • The extra costs of communication or transportation
  • Cost of transportation and tariffs when shipping the offshored components back

Therefore, domestic firms face a trade-off

  • the savings from lower wages vs. the extra costs of capital and trade \[ W_L\cdot L+W_H\cdot H\quad\text{vs.}\quad W'_L\cdot L'+W_H'\cdot H'+\text{ Extra Cost} \]
  • Cost saving is larger to offshore low-skill intensive activity
  • There is a point on the value chain, below which all activities are offshored to Foreign and above which all activities are performed at Home.

To simplify, we assume the extra cost of offshoring is the same for all activities.

  • apply uniformly across all the activities in the value chain

What if the capital required to offshore drops

Capital required to offshore drops due to investment cost reduction, relaxation of foreign restrictions, import tariff reduction by home country, or reduction in transportation.

Offshoring cost declines, leading to more activities being offshored. These activities are more skill-intensive than the activities formerly done in Foreign, but less skill-intensive than the activities now done at Home.

Change in Home Labor Demand and Relative Wage

With greater offshoring from Home to Foreign, some of the activities requiring less skill that were formerly done at Home are now done abroad.

It follows that the relative demand for skilled labor at Home increases, and the relative wage rises. * Change in Foreign Labor Demand and Relative Wage

The relative demand for skilled labor in Foreign also increases because the activities shifted to Foreign are more skill intensive than those formerly done there.

It follows that the relative wage for skilled labor in Foreign also rises.

Offshoring on Inequality

In Feenstra-Hanson (1996) model, both countries experience an increase in the relative wage of skilled labor due to increased outsourcing.

As activities in the middle of the value chain are shifted from Home to Foreign, they raise the relative demand for skilled labor in both countries.

These newly-offshored activities are the least skill-intensive of those formerly done at Home but the most skill-intensive of those done in Foreign.

A Simplified Offshoring Model

Suppose there are only two activities

  • components production
  • research and development (R&D)

Each activity uses skilled and unskilled labor

  • components production uses unskilled labor intensively
  • R&D uses skilled labor intensively

The costs of capital are equal in both activities We want to compare the no-trade situation to an equilibrium with trade through offshoring, to determine if there are overall gains from trade.

No-Trade Equilibrium for the Home Firm

The PPF shows the combinations of components and R&D that can be undertaken by a firm with a given amount of labor and capital. In the absence of offshoring, the firm produces at point A, using quantities QC of components and QR of R&D to produce amount \(Y_0\) of the final good.

The line tangent to the isoquant through point A measures the value that the firm puts on components relative to R&D, or their relative price \((\frac{P_C}{P_R})^A\).

Amount \(Y_1\) of the final good cannot be produced in the absence of offshoring because it lies outside the PPF for the firm.

Equilibrium with Offshoring

Suppose the firm can import and export its production activities through offshoring.

The quantity of the final good is no longer constrained by the Home PPF.

  • A higher level of production (isoquant) is possible by trading intermediate activities.
  • We will refer to the relative price of the two activities that the Home firm has available through outsourcing as their world relative price \((\frac{P_C}{P_R})^W\).

With a lower relative wage of unskilled labor in Foreign, the components assembly will also be cheaper in Foreign. It follows that Home will want to outsource components, which are cheaper abroad, while Home firms will be exporting R&D, which is cheaper at Home.

Given the firms production abilities from the isoquants, we can see the firm can now produce Y1 using corresponding levels of R&D and Components. The increase of final goods produced \((Y_0-Y_1)\) is a measure of the gains from trade to the Home firm from offshoring.

Because more of the final good is produced with the same overall amount of skilled and unskilled labor available in Home, the Home company is more productive.

  • Both its costs of production, and the price of the final product falls.
  • So the gains for this company are also spread to consumers.

Therefore, when comparing a no-trade situation to the equilibrium with offshoring, and assuming that the world relative price differs from that at Home, there are always gains from offshoring.

  • See Grossman and Rossi-Hansberg (2008 AER) for a generalization of the productivity effect due to offshoring.

More liberalization: Fall in the Price of Components

If the relative price of components falls from \((\frac{P_C}{P_R})^{W_1}\) to \((\frac{P_C}{P_R})^{W_2}\), then the Home firm will do even more R&D and less components production. The increase in the terms of trade allows the Home firm to produce output \(Y_2\), and the gains from trade are higher than in the initial offshoring equilibrium.

A rise in the terms of trade indicates that a country is getting a higher price for its exports, or paying a lower price for its imports. – both benefit the country

More liberalization: Fall in the Price of R&D

A fall in the relative price of R&D makes the world price line steeper \((\frac{P_C}{P_R})^{W_3}\). As a result, the Home firm reduces its R&D activities and increases its components activities.

At the new world relative price, the Home firm faces a terms-of-trade loss and can no longer export each unit of R&D for as many components as it could in the initial offshoring equilibrium. The final good output is reduced from \(Y_1\) to \(Y_3\). Notice that the final good output \(Y_3\) is still higher than output without trade, \(Y_0\).

After the fall in the relative price of R&D, there are still gains from trade relative to no-trade, but losses relative to the initial offshoring equilibrium.

Lecture 7: Scale Economy and Monopolistic Competition

For some time now there has been considerable skepticism about the ability of comparative cost theory to explain the actual pattern of international trade. Neither the extensive trade among the industrial countries, nor the prevalence in this trade of two-way exchanges of differentiated products, make much sense in terms of standard theory. As a result, many people have concluded that a new framework for analyzing trade is needed.

What we have learned so far – trade happens due to difference

  • Ricardian model – trade of different goods between countries with different productivity
  • H-O model – trade of different goods between countries with different endowment
  • These models explains inter-industry trade

The first wave of globalization was characterized by such inter-industry trade. Countries exported goods that were very different to what they imported.

  • E.g. England exchanged machines for Australian wool and Indian tea.

In the second wave of globalization we are seeing a rise in intra-industry trade. Larger (and richer) countries tend to trade more with each other.

  • a low index (down to 0%) indicates that the good is either imported or exported but not both

The Grubel-Lloyd Index \[ 0\le GL_i=\frac{(X_i+M_i)-|X_i-M_i|}{X_i+M_i}=1-\frac{|X_i-M_i|}{X_i+M_i}=\frac{\min\{X_i,M_i\}}{\frac{1}{2}(X_i+M_i)}\le1 \]

So why do developed countries export/import the same category of products to/from similar countries - Similar in endowment, technology/productivity, income level (consumer preference/taste)

Introducing the Krugman Model

a new explanation for trade based on the model of monopolistic competition and increasing return to scale

Pioneered by Professor Paul Krugman: Major work in late 1970s & Won the Nobel Prize in 2008

What is Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition allows for imperfect competition – firms can influence the price they charge

Other imperfect competition models include Monopoly, Duopoly, and Oligopoly

Monopolistic competition has four key features/assumptions

Assumption One: The goods produced by different firms are differentiated.

Assumption Two: There are many firms in the industry.

  • If the number of firms is \(N\), then \(D/N\) is the share of demand that each firm faces when the firms are all charging the same price.
  • When only one firm changes its price, however, it will face a flatter demand curve.

Assumption Three: Firms produce using a technology with increasing returns to scale (IRS) (also called Economies of Scale).

  • Increasing returns to scale cause average costs to fall as the quantity produced increases.

Assumption Four: Free Entry

  • Because firms can enter and exit the industry freely, monopoly profits are zero in the long run.
  • Whenever the price charged is above average costs, then a firm earns monopoly profits.
  • Firms will enter as long as it is possible to make monopoly profits (i.e, when price is above average costs).
  • And the more firms that enter, the lower profits per firm become.
  • Profits for each firm end up as zero in the long run, just as in perfect competition.

Trade under Monopolistic Competition

Short-run Equilibrium without trade

The short-run equilibrium under monopolistic competition is the same as a monopoly equilibrium.

The firm chooses to produce the quantity \(Q_0\) at which the firm's marginal revenue \(MR_0\) equals its marginal cost \(MC\). The price charged is \(P_0\). Because price exceeds average cost, the firm makes monopoly profits.

Long-run Equilibrium without trade

Drawn by the possibility of making profits in the short-run equilibrium, new firms enter the industry and the firm's demand curve shifts to the left and becomes more elastic (i.e. flatter).

The long-run equilibrium under monopolistic competition occurs at the quantity \(Q_1\) where the marginal revenue curve \(MR_1\) (associated with demand curve) equals marginal cost. At that quantity, the no-trade price equals average costs.

In the long-run equilibrium, firms earn zero monopoly profits and there is no entry or exit. The quantity produced by each firm is less than in short-run equilibrium. \(Q_1\) is less than \(Q_0\) because new firms have entered the industry.

With a greater number of firms and hence more varieties available to consumers, the demand for each variety \(d_1\) is less than \(d_0\). The demand curve \(D/N^A\) shows the no-trade demand when all firms charge the same price.

Equilibrium with Free Trade

Assume Home and Foreign are exactly the same.

  • Same number of consumers, same technology and cost curves, and therefore same number of firms in the no-trade equilibrium.

Without Economies of Scale, there would be no reason for trade.

  • Under the Ricardian model, countries with identical technologies would not trade.
  • Under the Heckscher-Ohlin model, countries with identical factor endowments would not trade.

Under monopolistic competition with increasing returns to scale, two identical countries will still engage in trade.

Short-run Equilibrium with trade

We start with each country in long-run equilibrium without trade.

  • The number of firms in the no-trade equilibrium in each country is \(N^A\).

When trade opens, the number of customers doubles. Since there are twice as many consumers, but also twice as many firms, the ratio stays the same, the demand per product stays the same at the no-trade price.

The product varieties each consumer can buy also double. With the greater number of varieties available, the demand for each individual variety will be more elastic.

When trade is opened, the larger market makes the firm's demand curve \(d_2\) more elastic (with marginal revenue curve \(MR_2\)). The firm chooses to produce the quantity \(Q_2\) at which marginal revenue equals marginal costs. The price charged is \(P_2\). With sales \(Q_2\) at price \(P_2\), the firm makes profits since price is greater than AC.

When all firms lower their prices to \(P_2\), however, the relevant demand curve is \(D/N^A\), which indicates that they can sell only \(Q_2'\) at price \(P_2\). At this short-run equilibrium, price is less than average cost and all firms incur losses. As a result, some firms are forced to exit the industry.

Compared with the long-run equilibrium without trade, \(d_3\) (along with \(MR_3\)) has shifted out as domestic firms exited the industry and has become more elastic due to the greater total number of varieties with trade since \(2N^T>N^A\). Compared with the long-run equilibrium without trade, the trade equilibrium has a lower price and higher sales by all surviving firms.

Gains from Trade

The long-run equilibrium has two sources of gains from trade for consumers

A drop in price

  • A result of lower average cost (i.e. increased productivity) of the surviving firms coming from IRS.
  • Firms produce more since they face a larger market.

An increase in variety

  • Although there are fewer product varieties made within each country (by fewer firms), consumers have more product variety because they can choose products of the firms from both countries after trade.
  • \(N^T<N^A\quad\text{but}\quad2N^T>N^A\)

Although consumers gain lower prices and more varieties available, it is not clear how workers benefit from it.

  • Some gain as wage increases
  • Some lose as firms exit after trade due to competition since it takes time to find a new job

The Krugman Model

How to model imperfect competition

Key requirement for imperfect competition – firms choose prices

Easiest way

  • Suppose there are many firms, none of which has a substantial market share.
  • This is known as monopolistic competition.

Assume demand for a firm's product is

  • Decreasing in the price that the firm charges
  • Increasing in the price that other firm's charge
  • Decreasing in the number of other firms

Dixit-Stiglitz preferences

Dixit and Stiglitz developed a very simple mathematical framework.

Without going into details, they yield the following demand for firm's \(\varphi\) product \[ q(\varphi)=p(\varphi)^{-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1} \] where \(P\) is the Dixit-Stiglitz Price Index \[ P\equiv(\int_{\Omega}p(\varphi)^{1-\sigma}d\varphi)^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}} \]

  • \(\sigma>1\)​ is the elasticity of substitution
  • \(\Omega\) is the set of all the firms that produce
  • \(Y\) is the income of the consumers

Consumer welfare is simply their real income \[ W=\frac{Y}{P} \]

Model Parameters and Outcomes

Exogenous model parameters

  • the income \(Y\)
  • the marginal cost \(c\) & the fixed cost \(f\)

Endogenous model outcomes

  • Prices charged by each firm \(p(\varphi)\)
  • Quantities produced by each firm \(q(\varphi)\)
  • Set of firms that produce \(\Omega\)

Equilibrium

Equilibrium conditions

  1. Given every other firm's behavior and consumer demand, each firm chooses its price to maximize its profits.
  2. Consumers maximize their utility given their income.
    • Note that we've already solved this to get the demand function.
  3. In equilibrium, there is free entry, which implies the number of firms are set so that that there are no profits.

Profits

A firm \(\varphi\) chooses how much to produce and what price to charge in order to maximize its profits \(\pi(\varphi)\) \[ \pi(\varphi)=\max_{q(\varphi),p(\varphi)}(p(\varphi)q(\varphi)-cq(\varphi))-f \] subject to the consumer demand function \[ q(\varphi)=p(\varphi)^{-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1} \]

Substituting in the constraint yields a simple maximization problem where the firm just chooses the price \[ \pi(\varphi)=\max_{p(\varphi)}(p(\varphi)^{1-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1}-cp(\varphi)^{-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1})-f \]

Optimal Price

First order condition \[ ((1-\sigma)p(\varphi)^{-\sigma}+\sigma cp(\varphi)^{-\sigma-1})YP^{\sigma-1}=0 \] which gives the solution \[ p(\varphi)=\frac{\sigma}{1-\sigma}c \]

It shows that prices are just a constant mark-up over the marginal cost \(c\)

Price index

If all firms have the same marginal cost, then the price index becomes \[ P\equiv(\int_{\Omega}p(\varphi)^{1-\sigma}d\varphi)^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}}=(\int_{\Omega}(\frac{\sigma}{1-\sigma}c)^{1-\sigma}d\varphi)^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}}=(\frac{\sigma}{1-\sigma})c(\int_{\Omega}d\varphi)^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}}=(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})cN^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}} \] where \(N\equiv \int_{\Omega}d\varphi\) stands for the number (measure) of firms

Firms and welfare

Recall that welfare is the real income \[ W=\frac{Y}{P} \]

Since \(P\equiv(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})cN^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}}\), this implies that welfare can be re-written as \[ W=(\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma})\frac{1}{c}YN^{\frac{1}{\sigma-1}} \]

And since \(\sigma>1\), welfare is increasing in the number of firms \(N\).

Firm profits

Things we know about firms

  • From consumer demand, firms sell a quantity \(q(\varphi)=p(\varphi)^{-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1}\)
  • From profit maximization, firms charge a price \(p(\varphi)=\frac{\sigma}{1-\sigma}c\)

Together (and with a little algebra), this implies their profits are \[ \pi(\varphi)=p(\varphi)q(\varphi)-cq(\varphi)-f=(p(\varphi)^{1-\sigma}-cp(\varphi)^{-\sigma})YP^{\sigma-1}-f=\frac{1}{\sigma}(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})^{1-\sigma}c^{1-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1}-f \]

Recall that the price index can be written as \[ P=(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})cN^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}} \]

Combining the two beautifully yields \[ \pi(\varphi)=\frac{1}{\sigma}\frac{Y}{N}-f \]

Free entry

The free entry condition requires that firms continue to enter until profits go to zero \[ \pi(\varphi)=\frac{1}{\sigma}\frac{Y}{N}-f=0 \] which gives the solution of \(N\) \[ N=\frac{1}{\sigma}\frac{Y}{f} \]

Recap

Every firm produces a differentiated variety and maximizes profits given consumer demand.

  • Equation for profits: \(\pi(\varphi)=\frac{1}{\sigma}\frac{Y}{N}-f\)
  • Equation for price index: \(P=(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})cN^{\frac{1}{1-\sigma}}\)

As the number of firms producing increases, the profits for each firm declines as they face more competition.

  • Implies equilibrium number of firms: \(N=\frac{1}{\sigma}\frac{Y}{f}\)

How to model trade

The simplest way to model trade to assume that firms can sell to a bigger market. \[ Y=Y_1+Y_2 \]

So to examine the welfare effect of trade, we just need to examine how an increase in income affects welfare.

For simplicity, suppose that \(Y_1=Y_2\), so that opening up to trade doubles the income of the market.

Gains from Trade in New Trade Theory

Recall that welfare can be written as \[ W=\frac{Y}{P}=(\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma})\frac{1}{c}YN^{\frac{1}{\sigma-1}} \]

Substituting in the optimal number of firms yields \[ W=(\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma})\frac{1}{c}Y(\frac{1}{\sigma}\frac{Y}{f})^{\frac{1}{\sigma-1}}=(\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma})(\frac{1}{\sigma f})^{\frac{1}{\sigma-1}}\frac{1}{c}Y^{\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}} \]

Note that \(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}>1\), which implies that doubling income more than doubles welfare. Hence, opening up to trade improves the per capita welfare of both countries.

What's going on w.r.t. firms & consumers

  • As the income increases, the profits for any firm also increases (since the demand for their good goes up).
  • In equilibrium, this will cause more firms to enter the market.
  • More firms decreases the price index (because of consumers' love of variety).
  • Hence it is less costly for consumers to get the same amount of welfare.

New new trade theory

  • Pioneered by Marc Melitz (in his PhD dissertation)
    • Enormously influential – has averaged about one citation a day for the past decade
  • In the Krugman framework, all firms are assumed to be identical. Yet in reality, firms differ substantially.
    • In particular, larger firms are much more likely to engage in trade (export).
  • Melitz' major innovation was to take extend the Krugman trade model to allow for firms to vary in their productivities.
  • As we will see, this new new trade theory predicts that trade will increase competition, causing the least productive firms to exit and improving the overall productivity in the economy.

Exporters are the minority

Not all firms export, and exporters are in the minority.

Firms are heterogeneous

Firm Heterogeneity within category

  • vast heterogeneity across plants/firms in productivity (and capital intensity and skill intensity)
  • exporters are more produtive than non-exporters

Firm heterogeneity

Suppose that firms are heterogeneous (i.e. different).

  • the easiest way to model this – suppose that firms differ in their marginal cost \(c\) of production

Furthermore, suppose a firm incurs a fixed cost \(f_{US}\) for selling to the U.S. and \(f_{MEX}\) for selling to Mexico

  • assume \(f_{US}<f_{MEX}\) for U.S. firms

Finally, suppose a firm incurs an entry cost \(f_e\) for producing anything.

Firm heterogeneity and prices

Better firms are more productive (i.e. have lower \(c\)).

Recall from profit maximization that optimal price is \[ p(\varphi)=\frac{\sigma}{1-\sigma}c(\varphi) \] where \(c(\varphi)\) is the marginal cost of the firm producing good \(\varphi\)

Hence more productive firms will charge lower prices.

Note that the old equation for price index no longer holds since price charged varies across firms.

Firm heterogeneity and production

Recall consumer demand function \[ q(\varphi)=p(\varphi)^{-\sigma}YP^{\sigma-1} \] Hence, if they produce, more productive firms will sell greater quantities.

Variable profits (i.e. without fixed costs) are \[ \pi_i(\varphi)=\frac{1}{\sigma}(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})^{1-\sigma}c^{1-\sigma}Y_iP^{\sigma-1}_i \] Hence more productive firms will also have higher profits.

Firm heterogeneity and entry decisions

Profits for selling to country \(i\in\{US,MEX\}\) are \[ \pi_i(\varphi)=\frac{1}{\sigma}(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})^{1-\sigma}c^{1-\sigma}Y_iP^{\sigma-1}_i-f_i \]

Will only sell to the country if \(\pi_i(\varphi)\ge0\) \[ \pi_i(\varphi)=\frac{1}{\sigma}(\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1})^{1-\sigma}c^{1-\sigma}Y_iP^{\sigma-1}_i-f_i\ge0 \] which gives the solution of \[ c\le(\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma})(\frac{Y_i}{\sigma f_i})^{\frac{1}{\sigma-1}}P_i \]

Then for U.S. firms, some (less-productive) firms will only sell domestically, while the most productive firms will export.

Free entry

We can modify the free entry condition to say the expected profits of a firm are equal to zero \[ E(\pi_{US}(\varphi)+\pi_{MEX}(\varphi)-f_e)=0 \] Justify this by assuming that firms pay the entry cost \(f_e\) before realizing their marginal cost \(c\).

This means that some firms can realize that they have very high marginal costs and choose not to produce at all, but still have to pay \(f_e\).

Effect of (more free) trade

Suppose that the cost of exporting to Mexico \(f_{MEX}\) declines, so that the two countries become more integrated.

First, lowering \(f_{MEX}\) causes less productive firms to begin exporting to Mexico.

Second, exporting to Mexico raises these firms profits, which raises the expected profits of all firms.

  • This causes more firms to begin entering the market, which drives down the profits for all firms.
  • Firms that were before indifferent between selling in the U.S. now do not find it profitable to do so.
  • Hence, \(c^*_{US}\) decreases, meaning that the average US productivity of firms producing domestically increases.

Gains from Trade in New New Trade Theory

  • Still gains from trade resulting from increased varieties (as in the new trade theory)
  • In addition, opening up to trade increases the potential profits of firms, which increases the competition amongst entrants.
  • This increased competition amongst entrants means that the least profitable firms no longer produce.
  • Hence, trade increases the average productivity of a country.
