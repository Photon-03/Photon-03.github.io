LINEAR REGRESSION ANALYSIS

Posted on 2023-04-12 Edited on 2023-04-20

My notes on the 2023 spring Linear Regression Analysis course taught by Professor Zaiying Zhou from Center for Statistical Science, Tsinghua University.

Lecture 1: Prelude

Goals of the Course

We will cover simple and multiple regression, one and two-way analysis of variance. The emphasis will be on

  1. conceptual understanding of the models
  2. analysis methods and their intuitions
  3. interpretation of results
  4. proper use of linear model software (R)

Mathematical manipulations are important but not the focus. Intuitive and statistical understanding of the theory is nonetheless required.

History of Regression

Regression-type problems first considered in the 18th century to aid navigation with the use of astronomy. Legendre developed the method of least squares in 1805. Gauss claimed to have developed the method a few years earlier and in 1809 showed that least squares is the optimal solution when the errors are normally distributed.

Used almost exclusively in the physical sciences until later in the 19th century.

The Lurking Problem

Intergenerational transfers passed heritable variation to offspring.

Population dispersion is stable over the short term.

The Experiment

In 1875, Galton started growing sweet peas.

Weights of the daughter seeds (Offspring) against the weights of the mother seeds (Parent).

Regression & Slope

Galton's first documented study of this type suggested a slope of 0.33.

  • Affirmed his basic assumptions concerning the heritability.

This finding is prototypical of regression to the mean.

  • He designated the slope \(r\) for regression.

Generality of Regression Slope

Galton believed he had estimated a single, universal heredity constant: \[ \frac{y-\bar{y}}{S_y}=r\cdot\frac{x-\bar{x}}{S_x} \]

  • Used an estimated value of \(r\).
  • \((S_y/S_x)\) allowed him to adjust the observed slope.
  • Quantile Deviation: semi-interquartile range.

Introducing the Inclined Chutes

Galton called this reversion.

  • Not coincidental but necessary for population stability

His 1877 version of the quincunx, showing:

  • how the inclined chutes compensate for the increase in dispersion below to maintain constant population dispersion
  • how the offspring of two of the upper-level stature groups can be traced through the process to the lowest level

Two Regression Lines Together

regression towards mediocrity

similar results for brothers data

The explanation must be statistical, not biological

Geometric Relationship of the Data

Galton smoothed the counts to make the pattern stand out better.

Could see a rough elliptical contour emerging around the densest portion.

Bivariate Normal Density & Regression Lines

Galton found a theoretical representation for the table – the bivariate normal density.

Two regression lines:

  • the expected height of the mid-parent as a function of the child's height
  • the expected height of the offspring as a function of the mid-parent's height
  • \(E(Y|X=x)\)

Galton's Solution to Darwin's Problem

Regression toward the mean was

  • a necessary requirement for natural selection
  • a simple consequence of the imperfect correlation between parents and offspring

The population dispersion is in an approximate evolutionary equilibrium

  • The movement from the population center toward the extremes was balanced by the movement back.

Galton's Interpretation

\(F=G+V_1,\ S=G+V_2\)

  • \(G\) – an unobserved persistent (genetic) component that blood relatives hold in common with each other
  • \(V_i\) – unobserved transitory (random) components, uncorrelated with \(G\) and with each other

\(E(G)=P\)

\(E(V_1)=E(V_2)=0\)

One Step Further: Multiple Regression

Galton noticed that

  • prior generations could also influence individual characteristics
  • effects are diminishing

He had hit upon the kernel of the idea of multiple regression:

  • A variable may be influenced by a multitude of causes of greater and lesser importance
  • Some of these causes may even overlap with one another

Before Started

Statistics

  1. starts with a problem
  2. proceeds with the collection of data
  3. continues with the data analysis
  4. finishes with conclusions

A common mistake is to plunge into a complex analysis without paying attention to what the objectives are or even whether the data are appropriate for the proposed analysis.

Formulate the Problem Correctly

  • Understand the physical background
  • Understand the objective
  • Make sure you know what the client wants
  • Put the problem into statistical terms

How the Data Were Collected?

  1. Were they obtained via a designed sample survey?

    How the data were collected has a crucial impact on what conclusions can be made.

  2. Is there nonresponse?

    The data you do not see may be just as important as the data you do see.

  3. Are there missing values?

    This is a common problem that is troublesome and time consuming to handle.

  4. How are the data coded?

    In particular, how are the qualitative variables represented?

  5. What are the units of measurement?

  6. Beware of data entry errors and other corruption of the data. Perform some data sanity checks.

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Promoted by John Tukey to encourage statisticians to explore the data, and possibly formulate hypotheses that could lead to new data collection and experiments.

EDA is an approach simple but vital.

  1. to analyzing data sets
  2. to summarize their main characteristics
  3. often with visual methods

EDA in Practice

  • Numerical summaries such as means, standard deviations, maximum and minimum, correlations and whatever else is appropriate to the specific dataset.
  • Graphical summaries
    • For one variable, boxplots, histograms, density plots
    • For two and more variables, scatterplots are standard
    • Interactive and dynamic graphics
  • Look for outliers, data-entry errors, skewed or unusual distributions and structures.

Analysis: Terminologies

\(X\) – predictor, input, independent or explanatory variable

\(Y\) – response, output, dependent variable

\(Y\sim X\) – simple regression

\(Y\sim X_1,\dots,X_p\) – multiple/multivariate regression

A multiple regression has more than one \(X\) in one formula

A multivariate regression has more than one \(Y\), but in different formulae

A multivariate multiple regression has multiple \(X\)'s to predict multiple \(Y\)'s with each \(Y\) in a different formula, usually based on the same data.

Various Forms of Regressions

  1. \(Y\) continuous & \(X\) continuous, discrete or categorical
    • diabetes ~ diastolic + bmi – a multiple regression, only quantitative variables, will be tackled shortly
    • bmi ~ diastolic + test – both quantitative and qualitative predictors, will be considered on analysis of covariance
    • diastolic ~ test – only qualitative predictors, called analysis of variance (ANOVA)
  2. \(Y\) qualitative (categorical, here binary), needs a logistic regression
    • test ~ diastolic + bmi
  3. \(Y\) discrete (count), needs a Poisson regression
    • pregnant ~ diastolic + bmi

Possible Objectives

  • Prediction of future observations
  • Assessment of the effect of, or relationship between, explanatory variables and the response
  • A general description of data structure

Steps in a Regression Analysis

A regression analysis includes the following steps:

  1. Statement of the problem
  2. Selection of potentially relevant variables
  3. Data collection
  4. Model specification
  5. Choice of fitting method
  6. Model fitting
  7. Model validation and criticism
  8. Using the chosen model(s) for the solution of the posed problem

Lecture 2: Simple Linear Regression

Simple Linear Regression Model

  • Model parameters
  • Model assumptions
  • Distribution of error terms

Data for Simple Linear Regression

  • Observe pairs of the variables
  • Each pair \((X_i,Y_i)\), often called a case
    • \(X_i\) – observed explanatory variable
    • \(Y_i\) – observed response variable
  • \((X_1,Y_1),(X_2,Y_2),\dots,(X_n,Y_n)\) – Sample/ Observations/ Data
  • \(n\) – Sample size

Simple Linear Regression Model

\[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i \]

  • \(\beta_0\) is the intercept
  • \(\beta_1\) is the slope
  • \(\varepsilon_i\) is a random error term
    • Mean \(E(\varepsilon_i)=0\) and Variance \(Var(\varepsilon_i)=\sigma^2\)
    • \(\varepsilon_i\) and \(\varepsilon_j\) are uncorrelated
    • \(\sigma^2\) represents intrinsic variability

Simple Linear Regression Model with Normal Error

\(\varepsilon_i\) is normally distributed

\(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2,\dots,\varepsilon_n\) are Independent and Identically Distributed (i.i.d.) as \(N(0,\sigma^2)\).

Model Parameters

Parameters are unknown constants in the model and targets of inference.

  • \(\beta_0\) – the intercept
  • \(\beta_1\) – the slope
  • \(\sigma^2\) – the variance of the error term

Statistical Inference: the process to deduce the properties of a parameter from data

Common Features of Both Models

The model with general error versus the model with normal error \[ Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i \]

  • \(E(Y_i)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+E(\varepsilon_i)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_i\)

  • \(Var(Y_i)=0+Var(\varepsilon_i)=\sigma^2\)

Mean of \(Y_i\) determined by value of \(X_i\). All possible means fall on a line and the \(Y_i\)'s vary about this line

Unique Feature of the Model with Normal Error

The model is equivalent to: \(Y_1,\dots,Y_n\) are independent and for each \(i\) \[ Y_i\sim N(\beta_0+\beta_1X_i,\sigma^2),\ i=1,\dots,n \]

To Summarize: LINE

Linear Function

The mean of the response, \(E(Y_i)\), at each value of the predictor, \(X_i\), is a Linear function of the \(X_i\).

Independent

The errors, \(\varepsilon_i\), are Independent.

Normally Distributed

The errors, \(\varepsilon_i\), at each value of the predictor, \(X_i\), are Normally distributed.

Equal variances (denoted \(\sigma^2\))

The errors, \(\varepsilon_i\), at each value of the predictor, \(X_i\), have Equal variances \(\sigma^2\).

Estimation of Parameters

  • Method of least squares
  • Maximum likelihood

The Least Squares Principle

For each \(i\)

  • Observed response – \(Y_i\)
  • Response predicted by model – \(\beta_0+\beta_1X_i\)
  • Difference – \(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i\)
  • Squared diff – \((Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)^2\)

For all cases

  • Sum of squares – \(\sum_{i=1}^n(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)^2\)

Least Squares (LS) Principle / LS Estimates

\[ \hat \beta_0,\hat \beta_1=\underset{\beta_0,\beta_1}{\mathbb Rm{argmin}}\sum(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)^2 \]

Use Calculus to Solve Minimization

\[ \begin{aligned} b_1&=\hat \beta_1=\frac{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)(Y_i-\bar Y)}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}\\ b_0&=\hat \beta_0=\bar Y-b_1\bar X \end{aligned} \] where \(\bar X=\frac{\sum_iX_i}{n}\) and \(\bar Y=\frac{\sum_iY_i}{n}\)

  • \(b_1\) – estimated slope or an estimator of the slope \(\beta_1\)
  • \(b_0\) – estimated intercept or an estimator of the intercept \(\beta_0\)

Fitted Model and Residuals

Fitted Model \[ \hat Y_i=b_0+b_1X_i \]

  • Predicted or fitted Response – \(\hat Y_i\)
  • Observed Response – \(Y_i\)
  • Residual – \(e_i\) \[ e_i=Y_i-\hat Y_i=(\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i)-(b_0+b_1X_i)\approx \varepsilon_i \]

Residuals reflect error terms when fitted model is good, are highly useful for studying whether a given regression model is appropriate for the data at hand.

Properties of Residuals

Recall that \(\hat \beta_0,\hat \beta_1\) minimize \[ Q=\sum(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)^2 \] Normal equations: \[ \begin{cases} \frac{\partialial Q}{\partialial \beta_0}=-2\sum_{i=1}^n(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)=0\\ \frac{\partialial Q}{\partialial \beta_1}=-2\sum_{i=1}^nX_i(Y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)=0 \end{cases} \] Replace \(\beta_0,\beta_1\) by \(b_0,b_1\), that is \[ \begin{cases} \sum_{i=1}^ne_i&=0\\ \sum_{i=1}^nX_ie_i&=0 \end{cases} \]

Estimation of Variance \(\sigma^2\)

The Degree of Freedom of \(e_1,\dots,e_n\) is \(df_E=n-2\)

Therefore \[ s^2=\hat \sigma^2=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^ne_i^2}{n-2}=\frac{SSE}{df_E}=MSE \] where Sum of Squared Errors \(SSE=\sum_{i=1}^n(Y_i-\hat Y_i)^2\) and \(MSE\) stands for Mean of Squared Errors

Properties of LS estimates

  • Unbiased

    \(E(b_0)=\beta_0,\ E(b_1)=\beta_1,\ E(s^2)=E(MSE)=\sigma^2\)

  • Optimal (most accurate, minimum variance)

    \(b_0,b_1\) – best linear unbiased estimator for \(\beta_0,\beta_1\)

    \(s^2\) – best among quadratic estimators for \(\sigma^2\)

  • Residuals are linearly dependent

    \(\sum_{i=1}^ne_i=0,\ \sum_{i=1}^nX_ie_i=0\)

    \(\sum_{i=1}^nY_i=\sum_{i=1}^n\hat Y_i,\ \sum_{i=1}^n\hat Y_ie_i=0\)

  • The regression line \(y=b_0+b_1x\) pass \((\bar X,\bar Y)\)

Residual Plot

The residuals not only tell about the errors, but also how well the model fits data.

Residual plot – plot residuals against predictor variable / fitted values

Useful for checking assumptions and model diagnostics

QQ plot / Probability Plot

Assessing whether or not a data set follows a given distribution \(F\)

  • \(Y\) axis – ordered response values
  • \(X\) axis – corresponding quantiles of \(F\)

The points should form approximately a straight line

Departures from this straight line indicate departures from the specified distribution \(F\)

Draw a Normal Probability Plot

  1. Order the data to get ordered statistics \(X_{(1)},\dots,X_{(n)}\)
  2. For \(j=1,\dots,n\) specify the data quantile \(p_j=\frac{j-0.5}{n}\)
  3. Find \(Z_j=\Phi^{-1}(p_j)\) where \(\Phi\) is the CDF of the standard normal distribution
  4. Plot \(X_{(j)}\) against \(Z_j\)

Maximum Likelihood Principle / Estimation

Under normal errors, the distributional model \[ Y_i\sim N(\beta_0+\beta_1X_i,\sigma^2) \]

Density function: \[ f_i(y|\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\{-\frac{(y-\beta_0-\beta_1X_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\} \]

Likelihood function: \[ L_i(\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2|Y_i)=f_i(Y_i|\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2) \]

Joint likelihood function \[ L(\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2)=L_1\times L_2\times \cdots\times L_n \]

Thus \[ \begin{aligned} \hat \beta_0^{ml},\hat \beta_1^{ml},\hat \sigma^{2,ml}&=\underset{\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2}{\mathbb Rm{argmax}}\ L(\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2)\\ &=\underset{\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2}{\mathbb Rm{argmin}}\ (-\log L(\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma^2)) \end{aligned} \]

Use Calculus to Solve Minimization

\[ \hat \beta_0^{ml}=b_0,\ \hat \beta_1^{ml}=b_1\\ \hat \sigma^{2,ml}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^ne_i^2}{n}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(Y_i-\hat Y_i)^2}{n}\neq s^2 \]

ML estimates rely on distributional assumptions, LS estimates do not.

In general, MLEs and LSEs are different.

Lecture 3: Statistical Inference & Power

Review: Basics of Statistical Inference

  • Sampling distribution
  • Testing hypotheses
  • Constructing confidence intervals

Using Normal Mean as an Example

Sample, Model, and Parameters \[ X_1,\dots,X_n\overset{i.i.d}{\sim}N(\mu,\sigma^2) \] Estimates – Sample mean & Sample variance \[ \begin{aligned} \hat \mu&=\bar X=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^nX_i}{n}\\ \hat \sigma^2&=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar X)^2}{n-1}=s^2\ \text{and}\ df_{s^2}=n-1\\ \hat \sigma&=s=\sqrt{s^2} \end{aligned} \]

Sampling Distribution of \(\bar X\) and Standard Error

The distribution of \(\bar X\) is called its Sampling Distribution \[ \bar X\sim N(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}) \]

Normal with same mean but a much reduced variance

\[ \begin{aligned} \sigma^2(\bar X)&=E(\bar X-\mu)^2=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\\ \hat \sigma^2(\bar X)&=s^2(\bar X)=\frac{s^2}{n} \end{aligned} \]

Standard Error (SE) of \(\bar X\) \[ SE_{\bar X}=s(\bar X)=\sqrt{s^2(\bar X)}=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Typical/Standard deviation/error of \(\bar X\) from its target \(\mu\), telling the accuracy of \(\bar X\)

Chi-squared Distribution

Sample of Standard Normal Random Variables:

\[ Z_1,Z_2,\dots,Z_k\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(0,1) \]

Sum of Squared Standard Normals:

\[ U=Z_1^2+Z_2^2+\dots+Z_k^2 \]

The distribution of \(U\) is defined to be a chi-squared distribution with degree of freedom \(k\) – \(\chi_k^2\) \[ \begin{aligned} E(U)&=k\\ Var(U)&=2k \end{aligned} \]

Sampling Distribution of \(s^2\)

We can show that \[ \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma^2}=Z_1^2+Z_2^2+\dots+Z_{n-1}^2 \] where \[ Z_1,Z_2,\dots,Z_{n-1}\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(0,1) \] Therefore \[ \frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2_{n-1} \]

After scaling, \(s^2\) follows Chi-squared distribution with \(df=n-1\).

Fisher's Theorem

\(\bar X\) and \(s^2\) are independent if the population model is normal

Student's \(t\)-distribution

Consider \[ Z_0,Z_1,Z_2,\dots,Z_k\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(0,1) \] Then \[ U=Z_1^2+Z_2^2+\dots+Z_k^2\sim\chi^2_k \] And \(Z_0,U\) are independent with each other. \[ T=\frac{Z_0}{\sqrt{U/k}} \] The distribution of \(T\) is defined to be student's \(t\)-distribution with degree of freedom \(k\) – \(t_k\)

\(t\)-statistic and \(t\)-distribution

How far away is sample mean \(\bar X\) from its target in terms of Standard Error (SE) \[ T=\frac{\bar X-\mu}{SE_{\bar X}}=\frac{\bar X-\mu}{s/\sqrt n}=\frac{\frac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}}{\frac{\sqrt{s^2/n}}{\sigma/\sqrt n}}=\frac{\frac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}}{\sqrt{\frac{(n-1)s^2}{\sigma^2(n-1)}}}\sim t_{n-1} \]

Hypothesis Testing

\[ H_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1 \]

Errors

  • Type I Error \[ \alpha=\text{Prob}(\text{Type I Error})=\text{Prob}(\text{reject }H_0|H_0\text{ true}) \]
  • Type II Error \[ \beta=\text{Prob}(\text{Type II Error})=\text{Prob}(\text{fail to reject }H_0|H_1\text{ true}) \]

The power of a test \[ 1-\beta,\quad \text{which depends on }H_1 \]

Choose a decision rule such that first \(\alpha\) is controlled at level \(\alpha_0\) (significance level), and second \(\beta\) is made as small as possible.

Two-sided Test for Normal Mean

\[ X_1,\dots,X_n\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(\mu,\sigma^2) \]

Hypotheses \[ H_0:\mu=\mu_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0 \]

Test statistic \[ T=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}\overset{H_0}{\sim}t_{n-1} \] Denote the calculated \(T\) as \(t_0\)

Decision Rule 1 – The critical value approach \[ \text{Reject }H_0\text{ if}\quad|t_0|>t_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}\triangleq t^* \]

Decision Rule 2 – The P-value Approach \[ \text{Reject }H_0\text{ if}\quad P(T\ge|t_0|)+P(T\le-|t_0|)<\alpha \]

Confidence Intervals

\[ X_1,\dots,X_n\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(\mu,\sigma^2) \]

How about the Interval Estimator \[ (\bar X-c,\bar X+c) \] such that \[ P(\mu\in(\bar X-c,\bar X+c))=1-\alpha \]

Confidence is placed on the procedure, and used to describe the long-run behavior.

If data is non-normal, the procedure only approximates according to central limit theorem, then the confidence level and the coverage probability may not be exact

Confidence Interval (Interval estimator) for \(\mu\)

We have \[ T=\frac{\bar X-\mu}{s/\sqrt n}\sim t_{n-1} \] thus \[ P(-t_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}<\frac{\bar X-\mu}{s/\sqrt n}<t_{n-1,1-\alpha/2})=1-\alpha \] And we get the \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) Confidence Interval for \(\mu\) \[ (\bar X-t_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt n},\bar X+t_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt n}) \]

  • Center – \(\bar X\)
  • Margin of Error – \(t^*\cdot SE(\bar X)\)
  • Confidence Level – \(1-\alpha\)

Hypothesis Testing via Confidence Intervals

Confidence Interval (CI) approach to testing \[ H_0:\mu=\mu_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0 \]

Construct a \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) CI - If the CI contains \(\mu_0\), do not reject \(H_0\). - If the CI does not contain \(\mu_0\), reject \(H_0\).

The CI approach to testing is equivalent to the critical value and P-value approach to testing two-sided hypothesis.

When testing a one-sided hypothesis at the \(\alpha\) level, use a \(100(1-2\alpha)\%\) confidence interval.

Statistical Inference

  • Statistical Inference for \(\beta_1\)
  • Statistical Inference for \(\beta_0\)
  • For Both \(\beta_1\) and \(\beta_0\): Gauss-Markov Theorem
  • Joint Estimation of \(\beta_1\) and \(\beta_0\)

Motivation in the First Place

The reason for interest in testing whether or not \(\beta_1=0\) is that, when \(\beta_1=0\), there is no linear association between \(Y\) and \(X\).

Inferences Concerning \(\beta_1\)

LS estimator (or estimate) of \(\beta_1\) \[ b_1=\frac{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)(Y_i-\bar Y)}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2} \] Sampling distribution of \(b_1\) representing the uncertainty for estimating \(\beta_1\) \[ \begin{aligned} b_1&=\frac{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)(Y_i-\bar Y)}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}\\ &=\frac{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)Y_i}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}\\ &=\frac{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)(\beta_0+\beta_1X_i+\varepsilon_i)}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}\\ &=\beta_1+\frac{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)\varepsilon_i}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2} \end{aligned} \] Let \(S_{XX}=\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2\) \[ b_1\sim N(\beta_1,\sigma^2_{b_1}) \] where \(\sigma^2_{b_1}=\sigma^2/S_{XX}\) or \(\sigma_{b_1}=\sigma/\sqrt{S_{XX}}\)

Significance Test for \(\beta_1\)

\[ H_0:\beta_1=0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\beta_1\neq0 \]

The evidence/information regarding \(\beta_1\) is summarized by the value of \(b_1\)

The uncertainty of \(b_1\) as estimate of \(\beta_1\) is represented by the variance or standard deviation of \(b_1\)

Standard Error of \(b_1\) \[ s_{b_1}=s/\sqrt{S_{XX}} \] where \[ s=\sqrt{\hat \sigma^2}=\sqrt{\frac{\sum_i(Y_i-\hat Y_i)^2}{n-2}} \]

The evidence/information concerning \(H_0\) is summarized by the test statistic \[ T=\frac{b_1-0}{s_{b_1}}\overset{H_0}{\sim}t_{n-2} \] Under \(H_0\) the sampling distribution of \(T\) is a student's \(t\)-distribution with \(df=n-2\)

Decision rule at \(\alpha\) – Reject \(H_0\) if the calculated statistic \[ |t_0|>t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2} \] The P-value approach can also be used

For one-sided tests or test \(H_0:\beta_1=c\), the test statistic and decision rules should be modified accordingly.

Six Possible Outcomes

If we don't reject \(H_0\), any of the following three realities are possible:

  1. We committed a Type II error. That is, in reality \(\beta_1\ne0\) and our sample data just didn't provide enough evidence to conclude that \(\beta_1\ne0\).
  2. There really is not much of a linear relationship between \(X\) and \(Y\).
  3. There is a relationship between \(X\) and \(Y\) – it is just not linear.

If we do reject \(H_0\), any of the following three realities are possible:

  1. We committed a Type I error. That is, in reality \(\beta_1=0\) but we have an unusual sample that suggests that \(\beta_1\ne0\).
  2. The relationship between \(X\) and \(Y\) is indeed linear.
  3. A linear function fits the data okay, but a curved (curvilinear) function would fit the data even better.

Confidence Interval for \(\beta_1\)

Suppose \(\beta_1\) is the true slope. Then \[ \frac{b_1-\beta_1}{s_{b_1}}\sim t_{n-2} \] According to \[ P(\frac{|b_1-\beta_1|}{s_{b_1}}<t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2})=1-\alpha \] We get the \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) Confidence Interval for \(\beta_1\) \[ (b_1-t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{b_1},b_1+t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{b_1}) \]

Testing \(H_0:\rho=0\)

Let \((x_i,y_i)\) be a bivariate normal random sample of size \(n\) \[ E(Y|X=x)=(\mu_Y-\rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}\mu_X)+\rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}x=\beta_0+\beta_1x \]

  • \(b_1=r\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}\)

Testing \(H_0:\rho=0\) is equivalent to testing \(H_0:\beta_1=0\)

Note that \[ t=\frac{r}{\sqrt{1-r^2}}\sqrt{n-2}\sim t_{n-2} \]

cor.test(x, y)

More appropriate when it is not obvious which variable is the response

Inferences Concerning \(\beta_0\)

\[ b_0=\bar Y-b_1\bar X \]

We can show that \(Cov(\bar Y,b_1)=0\) and \[ b_0\sim N(\beta_0,\sigma^2_{b_0}) \] where \[ \sigma^2_{b_0}=\sigma^2(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{\bar X^2}{S_{XX}}) \]

Standard error of \(b_0\) \[ s_{b_0}=s\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}+\frac{\bar X^2}{S_{XX}}} \]

\(t\) statistic \[ \frac{b_0-\beta_0}{s_{b_0}}\sim t_{n-2} \]

Hypothesis Testing and CI for \(\beta_0\)

Significance test \[ H_0:\beta_0=0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\beta_0\neq0 \] Reject \(H_0\) if \[ \frac{|b_0|}{s_{b_0}}>t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2} \]

For one-sided tests and test \(H_0:\beta_0=c\), the test statistic and decision rules can be derived accordingly.

\(100(1-\alpha)\%\) Confidence Interval for \(\beta_0\) \[ (b_0-t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{b_0},b_0+t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{b_0}) \]

Remarks

Issues related to design (i.e. where to collect data)

  • Scope – the range of the explanatory variable
  • Optimal allocation of \(X_1,\dots,X_n\) \[ \min\sigma^2_{b_1}=\frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2} \]
  • Sample size determination

Inference concerning \(\beta_0\) usually not of interest, because shift in \(X\) cause shift in \(\beta_0\) and \(X=0\) may not be in the scope.

If errors are not normal but are relatively symmetric, the tests and CIs are reasonable approximations, and get improved when sample size increases.

OLS Estimators Are BLUE

Finally we establish the fundamental optimality property of the OLS estimators.

For an independent random sample \((Y_1,\dots,Y_n)\) from a population with mean \(\mu=E(Y)\), the sample mean \(\bar Y\) was shown to be a best linear unbiased (BLU) estimator of \(\mu\).

This optimality property turns out to be shared by the least-squares estimators \((b_0,b_1)\) above. This result, known as the Gauss-Markov Theorem, provides the single strongest justification for linear least-squares estimation.

Joint Inference of \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\)

Confidence intervals are used for a single parameter

Confidence region for two or more parameters \[ P((\beta_0,\beta_1)\in S\subset R^2)=100(1-\alpha)\% \]

Since \[ (b_0,b_1)^T\sim N((\beta_0,\beta_1)^T,\sigma^2\Sigma_{2\times2}) \] the natural (i.e. smallest) confidence region is an ellipse

Textbook considers rectangles – region formed from the product orunion of two separate confidence intervals

Need to adjust confidence level of each CI so that the region has proper \(1-\alpha\) level

Bonferroni Inequality and Correction

Individual CIs \[ P(\beta_0\in CI_0)=1-\alpha\qquad P(\beta_1\in CI_1)=1-\alpha \] Joint confidence region \[ S=CI_0\times CI_1=\{(\beta_0,\beta_1):\beta_0\in CI_0\ \text{and}\ \beta_1\in CI_1\} \] Confidence level \[ \begin{aligned} P((\beta_0,\beta_1)\in S)&=P((\beta_0\in CI_0)\cap(\beta_1\in CI_1))\\ &=1-P((\beta_0\notin CI_0)\cup(\beta_1\notin CI_1))\\ &<1-P(\beta_0\notin CI_0)\\ &=1-\alpha \end{aligned} \] In order to achieve confidence level at least \(1-\alpha\), set \(\alpha_0=\alpha/2\) \[ P(\beta_0\in CI_0)=1-\alpha_0\qquad P(\beta_1\in CI_1)=1-\alpha_0 \] Thus \[ \begin{aligned} P((\beta_0,\beta_1)\in S)&=P((\beta_0\in CI_0)\cap(\beta_1\in CI_1))\\ &=1-P((\beta_0\notin CI_0)\cup(\beta_1\notin CI_1))\\ &\ge1-(P(\beta_0\notin CI_0)+P(\beta_1\notin CI_1))\\ &=1-2\alpha_0\\ &=1-\alpha \end{aligned} \]

Recall that individually \[ CI_0':b_0\pm t_{n-2,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}}\cdot s_{b_0}\quad\text{for}\quad\beta_0\\ CI_1':b_1\pm t_{n-2,1-\frac{\alpha}{2}}\cdot s_{b_1}\quad\text{for}\quad\beta_1 \] Thus jointly for \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\) \[ CI_0':b_0\pm t_{n-2,1-\frac{\alpha}{2\times2}}\cdot s_{b_0}\quad\text{for}\quad\beta_0\\ CI_1':b_1\pm t_{n-2,1-\frac{\alpha}{2\times2}}\cdot s_{b_1}\quad\text{for}\quad\beta_1 \] And we get the Confidence Region for \((\beta_0,\beta_1)\) with level at least \(1-\alpha\) \[ S=CI_0\times CI_1 \]

Definition of Power

The power of a significance test is the probability that the null hypothesis is rejected when, in fact, the alternative hypothesis \(H_1\) is true. \[ \text{Power}=1-P(\text{Type II error}) \]

  • This probability depends on the particular value of the parameter in \(H_1\)
  • Power is often depicted by a curve as the value of the parameter in \(H_1\) changes

Once the significance level (type I error) is set, the test should also be properly powered for the value of \(H_1\), which is of primary interest.

  • neither over-powered nor under-powered

Power for Testing \(\beta_1\)

The test \[ H_0:\beta_1=0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\beta_1\neq0 \] Reject \(H_0\) if \(\frac{|b_1|}{s_{b_1}}>t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\)

Power \[ \text{Power}(\beta_1)=P(\text{Reject }H_0|H_1\text{ holds})=P(\frac{|b_1|}{s_{b_1}}>t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}|\beta_1\ne0) \]

\(\text{Power}(0)=\alpha\) when \(\beta_1=0\), which means that the power becomes the probability of type I error.

A difficulty arises when \(\beta_1\neq0\), that is, the distribution of \[ T=\frac{b_1}{s_{b_1}}\quad \text{where} \quad b_1\sim N(\beta_1,\sigma_{b_1}^2) \] is not centered at \(0\), and \(T\) does not follow \(t_{n-2}\).

Non-central \(t\)-distribution

When \(H_1:\beta_1\ne0\) \[ T=\frac{b_1}{s_{b_1}}=\frac{(b_1-\beta_1)+\beta_1}{s_{b_1}}=\frac{(b_1-\beta_1)/\sigma_{b_1}+\beta_1/\sigma_{b_1}}{s_{b_1}/\sigma_{b_1}} \] follows non-central distribution \(t_{n-2,\delta}\) with the non-centrality parameter \[ \delta=\frac{\beta_1}{\sigma_{b_1}} \]

Assuming \(\sigma_{b_1}^2\)

In order to calculate \(\text{Power}(\beta_1)\) for various values of \(\beta_1\), we need to know or assume \(\sigma_{b_1}^2\) \[ \sigma_{b_1}^2=\frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2} \]

  • Need to know the design \(X_1,X_2,\dots,X_n\) and error variance \(\sigma^2\) from prior knowledge or pilot study

Then we can use tables or statistical packages to numerically compute \(\text{Power}(\beta_1)\) \[ \text{Power}(\beta_1)=P(T>t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2})+P(T<-t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}) \] where \[ T\sim t_{n-2,\beta_1/\sigma_{b_1}} \]

Generating the Power Function Curve

\[ H_0:\beta_1=0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\beta_1\neq0 \]

We are interested in power over a range of \(\beta_1\) values.

  • Determine a range and equally spaced vary \(\beta_1\) in the range
  • Calculate \(\text{Power}(\beta_1)\) using the previous procedure for each value of \(\beta_1\)
  • Plot \(\text{Power}(\beta_1)\) against \(\beta_1\) to generate the power curve of the test

How to Increase the Power

A primary way to increase power is to increase the sample size \(n\) and the design \(X_1,X_2,\dots,X_n\) to increase \[ SSX=\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2 \] thus achieving a small \(\frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}=\sigma_{b_1}^2\).

Sample Size Calculation

Suppose the desired power at \(\beta_1=1\) is \(\text{Power}(1)=0.95\).

Sample size \(n\) \(SSX\) \(\sigma^2\) \(\text{Power}(1)\)
30 23800 2500 0.846
35 27800 2500 0.899
40 31800 2500 0.935
45 35800 2500 0.959

Therefore, the proper sample size is \(n=45\) with a design such that \(SSX=35800\).

Simple Normal Example

Based on a single random sample from a population which is assumed to be normally distributed with known variance equal to 1.

\[ H_0:\mu=0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\mu=1 \]

Suppose we are interested in the sample size for a power of 0.90 and a significance level of 0.05.

The \(Z\)-test based on \(n\) observations and the null hypothesis is rejected when \[ \sqrt{n}\bar X\ge Z_{.95}=1.64 \]

  • \(0.90=\gamma(1)=P(\sqrt{n}\bar X\ge1.64|\mu=1)=P(\sqrt{n}(\bar X-1)\ge1.64-\sqrt n)\)
  • \(\Phi(1.64-\sqrt{n})=0.10\qquad1.64-\sqrt{n}=-1.28\qquad n=8.52\text{ and take }n=9\)

Remarks

Formulas, procedures, and R code for two-sided \(t\)-tests can be modified for one sided \(t\)-tests, and for testing \(\beta_0\).

Power calculation and sample size determination are part of study/experimental design, and need to be carefully done before carrying out the study/experiment.

Consult a good/experienced Applied Statistician for power calculation, sample size determination, and study/experimental design, when the study/experiment is important.

Confidence Intervals vs. Hypothesis Testing

Statistical significance is not equivalent to practical significance.

A model is usually only an approximation of underlying reality. In many cases, we know the point null hypothesis is false without even looking at the data. If we fail to reject the null hypothesis, we might simply conclude that we did not have enough data to get a significant result. According to this view, the hypothesis test just becomes a test of sample size.

The larger the sample, the smaller the p-values, the greater the power, and the more easily to get statistically significant results, but the actual effects may be unimportant.

CIs are useful even when the null hypothesis is not rejected, because they tell us how confident we are that the true effect or value is close to the null.

To conclude, Confidence Intervals construction is preferred.

Estimation of \(E(Y_h)\)

At \(X=X_h\), the mean response \(E(Y_h)\) is \[ \mu_h=E(Y_h)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_h \]

We estimate \(E(Y_h)\) by \[ \hat \mu_h=b_0+b_1X_h \]

We use \(\hat \mu_h\) to emphasize that it is an estimator/estimate of an induced parameter, that is the mean response at \(X_h\).

Theoretical Properties of \(\hat \mu_h\)

Unbiased \[ E(\hat \mu_h)=E(b_0+b_1X_h)=\beta_0+\beta_1X_h=\mu_h \]

Minimum Variance \[ \sigma^2_{\hat \mu_h}=\sigma^2(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}) \]

Under i.i.d. normal error terms \[ \hat \mu_h\sim N(\mu_h,\sigma^2_{\hat \mu_h}) \] The normality of \(\hat \mu_h\) comes from the normality of \(b_0\) and \(b_1\)

Inferences Concerning \(\mu_h=E(Y_h)\)

Estimate \(\sigma^2_{\hat \mu_h}\) by \[ s^2_{\hat \mu_h}=s^2(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}) \]

Then we have that \[ \frac{\hat \mu_h-\mu_h}{s_{\hat \mu_h}}\sim t_{n-2} \]

Based on the results, significance tests and confidence intervals for \(\mu_h=E(Y_h)\) can be obtained. For example, \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) Confidence Interval is \[ \hat \mu_h\pm t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{\hat \mu_h} \]

Remarks

Standard error is affected by how far \(X_h\) is from \(\bar X\) \[ \sigma^2_{\hat \mu_h}=\sigma^2(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}) \]

Recall standard error for intercept \[ s_{b_0}=s\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}+\frac{\bar X^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}} \]

A change in the slope has bigger impact on \(Y\) as you move away from \(\bar X\), the middle of the teeter-totter.

Prediction of \(Y_{h(\text{new})}\)

Handling missing values – Want to predict value for a new observation at \(X=X_h\)

Model \[ Y_{h(\text{new})}=\beta_0+\beta_1X_h+\varepsilon \]

Since \(E(\varepsilon)=0\), the predicted value is the same as for \(E(Y_h)\) \[ \hat Y_{h(\text{new})}=\hat \mu_h=b_0+b_1X_h \]

Prediction Interval

A type of Confidence Interval

The basic idea is to choose a range in the distribution of \(Y\) wherein most of the observations will fall, and then to declare that the next observation will fall in this range.

The usefulness depends, as always, on the width of the interval and the needs for precision by the user.

Should be wider given additional variation about regression line

Easy Case: When Parameters Known

The relevant parameters of the regression model are known as \(\beta_0,\beta_1,\sigma\)

Then the \(1-\alpha\) prediction limits for \(Y_{h(\text{new})}\) are \[ E(Y_h)\pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\sigma \]

General Case: When Parameters Unknown

When the regression parameters are unknown, prediction limits must take account of two elements

  • Variation in possible location of the distribution of \(Y\)
  • Variation within the probability distribution of \(Y\)

Prediction Error \(d_h\) \[ d_h=Y_{h(\text{new})}-\hat Y_{h(\text{new})}=Y_{h(\text{new})}-\hat \mu_h \]

Mean \[ E(d_h)=0 \]

Variance \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma^2_{d_h}&=Var(Y_{h(\text{new})}-\hat \mu_h)\\ &=Var(Y_{h(\text{new})})+Var(\hat \mu_h)\\ &=\sigma^2+\sigma^2(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2})\\ &=\sigma^2(1+\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}) \end{aligned} \]

General Case: Use the \(t\)-statistic

Under normal error terms \[ d_h\sim N(0,\sigma^2_{d_h}) \]

Estimate \(\sigma^2_{d_h}\) and standard error \[ s^2_{d_h}=s^2(1+\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}) \] and we denote \[ s_{\text{pred}}\triangleq s_{d_h} \]

\(t\)-distribution \[ \frac{d_h}{s_{d_h}}=\frac{Y_{h(\text{new})}-\hat \mu_h}{s_{\text{pred}}}\sim t_{n-2} \]

And we get the \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) prediction interval for \(Y_{h(\text{new})}\) \[ \hat \mu_h\pm t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{\text{pred}} \]

Mean of \(m\) New Observations

The estimator is also \(\hat \mu_h\)

The \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) prediction interval can be modified for predicting the mean of \(m\) new observations at \(X=X_h\) \[ \hat \mu_h\pm t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2}\cdot s_{\text{pred mean}} \] where \[ s^2_{\text{pred mean}}=s^2(\frac{1}{m}+\frac{1}{n}+\frac{(X_h-\bar X)^2}{\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)^2}) \]

Confidence Band for the Entire Regression Line

Find the lower and upper (sample-dependent) functions \(L(x)\) and \(U(x)\), such that \[ P(L(x)<\beta_0+\beta_1x<U(x)\text{ for all }x)=1-\alpha \]

Then the confidence band is \[ \{(x,y):L(x)<y<U(x),x\in\mathbb R\} \]

Compared to the Confidence Interval \((l(x),u(x))\) for one fixed \(x\) \[ P(l(x)<\beta_0+\beta_1x<u(x))=1-\alpha \]

Instead of using the sampling distribution of \(\frac{\hat \mu_x-\mu_x}{s_{\hat \mu_x}}\), consider that of \((\max\frac{\hat \mu_x-\mu_x}{s_{\hat \mu_x}})\) over all \(x\) and we can derive that \[ (L(x),U(x))=\hat \mu_x\pm W\cdot s_{\hat \mu_x} \] where \[ W=\sqrt{2F_{1-\alpha,2,n-2}} \]

The confidence band is wider than individual confidence intervals. \[ W>t_{n-2,1-\alpha/2} \]

Analysis of Variance

Total variation: Total Sum of Squares \(SST\) \[ SST=\sum_i(Y_i-\bar Y)^2 \]

Partition: Quantify and classify various types of variations and their sources

Partition of Sum of Squares Accordingly

Partition \(SST\) into two sources \[ Y_i-\bar Y=(Y_i-\hat Y_i)+(\hat Y_i-\bar Y) \]

  • Variation due to Error \(SSE\) – attributed to error terms
  • Variation due to Model \(SSR\) – explained by regression line

Total Sum of Squares Partition

\(SST\) quantifies the total amount of variation in data

  • If all observations are equal, then \(SST=0\). Otherwise, \(SST>0\).

\(SST=SSR+SSE\) \[ \sum(Y_i-\bar Y)^2=\sum[(Y_i-\hat Y_i)+(\hat Y_i-\bar Y)]^2=\sum(Y_i-\hat Y_i)^2+\sum(\hat Y_i-\bar Y)^2+2\sum(Y_i-\hat Y_i)(\hat Y_i-\bar Y) \] where \[ \sum(Y_i-\hat Y_i)(\hat Y_i-\bar Y)=\sum(b_0+b_1X_i)e_i-\sum(b_0+b_1\bar X)e_i=0 \]

For the Model Sum of Squares

\[ SSR=\sum(\hat Y_i-\bar Y)^2 \]

Note that \(\bar{\hat Y}=\bar Y\)

The degree of freedom of \(SSR\) is \(df_R=1\), because \[ SSR=b_1^2\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2 \]

Mean Squares of Regression (Model) \(MSR\) \[ MSR=\frac{SSR}{df_R}=SSR \]

For the Error Sum of Squares

\[ SSE=\sum(Y_i-\hat Y_i)^2=\sum e_i^2 \]

The degree of freedom of \(SSE\) is \(df_E=n-2\), adjusting for the intercept and slope

Mean Square of Error \(MSE\) \[ MSE=\frac{SSE}{df_E} \]

\(MSE=s^2\) is an estimator/estimate of \[ \sigma^2=Var(Y|X)=Var(\varepsilon) \]
