Lecture 1: Introduction

Introduction to Multivariate Statistical Analysis

What is Multivariate Data

The majority of data sets collected in all disciplines are multivariate.

  • Medical record / Credit card application / Image analysis / Shopping receipt

What can we Achieve by Multivariate Analysis

Variety goals

  1. Find pattern and correlation
  2. Make informed decision / prediction
  3. Gain deeper understanding of domain problems

Variety fields

  • Psychology, Ecology, Medicine, Economy, Literature
  • Multivariate Testing / Dimension Reduction / Clustering / Classification

Course Goals

  • Identify the need for multivariate statistical techniques
  • Recognize the appropriate multivariate method for a problem
    • Understanding the idea
    • Statistical / Mathematical reasoning
  • Employ statistical software to conduct the appropriate analysis
  • Interpret and present results

Foundation of Multivariate Statistical Analysis

Organization of Multivariate Data

Display or extract the signal in multivariate data in presence of noise.

The data matrix of \(n\) items sampled from a large population with \(p\) variables \[ X=\begin{bmatrix} x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1p} \\ x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_{n1} & x_{n2} & \cdots & x_{np} \end{bmatrix} \]

  • Variables \(X_1,X_2,\dots,X_p\)
  • Rows correspond to items & Columns correspond to variables

Modeling of Multivariate Data

From observed value \(x_{jk}\) to random variable \(X_{jk}\) \[ X=\begin{bmatrix} X_{11} & X_{12} & \cdots & X_{1p} \\ X_{21} & X_{22} & \cdots & X_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ X_{n1} & X_{n2} & \cdots & X_{np} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Random Sampling

\(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) are said to form a random sample from \(f(\boldsymbol{x})\) if

  • \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) represent independent observations
  • \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) are from a common joint distribution with density function \[ f(\boldsymbol{x})=f(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_p) \]

Usually, measurements of \(p\) variables in a single experimental unit will be correlated.

Given the random sampling assumption, the multivariate dataset can be modelled with the joint density distribution of \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) \[ f(\boldsymbol x_1,\boldsymbol x_2,\dots,\boldsymbol x_n)=\prod_{i=1}^n{f(\boldsymbol x_i)} \] where \(f(\boldsymbol{x_i})=f(x_{i1},x_{i2},\dots,x_{ip})\)

Descriptive Statistics

Single variable

  • Sample mean: center / location \[ \bar{x}_k=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}x_{jk},\quad k=1,2,\dots,p \]
  • Sample variance, sample standard deviation: spread / dispersion \[ s_k^2=s_{kk}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}(x_{jk}-\bar{x}_k)^2,\quad k=1,2,\dots,p \]

Two variables

  • Sample covariance: linear association between two variables \[ s_{ik}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}(x_{ji}-\bar{x}_i)(x_{jk}-\bar{x}_k),\quad i,k=1,2,\dots,p \]
  • Sample correlation \[ r_{ik}=\frac{s_{ik}}{\sqrt{s_{ii}}\sqrt{s_{kk}}}=\frac{\sum_{j=1}^{n}{(x_{ji}-\bar{x}_i)(x_{jk}-\bar{x}_k)}}{\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}(x_{ji}-\bar{x}_i)^2}\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{n}(x_{jk}-\bar{x}_k)^2}} \]
    • It measures linear association
    • Scale free and unit free
    • Covariance of standardized data
    • \(r\) does not change upon linear transformation of the variables

Descriptive Statistics in Matrix Form

  • Sample means \[ \bar{x}= \begin{bmatrix} \bar{x}_1 \\ \bar{x}_2 \\ \vdots \\ \bar{x}_p \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

  • Sample variances and covariances \[ S_n= \begin{bmatrix} s_{11} & s_{12} & \cdots & s_{1p} \\ s_{21} & s_{22} & \cdots & s_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ s_{p1} & s_{p2} & \cdots & s_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

  • Sample correlations \[ R_n= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & r_{12} & \cdots & r_{1p} \\ r_{21} & 1 & \cdots & r_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ r_{p1} & r_{p2} & \cdots & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Note that sample covariance and correlation matrices are symmetric and nonnegative-definite.

Geometry of Random Sample

  • p-dimensional space & n points
  • Scatter plots provides information of location and variability of the points

Mahalanobis Distance

  • To define distance/similarity between two multivariate data points
  • If each of these axes is re-scaled to have unit variance, then the Mahalanobis distance corresponds to standard Euclidean distance in the transformed space.
  • The Mahalanobis distance is unitless and scale-invariant, and takes into account the correlations of the data set. Its applications include outlier detection, classification, etc.

The Mahalanobis distance of an observation \(\boldsymbol x=(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_p)'\) from a set of observations with mean \(\boldsymbol \mu=(\mu_1,\mu_2,\dots,\mu_p)'\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) is defined as \[ d(\boldsymbol x,\boldsymbol \mu)=\sqrt{(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol\mu)} \]

The Mahalanobis distance can also be defined as a dissimilarity measure between two random vectors \(\boldsymbol{x}\) and \(\boldsymbol{y}\) of the same distribution with the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) \[ d(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{y})=\sqrt{(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})} \]

Quadratic Form

A quadratic form \(Q(\boldsymbol{x})\) in the \(p\) variables \(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_p\) is \(Q(\boldsymbol{x})=\boldsymbol x'A\boldsymbol x\), where \(\boldsymbol{x}=(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_p)'\) and \(A\) is a \(p\times p\) symmetric matrix.

Apparently, both Mahalanobis Distance and Euclidean distance can be expressed as quadratic forms.

Basic Population Notations

  • Random vector \[ \boldsymbol X=\begin{bmatrix} X_1\\ \vdots\\ X_p\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

  • The population mean vector \[ \boldsymbol \mu=E(\boldsymbol X)=\begin{bmatrix} E(X_1)\\ \vdots\\ E(X_p)\\ \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} \mu_1\\ \vdots\\ \mu_p\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

  • The population covariance matrix \[ \Sigma=Cov(\boldsymbol X)=E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)')=\begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \cdots & \sigma_{1p} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \sigma_{p1} & \cdots & \sigma_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix} \] The matrix \(\Sigma\) is \(p\times p\) with \(\sigma_{ij}=Cov(X_i,X_j)=E((X_i-\mu_i)(X_j-\mu_j))\).

Expectation of Distance

Theorem 1.

Let \(Z\) be a random vector with finite mean \(\mu_Z\) and finite covariance matrix \(\Sigma_Z\). Then \[ E(Z'AZ)=tr(A\Sigma_Z)+\mu_Z'A\mu_Z \] Proof \[ E(Z'AZ)=tr(E(Z'AZ))=E(tr(Z'AZ))=E(tr(AZZ'))=tr(AE(ZZ')) \] \[ E(Z'AZ)=tr(A(\Sigma_Z+\mu_Z\mu_Z'))=tr(A\Sigma_Z)+tr(A\mu_Z\mu_Z')=tr(A\Sigma_Z)+\mu_Z'A\mu_Z \]

Theorem 2 (Expected Euclidean distance).

Suppose \(p\)-dimensional random vector \(X\) has finite mean \(\mu\) and finite covariance matrix \(\Sigma\). Then \[ E(\|X-\mu\|_2^2)=\sum_{j=1}^p\sigma_{jj} \] Proof \[ E(\|X-\mu\|_2^2)=E((X-\mu)'I(X-\mu))=tr(I\Sigma)+(E(X-\mu))'I(E(X-\mu))=tr(\Sigma)=\sum_{j=1}^p\sigma_{jj} \]

Theorem 3 (Expected Mahalanobis distance).

Suppose \(p\)-dimensional random vector \(X\) has finite mean \(\mu\) and finite covariance matrix \(\Sigma\). Then Mahalanobis distance between \(X\) and \(\mu\) can be expressed as \[ \|X-\mu\|_\Sigma=d(X,\mu)=\sqrt{(X-\mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(X-\mu)} \] Consequently, \[ E(\|X-\mu\|_\Sigma^2)=E((X-\mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(X-\mu))=p \] Proof \[ E(\|X-\mu\|_\Sigma^2)=E((X-\mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(X-\mu))=tr(\Sigma^{-1}\Sigma)+(E(X-\mu))'\Sigma^{-1}(E(X-\mu))=tr(I)=p \]

Theorem 4 (Expected Euclidean distance).

Suppose \(p\)-dimensional \(X,Y\) are independent, identically distributed random vectors with finite mean \(\mu\) and finite covariance matrix \(\Sigma\). Then \[ E(\|X-Y\|^2_2)=2\sum_{j=1}^p\sigma_{jj} \]

Lemma

Suppose \(p\)-dimensional \(X,Y\) are independent, identically distributed random vectors. Then \[ Cov(X-Y)=2Cov(X) \] Proof \[ Cov(X-Y)=\begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \cdots & \sigma_{1p} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \sigma_{p1} & \cdots & \sigma_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix} \] where \[ \sigma_{ij}=Cov(X_i-Y_i,X_j-Y_j)=Cov(X_i,X_j)-Cov(X_i,Y_j)-Cov(Y_i,X_j)+Cov(Y_i,Y_j)=2Cov(X_i,X_j) \]

Proof \[ E(\|X-Y\|_2^2)=E((X-Y)'I(X-Y))=tr(I2\Sigma)+(E(X-Y))'I(E(X-Y))=2tr(\Sigma)=2\sum_{j=1}^p\sigma_{jj} \]

Theorem 5 (Expected Mahalanobis distance).

Suppose \(p\)-dimensional \(X,Y\) are independent, identically distributed random vectors with finite mean \(\mu\) and finite covariance matrix \(\Sigma\). Then Mahalanobis distance between \(X\) and \(Y\) can be expressed as \[ \|X-Y\|_\Sigma=d(X,Y)=\sqrt{(X-Y)'\Sigma^{-1}(X-Y)} \] Consequently, \[ E(\|X-Y\|_\Sigma^2)=E((X-Y)'\Sigma^{-1}(X-Y))=2p \] Proof \[ E(\|X-Y\|_2^2)=E((X-Y)'\Sigma^{-1}(X-Y))=tr(\Sigma^{-1}2\Sigma)+(E(X-Y))'\Sigma^{-1}(E(X-Y))=2tr(I)=2p \]

Lecture 2: Linear Algebra Basis

Review of Matrix Operations

Length and Direction of a Vector

  • Inner (dot) product \[ \boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol y=x_1y_1+x_2y_2+\dots +x_ny_n=L_\boldsymbol xL_\boldsymbol y\cos(\theta) \]

  • Angle between two vectors \[ \cos(\theta)=\frac{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol y}{L_\boldsymbol xL_\boldsymbol y}=\frac{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol y}{\sqrt{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol x}\sqrt{\boldsymbol y'\boldsymbol y}} \]

  • Length \[ L_\boldsymbol x=\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2+\dots +x_n^2}=\sqrt{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol x} \]

  • Projection of \(\boldsymbol x\) on \(\boldsymbol y\) \[ \frac{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol y}{L_\boldsymbol y}\frac{\boldsymbol y}{L_\boldsymbol y}=\frac{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol y}{\boldsymbol y'\boldsymbol y}\boldsymbol y \]

Matrix

Mapping from \(k\)-dim space to \(m\)-dim space

\[ \underset{(m\times k)}{A}= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1k} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \cdots & a_{mk} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

\[ A\boldsymbol x=x_1\boldsymbol a_1+x_2\boldsymbol a_2+\dots +x_n\boldsymbol a_n \]

Special Matrices

  • Square matrix & Symmetric matrix (e.g. (sample) covariance matrix) \[ \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1p} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{p1} & a_{p2} & \cdots & a_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

  • Diagonal matrix \[ \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & a_{22} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & a_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

  • Identity matrix \[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Orthogonal Matrix

A square matrix \(A\) is said to be orthogonal if its rows, considered as vectors, are mutually perpendicular and have unit lengths; or \(AA'=I\).

A square matrix A is orthogonal if and only if \(A^{-1}=A'\). For an orthogonal matrix, \(AA'=A'A=I\), so the columns are also mutually perpendicular and have unit length.

\[ {A}= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1p} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{p1} & a_{p2} & \cdots & a_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol a_{1} \\ \boldsymbol a_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \boldsymbol a_{p} \\ \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol e_{1} & \boldsymbol e_{2} & \cdots & \boldsymbol e_{p} \end{bmatrix} \]

Note that \(P=\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \\ \end{bmatrix}\) rotates the coordinates \(\theta\) anticlockwise.

Eigen System

Let \(A\) be a \(k\times k\) symmetric matrix. Then \(A\) has \(k\) pairs of eigenvalues and eigenvectors \[ \lambda_1\boldsymbol e_1,\lambda_2\boldsymbol e_2,\dots,\lambda_k\boldsymbol e_k \]

The eigenvectors can be chosen to satisfy \(1=\boldsymbol e_1'\boldsymbol e_1=\dots=\boldsymbol e_k'\boldsymbol e_k\) and be mutually perpendicular, then \(\{\boldsymbol e_1,\dots,\boldsymbol e_k\}\) forms an orthogonal basis. Let \(P=\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol e_{1} & \boldsymbol e_{2} & \cdots & \boldsymbol e_{k} \end{bmatrix}\), then \(PP'=P'P=I\).

The eigenvectors are unique unless two or more eigenvalues are equal.

Spectral Decomposition

Let \(A\) be a \(k\times k\) symmetric matrix. Then \(A\) can be expressed in terms of its \(k\) eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs. \[ A=\lambda_1\boldsymbol e_1\boldsymbol e_1'+\lambda_2\boldsymbol e_2\boldsymbol e_2'+\dots+\lambda_k\boldsymbol e_k\boldsymbol e_k'=\sum_{i=1}^k\lambda_i\boldsymbol e_i\boldsymbol e_i' \]

Or denote \(P=\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol e_{1} & \boldsymbol e_{2} & \cdots & \boldsymbol e_{k} \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\Lambda={diag}\{\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_k\}\), then \[ A=P\Lambda P' \]

Nonnegative / Positive Definite Matrix

\(A\) is a \(k\times k\) symmetric matrix. If for all \(\boldsymbol x'=[x_1,x_2,\dots,x_k]\), \(\boldsymbol x'A\boldsymbol x\geq0\), then both the matrix $ A$ and the quadratic form are said to be nonnegative definite, or positive semi-definite.

If \(\boldsymbol x'A\boldsymbol x>0\) for all vectors \(\boldsymbol x\neq\boldsymbol0\), the matrix $ A$ and the quadratic form are said to be positive definite.

For nonnegative or positive definite matrix $ A$, \(\boldsymbol x'A\boldsymbol x=\sum\lambda_iy_i^2=\sum(\sqrt{\lambda_i}y_i)^2\), where \(y_i=\boldsymbol x\boldsymbol e_i\).

  • $ A$ is positive definite matrix if and only if every eigenvalue of $ A$ is positive.
  • $ A$ is nonnegative definite matrix if and only if all of its eigenvalues are greater than or equal to zero.

Inverse and Square-root Matrix

Let $ A$ be a \(k\times k\) matrix with all eigenvalues positive, and decomposition \(A=\sum_{i=1}^k\lambda_i\boldsymbol e_i\boldsymbol e_i'\). Denote \(P=\begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol e_{1} & \boldsymbol e_{2} & \cdots & \boldsymbol e_{k} \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\Lambda={diag}\{\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_k\}\), with \(\lambda_i>0\). Then \(A=P\Lambda^{-1}P'\).

  • Inverse matrix \[ A^{-1}=P\Lambda^{-1}P' \]
  • Square-root Matrix \[ A^{\frac{1}{2}}=P\Lambda^{\frac{1}{2}}P'=\sum_{i=1}^k\sqrt{\lambda_i}\boldsymbol e_i\boldsymbol e_i' \]

Note that \[ A^{\frac{1}{2}}A^{\frac{1}{2}}=A\qquad (A^{\frac{1}{2}})'=A^{\frac{1}{2}} \] and \[ (A^{\frac{1}{2}})^{-1}=A^{-\frac{1}{2}}=\sum_{i=1}^k\frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_i}}\boldsymbol e_i\boldsymbol e_i'=P\Lambda^{-\frac{1}{2}}P' \]

Geometric Interpretation of Mahalanobis-Distance

  • \(p\)-dimensional space & \(n\) points
  • take sample mean \(\boldsymbol \mu=\boldsymbol 0\)

We have the original data \(X\). We wish to get transformed data \(Y\) distributed on spheroids instead of ellipsoids. So we build a model: For every vector \(\boldsymbol x\) in original coordinate system, we let \[ \boldsymbol x=\tilde P\tilde \Lambda\boldsymbol y\quad\text{s.t.}\quad Var(\boldsymbol y)=I \]

Thus we have \[ \Sigma=Var(\boldsymbol x)=\tilde P\tilde \Lambda Var(\boldsymbol y)\tilde \Lambda\tilde P'=\tilde P\tilde \Lambda^2\tilde P' \] and thus the distance \[ \boldsymbol y'\boldsymbol y=(\boldsymbol x'\tilde P\tilde \Lambda^{-1})(\tilde \Lambda^{-1}\tilde P\boldsymbol x)=\boldsymbol x'(\tilde P\tilde \Lambda^{-2}\tilde P)\boldsymbol x=\boldsymbol x'\Sigma^{-1}\boldsymbol x \]

On the other hand, we can get sample covariance \(S\) from data, as the estimation for \(\Sigma\). So we usually use the following as the Mahalanobis-distance \[ \boldsymbol x'S^{-1}\boldsymbol x \] The angle \(\theta\) we rotate the coordinate system is decided by \(P\) with the spectral decomposition \(S=P\Lambda P'\).

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

Let $ A$ be a \(m\times k\) matrix of real numbers. Then there exist an \(m\times m\) orthogonal matrix $ U$ and a \(k\times k\) orthogonal matrix $ V$ such that \[ A=U\Lambda V' \]

\(\Lambda\) is an \(m\times k\) matrix with \((i,i)\) entry \(\lambda_i\ge0\) for \(i=1,2,\dots,\min(m,k)\) and the other entries are zero. And the positive constants \(\lambda_i\) are called the singular values of $ A$.

Matrix Inequalities

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

Let \(\boldsymbol b\) and \(\boldsymbol d\) be any \(p\times 1\) two vectors. Then \[ (\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol d)^2\le (\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol b)(\boldsymbol d'\boldsymbol d) \] with equality if and only if \(\boldsymbol b=c\boldsymbol d\) for some constant \(c\).

Extended Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

Let \(\boldsymbol b\) and \(\boldsymbol d\) be any \(p\times 1\) two vectors. And let $ B$ be a positive definite matrix. Then \[ (\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol d)^2\le (\boldsymbol b'B\boldsymbol b)(\boldsymbol d'B^{-1}\boldsymbol d) \] with equality if and only if \(\boldsymbol b=cB^{-1}\boldsymbol d\) for some constant \(c\).

Matrix Maximization

Maximization Lemma

Let $ B$ be a positive definite matrix and \(\boldsymbol d\) be a given vector. Then for any arbitrary nonzero vector \(\boldsymbol x\) \[ \max_{\boldsymbol x\ne\boldsymbol0}\frac{(\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol d)^2}{\boldsymbol x'B\boldsymbol x}=\boldsymbol d'B^{-1}\boldsymbol d \]

with maximum attained when \(\boldsymbol x=cB^{-1}\boldsymbol d\) for some constant \(c\).

Maximization of Quadratic Forms for Points on the Unit Sphere

Let $ B$ be a positive definite matrix with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\dots\ge\lambda_p\ge0\) and associated normalized eigenvectors \(\boldsymbol e_1,\boldsymbol e_2,\dots,\boldsymbol e_p\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} \max_{\boldsymbol x\ne\boldsymbol0}\frac{\boldsymbol x'B\boldsymbol x}{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol x}&=\lambda_1\\ \min_{\boldsymbol x\ne\boldsymbol0}\frac{\boldsymbol x'B\boldsymbol x}{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol x}&=\lambda_p \end{aligned} \] Moreover \[ \max_{\boldsymbol x\perp \boldsymbol e_1,\boldsymbol e_2,\dots,\boldsymbol e_k}\frac{\boldsymbol x'B\boldsymbol x}{\boldsymbol x'\boldsymbol x}=\lambda_{k+1} \]

Matrix operation for Descriptive Statistics

Sample Mean \[ \bar{\boldsymbol x}= \begin{bmatrix} \bar{x}_1 \\ \bar{x}_2 \\ \vdots \\ \bar{x}_p \\ \end{bmatrix}=\frac{1}{n}\begin{bmatrix} x_{11} & \cdots & x_{n1} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_{1p} & \cdots & x_{np} \\ \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ \end{bmatrix}=\frac{1}{n}X'\boldsymbol 1_n \]

Sample Covariance Matrix \[ B=\begin{bmatrix} x_{11}-\bar x_1 & \cdots & x_{1p}-\bar x_p \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_{n1}-\bar x_1 & \cdots & x_{np}-\bar x_p \\ \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} x_{11} & \cdots & x_{1p} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_{n1} & \cdots & x_{np} \\ \end{bmatrix}-\begin{bmatrix} \bar x_{1} & \cdots & \bar x_{p} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \bar x_{1} & \cdots & \bar x_{p} \\ \end{bmatrix}=X-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n'X=(I-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n')X \]

\[ nS_n=B'B=((I-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n')X)'(I-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n')X=X'(I-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n')^2X=X'(I-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n')X \]

  • It is straight forward to verify that \((I-\frac{1}{n}\boldsymbol 1_n\boldsymbol 1_n')\) is idempotent.

Sample Correlation Matrix \[ S=\begin{bmatrix} s_{11} & \cdots & s_{1p} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ s_{p1} & \cdots & s_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix}\quad D^{1/2}=\begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{s_{11}} && \\ & \ddots & \\ && \sqrt{s_{pp}} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Lecture 3: Multivariate Normal Distribution

Why Normal Distribution

The multivariate normal distribution plays a central role in multivariate statistics for several reasons.

  • It is considerably more mathematically tractable than other multivariate distributions.
  • The multivariate generalization of the central limit theorem says that properly centered and scaled sums of random vectors are asymptotically multivariate normal. Since many scientifically meaningful multivariate statistics are sums of vectors, this means that many statistics have approximately a multivariate normal distribution in large samples.
  • Much more general sampling models can be constructed using multivariate normal distributions as building blocks (e.g. mixtures).

Multivariate Normal Distribution

Continuous Variable completely characterized by its first two moments

Univariate Case: \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) \[ f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\} \]

Multivariate Case: \(\boldsymbol X\sim N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) \[ f(\boldsymbol x)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\exp\{-\frac{(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)}{2}\} \]

  • \(\boldsymbol \mu\) is the mean of \(\boldsymbol X\)
  • \(\Sigma\) is the variance-covariance matrix of \(\boldsymbol X\) and a positive definite matrix

Geometry of Multivariate Normal Distribution

Contours of constant density for \(\boldsymbol X\sim N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) can be expressed as \[ (\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)=c^2 \]

Suppose \(\Sigma\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\dots\ge\lambda_p\) and corresponding eigenvectors \(\boldsymbol e_1,\boldsymbol e_2,\dots,\boldsymbol e_p\). Then these ellipsoids are centered at \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and have axes \(\pm c\sqrt{\lambda_i}\cdot\boldsymbol e_i\).

Properties of Multivariate Normal Distribution

Equivalent Definition

\(\boldsymbol X\) is distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) if and only if \(\boldsymbol a'\boldsymbol X\) is distributed as \(N(\boldsymbol a'\boldsymbol \mu,\boldsymbol a'\Sigma\boldsymbol a)\) for every \(\boldsymbol a\in \mathbb R^p\).

Linear Combination

If \(\boldsymbol X\) is distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\), the \(q\) linear combinations \[ A\boldsymbol X=\begin{bmatrix} a_{11}X_1+\dots+a_{1p}X_p \\ a_{21}X_1+\dots+a_{2p}X_p \\ \vdots \\ a_{q1}X_1+\dots+a_{qp}X_p \end{bmatrix} \] is distributed as \(N_q(A\boldsymbol \mu,A\Sigma A')\). Also \(\boldsymbol X+\boldsymbol d\) , where \(\boldsymbol d\) is a vertor of constants, is distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu+\boldsymbol d,\Sigma)\).

How to get \(\boldsymbol X\sim N(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) from \(Z\sim N(0,1)\)

Let \(Z_1,\dots,Z_p\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(0,1)\) and \(\boldsymbol Z=(Z_1,\dots,Z_p)'\). Let \(\Sigma\) be a positive-definite symmetric matrix and \(\boldsymbol \mu\in\mathbb R^p\) be a real vector. Then \(\boldsymbol X=\boldsymbol \mu+\Sigma^{1/2}\boldsymbol Z\) is distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\).

The multivariate normal distribution is, in a fundamental sense, the distribution of affine transformations of independent Gaussians.

Marginal Distribution

All subsets of \(\boldsymbol X\) are normally distributed.

If we respectively partition \(\boldsymbol X\), its mean vector \(\boldsymbol \mu\), and its covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) as \[ \underset{(q_1+q_2)\times1}{\boldsymbol X}=\begin{bmatrix} \underset{q_1\times1}{\boldsymbol X_1}\\ \underset{q_2\times1}{\boldsymbol X_2} \end{bmatrix}\qquad \underset{(q_1+q_2)\times1}{\boldsymbol \mu}=\begin{bmatrix} \underset{q_1\times1}{\boldsymbol \mu_1}\\ \underset{q_2\times1}{\boldsymbol \mu_2} \end{bmatrix}\qquad \underset{(q_1+q_2)\times(q_1+q_2)}{\Sigma}=\begin{bmatrix} \underset{q_1\times q_1}{\Sigma_{11}} & \underset{q_1\times q_2}{\Sigma_{12}}\\ \underset{q_2\times q_1}{\Sigma_{21}} & \underset{q_2\times q_2}{\Sigma_{22}} \end{bmatrix} \] Then \(\boldsymbol X_1\) is distributed as \(N_{q_1}(\boldsymbol \mu_1,\Sigma_{11})\).

Independent vs. Uncorrelated

If \(\boldsymbol X_1\) and \(\boldsymbol X_2\) are independent, then \(Cov(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2)=\boldsymbol 0\), a \(q_1\times q_2\) matrix of zeros.

If we already have \[ \underset{(q_1+q_2)\times1}{\boldsymbol X}=\begin{bmatrix} \underset{q_1\times1}{\boldsymbol X_1}\\ \underset{q_2\times1}{\boldsymbol X_2} \end{bmatrix}\sim N_{q_1+q_2}( \begin{bmatrix} \underset{q_1\times1}{\boldsymbol \mu_1}\\ \underset{q_2\times1}{\boldsymbol \mu_2} \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} \underset{q_1\times q_1}{\Sigma_{11}} & \underset{q_1\times q_2}{\Sigma_{12}}\\ \underset{q_2\times q_1}{\Sigma_{21}} & \underset{q_2\times q_2}{\Sigma_{22}} \end{bmatrix}) \] then \(\boldsymbol X_1\) and \(\boldsymbol X_2\) are independent if and only if \(\Sigma_{12}=\boldsymbol 0\).

If \(\boldsymbol X_1\) and \(\boldsymbol X_2\) are independent and distributed as \(N_{q_1}(\boldsymbol \mu_1,\Sigma_{11})\) and \(N_{q_2}(\boldsymbol \mu_2,\Sigma_{22})\) respectively, then \[ \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol X_1\\ \boldsymbol X_2 \end{bmatrix}\sim N_{q_1+q_2}( \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol \mu_1\\ \boldsymbol \mu_2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{11} & \boldsymbol 0\\ \boldsymbol 0 & \Sigma_{22} \end{bmatrix}) \]

Conditional Distribution

If we have \[ \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol X_1\\ \boldsymbol X_2 \end{bmatrix}\sim N_{q_1+q_2}( \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol \mu_1\\ \boldsymbol \mu_2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{11} & \Sigma_{12}\\ \Sigma_{21} & \Sigma_{22} \end{bmatrix}) \] then the conditional distribution of \(\boldsymbol X_1\) given \(\boldsymbol X_2=\boldsymbol x_2\) is normal \[ \boldsymbol X_1\sim N(\boldsymbol \mu_1+\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(\boldsymbol x_2-\boldsymbol \mu_2),\Sigma_{11}-\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}) \]

Chi-Square Distribution

Let \(\boldsymbol X\) be distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\). Then

  • \((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)\) is distributed as \(\chi_p^2\), which denotes the chi-square distribution with p degree of freedom.
  • The distribution \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) assigns probability \(1-\alpha\) to the solid ellipsoid \[ \{\boldsymbol x:(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol x-\boldsymbol \mu)\le\chi_{p,\alpha}^2\} \] where \(\chi_{p,\alpha}^2\) denotes the upper \(\alpha\) percentile of the \(\chi^2_p\) distribution

Note that \(\boldsymbol Z=\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}}(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)\sim N_p(\boldsymbol 0,I_p)\). Then \[ (\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)=((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}})(\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}}(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu))=\boldsymbol Z'\boldsymbol Z=Z_1^2+\dots+Z_p^2 \]

Distribution of Linear Combinations of MVNs

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be mutually independent with \(\boldsymbol X_j\) distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_j,\Sigma)\). Note that each \(\boldsymbol X_j\) has the same covariance matrix \(\Sigma\). Then

  • \(\boldsymbol V_1=c_1\boldsymbol X_1+c_2\boldsymbol X_2+\dots+c_n\boldsymbol X_n\) is distributed as \(N_p(\sum_{j=1}^nc_j\boldsymbol \mu_j,(\sum_{j=1}^nc_j^2)\Sigma)\).
  • Moreover, \(\boldsymbol V_1\) and \(\boldsymbol V_2=b_1\boldsymbol X_1+b_2\boldsymbol X_2+\dots+b_n\boldsymbol X_n\) are jointly multivariate normal with covariance matrix \[ \begin{bmatrix} (\boldsymbol c'\boldsymbol c)\Sigma & (\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol c)\Sigma\\ (\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol c)\Sigma & (\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol b)\Sigma \end{bmatrix} \]
  • Consequently, \(\boldsymbol V_1\) and \(\boldsymbol V_2\) are independent if \(\boldsymbol b'\boldsymbol c=\sum_{j=1}^nb_jc_j=0\).

Inferences Under Normal Assumption

Modeling with Multivariate Normal Distribution

Assume that the \(p\times1\) vectors \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) represents a random sample from a multivariate normal population \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\).

  • \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) are mutually independent
  • \(\boldsymbol X_i\) follows \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) for every \(i=1,2,\dots,n\)

Then joint density of \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) is \[ \prod_{j=1}^n(\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}e^{-\frac{(\boldsymbol x_j-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol x_j-\boldsymbol \mu)}{2}})=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{np/2}|\Sigma|^{n/2}}e^{-\frac{\sum_{j=1}^n(\boldsymbol x_j-\boldsymbol \mu)'\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol x_j-\boldsymbol \mu)}{2}} \] which can also be seen as the Likelihood Function

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be a random sample from a multivariate normal population \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\). Then \[ \hat{\boldsymbol \mu}=\bar{\boldsymbol X}\qquad\text{and}\qquad\hat \Sigma=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})' \] are the maximum likelihood estimator of \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and \(\Sigma\) respectively.

Their observed values \(\bar{\boldsymbol x}\) and \(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n(\boldsymbol x_j-\bar{\boldsymbol x})(\boldsymbol x_j-\bar{\boldsymbol x})'\) are called the maximum likelihood estimates of \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and \(\Sigma\).

The maximum of the likelihood \[ L(\hat{\boldsymbol \mu},\hat \Sigma)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{np/2}|\hat \Sigma|^{n/2}}e^{-np/2}\qquad\propto\qquad(\text{Generalized Variance})^{-n/2} \]

Unbiased Estimation

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be a random sample from a multivariate normal population \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\). Then \[ \hat{\boldsymbol \mu}=\bar{\boldsymbol X}\qquad\text{and}\qquad S=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{j=1}^n(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})'=\frac{n}{n-1}\hat \Sigma \] are the unbiased estimator of \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and \(\Sigma\) respectively.

Sufficient Statistics

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be a random sample from a multivariate normal population \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\). \(\bar{\boldsymbol X}\) and \(S\) are sufficient statistics, which means that for normal populations, all of the information about the model parameters is contained in \(\bar{\boldsymbol X}\) and \(S\).

Lecture 4: Inference for One Population

Sampling Distribution of Sufficient Statistics

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be a random sample of size \(n\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\). Then

  • \(\bar{\boldsymbol X}\) is distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\frac{1}{n}\Sigma)\).
  • \((n-1)S\) is distributed as \(W_p(n-1,\Sigma)\).
  • \(\bar{\boldsymbol X}\) and \(S\) are independent.

Wishart Distribution

  • \(W_p(m,\Sigma)\) stands for the Wishart Distribution with \(m\) d.f. \[ \sum_{j=1}^m\boldsymbol Z_j\boldsymbol Z_j'\sim W_p(m,\Sigma)\quad\text{for}\quad \boldsymbol Z_1,\dots,\boldsymbol Z_m\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N_p(\boldsymbol 0,\Sigma) \]

Properties of Wishart Distribution

  1. If \(A_1\) and \(A_2\) are independently distributed as \(W_p(m_1,\Sigma)\) and \(W_p(m_2,\Sigma)\), respectively, then \(A_1+A_2\) is distributed as \(W_p(m_1+m_2,\Sigma)\). That is, the degrees of freedom add.
  2. If \(A\) is distributed as \(W_p(m,\Sigma)\), then \(CAC'\) is distributed as \(W_p(m,C\Sigma C')\). Here \(C\) is a \(p\times p\) invertible matrix.

Hypothesis Testing on One Sample Mean

Recall the hypotheses in the case of one dimension \[ H_0:\mu=\mu_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0 \] we have the Test Statistic \[ t=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}\overset{H_0}{\sim}t_{n-1}\qquad\text{and}\qquad t^2=(\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n})^2\overset{H_0}{\sim}F_{1,n-1} \]

Now for the case of p-dimension \[ H_0:\boldsymbol \mu=\boldsymbol \mu_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\boldsymbol \mu\ne\boldsymbol \mu_0 \] we similarly have a Test Statistic \[ T^2=\sqrt n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'S^{-1}\sqrt n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)=n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0) \] and the distribution of the Hotelling's T2 statisic \[ \frac{n-p}{p}\frac{T^2}{n-1}\overset{H_0}{\sim} F_{p,n-p} \]

Thus we reject \(H_0\) under significance level \(\alpha\) if \[ T^2>\frac{p(n-1)}{n-p}F_{p,n-p,\alpha} \]

Properties of Hotelling's T-Squared Statisic

Hotelling's T2 statisic is invariant to changes in the units of measurements for \(\boldsymbol X\).

Proof

Suppose \(C\) is a non-singular matrix and \[ \underset{(p\times1)}{\boldsymbol Y}=\underset{(p\times p)}C\underset{(p\times1)}{\boldsymbol X}+\underset{(p\times1)}{\boldsymbol d} \] Then we have \[ \bar{\boldsymbol Y}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n\boldsymbol Y_i}{n}=C\bar{\boldsymbol X}+\boldsymbol d \qquad\text{and}\qquad S_{\boldsymbol Y}=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{j=1}^n(\boldsymbol Y_j-\bar{\boldsymbol Y})(\boldsymbol Y_j-\bar{\boldsymbol Y})'=CSC' \] We also denote \[ \boldsymbol \mu_{\boldsymbol Y}=C\boldsymbol \mu+\boldsymbol d\qquad\boldsymbol \mu_{\boldsymbol Y,0}=C\boldsymbol \mu_0+\boldsymbol d \] Thus we have \[ \begin{aligned} T^2&=n(\bar{\boldsymbol Y}-\boldsymbol \mu_{\boldsymbol Y,0})'S_{\boldsymbol Y}^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol Y}-\boldsymbol \mu_{\boldsymbol Y,0})\\ &=n(C(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0))'(CSC')^{-1}(C(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0))\\ &=n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)C'(C')^{-1}S^{-1}C^{-1}C(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)\\ &=n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)\\ \end{aligned} \]

Hotelling's T-Squared vs. LRT

Consider the point null hypothesis \[ H_0:\boldsymbol \mu=\boldsymbol \mu_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\boldsymbol \mu\ne\boldsymbol \mu_0 \]

Then the corresponding likelihood ratio test statistic \[ \Lambda=\frac{\max_{H_0}L(\boldsymbol \mu_0,\Sigma)}{\max_{H_0\cup H_1}L(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)}=(\frac{|\sum_j(\boldsymbol X_j-\boldsymbol \mu_0)(\boldsymbol X_j-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'|}{|\sum_j(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})'|})^{-\frac{n}{2}}=(\frac{|\hat \Sigma_0|}{|\hat \Sigma|})^{-\frac{n}{2}} \]

In the case of one dimension \[ \frac{|\hat \Sigma_0|}{|\hat \Sigma|}=\frac{\sum_j(X_j-\mu_0)^2}{\sum_j(X_j-\bar X)^2}=\frac{\sum_j(X_j-\bar X)^2+\sum_j(\bar X-\mu_0)^2}{\sum_j(X_j-\bar X)^2}=1+\frac{t^2}{n-1} \] since we have \[ \frac{t^2}{n-1}=\frac{1}{n-1}(\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n})^2=\frac{n(\bar X-\mu_0)^2}{(n-1)s^2}=\frac{n(\bar X-\mu_0)^2}{\sum_j(X_j-\bar X)^2} \]

Similarly in the case of p-dimension \[ \Lambda=\frac{\max_{H_0}L(\boldsymbol \mu_0,\Sigma)}{\max_{H_0\cup H_1}L(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)}=(\frac{|\hat \Sigma_0|}{|\hat \Sigma|})^{-\frac{n}{2}}=(1+\frac{T^2}{n-1})^{-\frac{n}{2}} \] with \[ T^2=n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0) \]

Therefore, we can obtain \(T^2\) easily from determinant, avoiding the calculation of \(S^{-1}\). \[ T^2=(n-1)\frac{|\hat \Sigma_0|}{|\hat \Sigma|}-(n-1)=(n-1)\frac{|\sum_j(\boldsymbol X_j-\boldsymbol \mu_0)(\boldsymbol X_j-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'|}{|\sum_j(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_j-\bar{\boldsymbol X})'|}-(n-1) \]

Confidence Regions

Recall the case of one dimension \[ z_1,\dots,z_n\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N(\psi,\sigma^2) \] and we denote \[ \bar z=\frac{\sum_iz_i}{n}\qquad\text{and}\qquad s^2=\frac{\sum_i(z_i-\bar z)^2}{n-1} \] Thus we have \[ (\frac{\bar z-\psi}{s/\sqrt n})^2\sim F_{1,n-1} \] and the \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) condidence interval for \(\psi\) is \[ I=\{\psi:\frac{n(\bar z-\psi)^2}{s^2}\le c^2\} \] where \[ c^2=F_{1,n-1,\alpha}=t^2_{n-1,\alpha/2} \]

Similarly in the case of p-dimension \[ \boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\overset{i.i.d.}{\sim}N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma) \] and we denote \[ \bar{\boldsymbol X}=\frac{\sum_i\boldsymbol X_i}{n} \qquad \text{and}\qquad S=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_i(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})' \] Thus we have \[ \frac{n-p}{p}\frac{T^2}{n-1}=\frac{n-p}{p}\frac{n}{n-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\sim F_{p,n-p} \] and the \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence region for \(\boldsymbol \mu\) is \[ R=\{\boldsymbol \mu:n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\le c^2\} \] where \[ c^2=\frac{p(n-1)}{n-p}F_{p,n-p,\alpha} \]

Individual Coverage Intervals

Consider linear combinations \[ z_i=\boldsymbol a'\boldsymbol X_i\sim N(\boldsymbol a'\boldsymbol \mu,\boldsymbol a'\Sigma\boldsymbol a) \] and we denote \[ \psi=\boldsymbol a'\boldsymbol \mu\qquad\text{and}\qquad \sigma^2=\boldsymbol a'\Sigma\boldsymbol a \] thus we have \[ \bar z=\frac{\sum_iz_i}{n}=\boldsymbol a'\bar{\boldsymbol X} \qquad\text{and}\qquad s^2=\frac{\sum_i(z_i-\bar z)^2}{n-1}=\boldsymbol a'S\boldsymbol a \] So the \(100(1-\beta)\%\) confidence interval for the mean has the form \[ I=\bar z\pm t_{n-1,\beta/2}\cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt n}=\boldsymbol a'\bar{\boldsymbol X}\pm t_{n-1,\beta/2}\cdot\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'\frac{S}{n}\boldsymbol a} \]

Simultaneous Coverage Intervals

Let \(C_k\) denote the event \(\{I_k\text{ covers }\psi_k\}\), assuming each \(I_k\) is a \(100(1-\beta)\%\) confidence interval for \(\psi_k\). \[ P(\text{some }C_k\text{ false})\le \sum_{k=1}^mP(C_k\text{ false})=\sum_{k=1}^m\beta=m\beta \] By Bonferroni's Inequaliy \[ P(\text{all }C_k\text{ true})=1-P(\text{some }C_k\text{ false})\ge1-m\beta=1-\alpha \] So this simultaneous Confidence Interval is conservative. We call them Bonferroni Intervals \[ I_k=\bar z_k\pm t_{n-1,\alpha/2m}\cdot \frac{s_k}{\sqrt n} \]

We actually can find a lower bound for \(c\) to make the confidence interval cover \(\boldsymbol a'\boldsymbol \mu\) for all \(\boldsymbol a'\ne\boldsymbol 0\). \[ I=\bar z\pm c\cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt n}=\boldsymbol a'\bar{\boldsymbol X}\pm c\cdot\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'\frac{S}{n}\boldsymbol a} \] We consider the following probability \[ P(\forall\boldsymbol a\in\mathbb R^p,\frac{|\boldsymbol a'(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)|}{\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'\frac{S}{n}\boldsymbol a}}\le c)=1-\alpha \] so we consider the maximization problem \[ \max_{\boldsymbol a\ne\boldsymbol 0}(\frac{|\boldsymbol a'(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)|}{\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'\frac{S}{n}\boldsymbol a}})^2=n\cdot\max_{\boldsymbol a\ne\boldsymbol 0}\frac{(\boldsymbol a'(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu))^2}{\boldsymbol a'S\boldsymbol a}=n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'=T^2 \] Thus another type of interval with a simultaneous coverage property under the normal likelihood is the \(T^2\) interval \[ I=\boldsymbol a'\bar{\boldsymbol X}\pm c\cdot\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'\frac{S}{n}\boldsymbol a} \] where \[ c^2=T^2_{\alpha}=\frac{p(n-1)}{n-p}F_{p,n-p,\alpha} \]

Notations for Paired Samples

\[ {X_1}= \begin{bmatrix} X_{111} & X_{112} & \cdots & X_{11p} \\ X_{121} & X_{122} & \cdots & X_{12p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ X_{1n1} & X_{1n2} & \cdots & X_{1np} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

\[ {X_2}= \begin{bmatrix} X_{211} & X_{212} & \cdots & X_{21p} \\ X_{221} & X_{222} & \cdots & X_{22p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ X_{2n1} & X_{2n2} & \cdots & X_{2np} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Then we denote the difference matrix for the paired samples \[ {D}= \begin{bmatrix} D_{11} & D_{12} & \cdots & D_{1p} \\ D_{21} & D_{22} & \cdots & D_{2p} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ D_{n1} & D_{n2} & \cdots & D_{np} \\ \end{bmatrix} \] where \(D_{ij}=X_{1ij}-X_{2ij}\)

We also denote the mean vector and the covariance matrix for \(\boldsymbol D_j=\boldsymbol X_{1j}-\boldsymbol X_{2j}\) \[ E(\boldsymbol D_j)=\boldsymbol \delta=\begin{bmatrix} \delta_1\\ \delta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \delta_n \end{bmatrix}\qquad Cov(\boldsymbol D_j)=\Sigma_d \] And we have sample statistics \[ \bar{\boldsymbol D}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n\boldsymbol D_j\qquad S_d=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{j=1}^n(\boldsymbol D_j-\bar{\boldsymbol D})(\boldsymbol D_j-\bar{\boldsymbol D})' \] Then when we consider problems with respect to the hypothesis tesing, the confidence regions, and the confidence intervals, the analyzing and computing process should be the same as Inference for One Population.

Comparing Mean Vectors from Two Populations: Equal Variance and Normal Distribution

Consider the independent random samples \(\boldsymbol X_{11},\boldsymbol X_{12},\dots,\boldsymbol X_{1n_1}\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_1,\Sigma)\) and \(\boldsymbol X_{21},\boldsymbol X_{22},\dots,\boldsymbol X_{2n_2}\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_2,\Sigma)\)

  • Note that when \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) are relatively small, we assume \(\Sigma_1=\Sigma_2=\Sigma\).

Thus we have \[ E(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=E(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1})-E(\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2\qquad Cov(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=Cov(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1})+Cov(\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})\Sigma \]

Hotelling's T-Squared for Two Populations

Heuristically, consider that both \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) are unbiased estimator for \(\Sigma\), with \((n_1-1)\) d.f. and \((n_2-1)\) d.f. respectively.

We could combine the information together to get another estimator for \(\Sigma\) \[ S_{\text{pooled}}=\frac{\sum_{j=1}^{n_1}(\boldsymbol X_{1j}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_1})(\boldsymbol X_{1j}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_1})'+\sum_{j=1}^{n_2}(\boldsymbol X_{2j}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})(\boldsymbol X_{2j}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})'}{n_1+n_2-2}=\frac{(n_1-1)S_1}{n_1+n_2-2}+\frac{(n_2-1)S_2}{n_1+n_2-2} \] and we know from Properties of Wishart Distribution that \[ (n_1+n_2-2)\cdot S_{\text{pooled}}\sim W_p(n_1+n_2-2,\Sigma) \]

One Population vs. Two Population

Since we know the point estimation for one population \[ \sqrt{n}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\sim N_p(\boldsymbol 0,\Sigma)\qquad(n-1)S\sim W_p(n-1, \Sigma) \] And the corresponding Hotelling's T2 Statistic \[ T^2=\sqrt n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'S^{-1}\sqrt n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)\sim \frac{p(n-1)}{(n-1)-(p-1)}F_{p,(n-1)-(p-1)} \]

Consider the point null hypothesis \[ H_0:\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2=\boldsymbol \delta_0\quad\text{vs.}\quad H_1:\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2\ne\boldsymbol \delta_0 \] We can similarly use the point estimation for two population \[ (\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-(\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2))\sim N_p(\boldsymbol 0,\Sigma)\qquad(n_1+n_2-2)S_{\text{pooled}}\sim W_p(n_1+n_2-2, \Sigma) \] To construct Hotelling's T2 Statistic \[ T^2=(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta_0)'S_{\text{pooled}}^{-1}(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})^{-\frac{1}{2}}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta_0) \] And under \(H_0\) we have \[ \frac{(n_1+n_2-2)-(p-1)}{p}\frac{T^2}{n_1+n_2-2}\sim F_{p,(n_1+n_2-2)-(p-1)} \] Thus we reject \(H_0\) under significance level \(\alpha\) if \[ T^2>\frac{p(n_1+n_2-2)}{n_1+n_2-p-1}F_{p,n_1+n_2-p-1,\alpha} \]

Confidence Region & Simultaneous Coverage Intervals

Equivalently, the corresponding \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence region is \[ R=\{\boldsymbol \delta:(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta)'S_{\text{pooled}}^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta)\le c^2\} \] where \[ c^2=\frac{p(n_1+n_2-2)}{n_1+n_2-p-1}F_{p,n_1+n_2-p-1,\alpha} \]

We also have the \(T^2\) interval \[ I=\boldsymbol a'(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})\pm c\cdot\sqrt{(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})\boldsymbol a'S_{\text{pooled}}\boldsymbol a} \] where \[ c^2=\frac{p(n_1+n_2-2)}{n_1+n_2-p-1}F_{p,n_1+n_2-p-1,\alpha} \]

Which Direction Quantifies the Largest Population Difference

Our Goal is to find a vector \(\boldsymbol a\) to maximize the value of \[ |\frac{\boldsymbol a'(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})}{\sqrt{(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2})\boldsymbol a'S_{\text{pooled}}\boldsymbol a}}| \]

And according to Maximization Lemma, we get the solution \[ \boldsymbol a=c\cdot S_{\text{pooled}}^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}) \]

Large Sample Behavior of Sample Mean and Covariance

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be independent observations from any population with mean \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and finite covariance \(\Sigma\). We denote \[ \bar{\boldsymbol X}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n\boldsymbol X_i}{n} \qquad \text{and}\qquad S=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})' \]

Law of Large Numbers

\(\bar{\boldsymbol X}\) converges in probability to \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and \(S\) converges in probability to \(\Sigma\) \[ \bar{\boldsymbol X}\overset{p}\to\boldsymbol \mu\qquad{\text{and}}\qquad S\overset{p}\to\Sigma \] Proof \[ \begin{aligned} S&=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})'\\ &=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(\boldsymbol X_i+\boldsymbol \mu-\boldsymbol \mu-\bar{\boldsymbol X})(\boldsymbol X_i-\bar{\boldsymbol X})'\\ &=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(\boldsymbol X_i-\boldsymbol \mu)(\boldsymbol X_i-\boldsymbol \mu)'+\frac{n}{n-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'\to\Sigma \end{aligned} \]

The Central Limit Theorem

\(\sqrt n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\) is apporximately distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol 0,\Sigma)\) and \(n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\) is apporximately distributed as \(\chi^2_p\) \[ \sqrt n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\overset{d}\to N_p(\boldsymbol 0,\Sigma)\qquad{\text{and}}\qquad n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu)\overset{d}\to\chi^2_p \]

Large Sample Inference about Mean Vector

Let \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) be a random sample from a population with mean \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and positive definite covariance \(\Sigma\), and we assume \(n-p\) is large.

Hypothesis Tesing & Simultaneous Confidence Intervals

Consider the point null hypothesis \[ H_0:\boldsymbol \mu=\boldsymbol \mu_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\boldsymbol \mu\ne\boldsymbol \mu_0 \]

We reject \(H_0\) under significance level \(\alpha\) if \[ n(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)'S^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol \mu_0)>\chi^2_{p,\alpha} \] where \(\chi^2_{p,\alpha}\) is the upper \(\alpha\) percentile of a chi-square distribution with p d.f.

Note that when the tesing is appropriate (i.e. n is relatively large to p), the results are the same as for normal case \[ \frac{p(n-1)}{n-p}F_{p,n-p,\alpha}\approx\chi^2_{p,\alpha}\qquad\text{when }(n-p)\text{ is relatively large} \]

We similarly have the Simultaneous Confidence Intervals \[ I=\boldsymbol a'\bar{\boldsymbol X}\pm c\cdot\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'\frac{S}{n}\boldsymbol a} \] where \[ c^2=\chi^2_{p,\alpha} \]

Comparing Mean Vectors from Two Populations: Unequal Variance and Large Sample

Consider the independent random samples \(\boldsymbol X_{11},\boldsymbol X_{12},\dots,\boldsymbol X_{1n_1}\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_1,\Sigma_1)\) and \(\boldsymbol X_{21},\boldsymbol X_{22},\dots,\boldsymbol X_{2n_2}\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_2,\Sigma_2)\)

  • Note that when \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) are relatively large compared to \(p\), we allow \(\Sigma_1\ne\Sigma_2\).

Thus we have \[ E(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=E(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1})-E(\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2\qquad Cov(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=Cov(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1})+Cov(\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})=\frac{1}{n_1}\Sigma_1+\frac{1}{n_2}\Sigma_2 \]

By CLT we have \[ \bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}\overset{d}\to N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2,\frac{1}{n_1}\Sigma_1+\frac{1}{n_2}\Sigma_2) \] By LLN we have \[ S_1\overset{p}\to\Sigma_1\qquad{\text{and}}\qquad S_2\overset{p}\to\Sigma_2 \] thus \[ \frac{1}{n_1}S_1+\frac{1}{n_2}S_2\overset{p}\to\frac{1}{n_1}\Sigma_1+\frac{1}{n_2}\Sigma_2 \]

Similarly we have \[ (\frac{1}{n_1}\Sigma_1+\frac{1}{n_2}\Sigma_2)^{-\frac{1}{2}}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta)\overset{d}\to N_p(\boldsymbol 0,I_p) \] and by Slustky's Theorem we have \[ \qquad (\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta)'(\frac{1}{n_1}S_1+\frac{1}{n_2}S_2)^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta)\overset{d}\to \chi_p^2 \]

Hypothesis Tesing & Simultaneous Confidence Intervals

Consider the point null hypothesis \[ H_0:\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2=\boldsymbol \delta_0\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\boldsymbol \mu_1-\boldsymbol \mu_2\ne\boldsymbol \delta_0 \]

We reject \(H_0\) under significance level \(\alpha\) if \[ \qquad (\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta_0)'(\frac{1}{n_1}S_1+\frac{1}{n_2}S_2)^{-1}(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2}-\boldsymbol \delta_0)>\chi^2_{p,\alpha} \] where \(\chi^2_{p,\alpha}\) is the upper \(\alpha\) percentile of a chi-square distribution with p d.f.

We similarly have the Simultaneous Confidence Intervals \[ I=\boldsymbol a'(\bar{\boldsymbol X_1}-\bar{\boldsymbol X_2})\pm c\cdot\sqrt{\boldsymbol a'(\frac{1}{n_1}S_1+\frac{1}{n_2}S_2)\boldsymbol a} \] where \[ c^2=\chi^2_{p,\alpha} \]

Assumptions Underlying T-Squared Statistics

  • Equal variance. The data from both populations have common variance-covariance matrix \(\Sigma\).
  • Independence. The subjects from both populations are independently sampled. Note that this does not mean that the variables are independent of one another.
  • Normality. Both populations are multivariate normally distributed.

Testing Equal Variance Matrix for Two Populations

Consider the independent random samples \(\boldsymbol X_{11},\boldsymbol X_{12},\dots,\boldsymbol X_{1n_1}\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_1,\Sigma_1)\) and \(\boldsymbol X_{21},\boldsymbol X_{22},\dots,\boldsymbol X_{2n_2}\) from \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu_2,\Sigma_2)\)

Consider the point null hypothesis \[ H_0:\Sigma_1=\Sigma_2\quad \text{vs.}\quad H_1:\Sigma_1\ne\Sigma_2 \]

LRT for Testing Equal Variance

We denote the likelihood ratio test statistic \[ \Lambda=\frac{\max_{\theta\in\Theta_0}L(\theta)}{\max_{\theta\in\Theta}L(\theta)}=\prod_{i=1}^2(\frac{|S_i|}{|S_{\text{pooled}}|})^{\frac{n_i-1}{2}} \] Note the degree of freedom \[ \nu-\nu_0=(\text{dimension of }\Theta)-(\text{dimension of }\Theta_0) \]

Then under \(H_0\), asymptotically we have \[ -2\ln\Lambda=(n_1+n_2-2)\ln|S_{\text{pooled}}|-\sum_{i=1}^2(n_i-1)\ln|S_i|\sim \chi^2_{\nu-\nu_0} \]

Box's M Test: A Modified LRT

For samples from two multivariate normal population, this statistic follows a chi-square distribution approximately under \(H_0\) \[ (1-u)(-2\ln\Lambda)\sim\chi^2_\nu \] where the finite population correction factor \[ u=(\sum_i\frac{1}{n_i-1}-\frac{1}{\sum_i(n_i-1)})(\frac{2p^2+3p-1}{6(p+1)(2-1)}) \]

It's sensitive to departures from normality.

Statistical Testing

  • Goodness-of-fit tests (many are based on the empirical CDF)
    • Kolmogorov-Smirnov. Can also be used for testing other distributions.

The larger the JB statistic, the more the data deviates from the normal.
  • Consistent procedures based on the empirical characteristic function
    • Epps & Pulley (1983), competitive with the Shapiro-Wilk Test

Introduction to Principal Component Analysis

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is concerned with explaining the variance-covariance structure of a set of variables through a few linear combinations of these variables. The general objectives are Data Reduction and Interpretation.

PCA was invented in 1901 by Karl Pearson and was later independently developed and named by Harold Hotelling in the 1930s. It is now one of the most important and widely used statistical techniques in many various fields. About 1.5 million papers were published in the last ten years containing some mention of PCA.

Population Principle Component

Motivation

Let the random vector \(\boldsymbol X'=[X_1,X_2,\dots,X_p]\) have the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) with eigen values \[ \lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\dots\ge\lambda_p\ge0 \]

Our goal is to find First Principal Component \[ Y_1=\boldsymbol a_1'\boldsymbol X=a_{11}X_1+a_{12}X_2+\dots+a_{1p}X_p=\sum_{i=1}^pa_{1i}X_i \] that maximizes \(Var(Y_1)=\boldsymbol a_1'\Sigma\boldsymbol a_1\)

Some quick observations

  • What is the variance of \(Y_1'\) when we use \(c\cdot \boldsymbol a_1\) instead of \(\boldsymbol a_1\)
  • We should restrict attention to coefficient vectors of unit length or equivalently \[ \max_{\boldsymbol a_1\ne\boldsymbol 0}\frac{\boldsymbol a_1'\Sigma\boldsymbol a_1}{\boldsymbol a_1'\boldsymbol a_1} \]

And we also want to find Second Principal Component \[ Y_2=\boldsymbol a_2'\boldsymbol X=a_{21}X_1+a_{22}X_2+\dots+a_{2p}X_p \] that maximizes \(Var(Y_2)=\boldsymbol a_2'\Sigma\boldsymbol a_2\) subject to \[ \boldsymbol a_2'\boldsymbol a_2=1\qquad\text{and}\qquad Cov(Y_1,Y_2)=Cov(\boldsymbol a_1'\boldsymbol X,\boldsymbol a_2'\boldsymbol X)=\boldsymbol a_1'\Sigma\boldsymbol a_2=0 \]

How to Find Principal Component

Let \(\Sigma\) be the covariance matrix of the random vector \(\boldsymbol X'=[X_1,X_2,\dots,X_p]\). And let \(\{(\lambda_i,\boldsymbol e_i),i=1,\dots,p\}\) be the eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs of \(\Sigma\) with \(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\dots\ge\lambda_p\ge0\). Then, according to Maximization of Quadratic Forms for Points on the Unit Sphere, the ith principal component (score) is given by \[ Y_i=\boldsymbol e_i'\boldsymbol X=e_{i1}X_1+e_{i2}X_2+\dots+e_{ip}X_p\quad\text{for}\quad i=1,2,\dots,p \] With these choices, we have some properties \[ Var(Y_i)=\boldsymbol e_i'\Sigma\boldsymbol e_i\qquad\text{and}\qquad Cov(Y_i,Y_j)=\boldsymbol e_i'\Sigma\boldsymbol e_j=0 \] Note that if some eigenvalues are equal, the choices of the corresponding coefficient vectors and hence the principal components are not unique.

How to Interpret Principal Component

Note that \[ \sum_{i=1}^pVar(X_i)=\sigma_{11}+\sigma_{22}+\dots+\sigma_{pp}=\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\dots+\lambda_p=\sum_{i=1}^pVar(Y_i) \] Then the eigenvalues can be seen as the percentage of total population variance due to the corresponding kth PC \[ \frac{\lambda_k}{\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\dots+\lambda_p} \] Consider the ith Principal Component \[ Y_i=\boldsymbol e_i'\boldsymbol X=e_{i1}X_1+e_{i2}X_2+\dots+e_{ip}X_p \] Then \(e_{ik}\) of the coefficient vector \(\boldsymbol e_i\) can be interpreted as the importance of the kth variable \(X_k\) to the ith component \(Y_i\)

And we can obtain the covariance between the kth variable \(X_k\) to the ith component \(Y_i\) \[ Cov(Y_i,X_k)=Cov(\boldsymbol e_i'\boldsymbol X,\boldsymbol 1_k'\boldsymbol X)=\boldsymbol e_i'\Sigma\boldsymbol 1_k=\lambda_i\cdot\boldsymbol e_i'\boldsymbol 1_k=\lambda_i\cdot e_{ik} \] Naturally we can further obtain the correlation coefficient \(\rho\) \[ \rho_{Y_i,X_k}=\frac{e_{ik}\sqrt{\lambda_i}}{\sqrt{\sigma_{kk}}} \] And we have \[ \sum_{i=1}^p\rho_{Y_i,X_k}^2=1 \] This means this correlation coefficient can be seen as the proportion of the variance of \(X_k\) explained by the correlation with \(Y_i\)

Principal Components Based on Standardized Variables

We have the Standardized Variables \[ Z_k=\frac{X_k-\mu_k}{\sqrt{\sigma_{kk}}}\quad\text{for}\quad k=1,2,\dots p \] Or equivalently we have \[ \boldsymbol Z=V^{-1/2}(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu) \] with \[ V=\begin{bmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & \sigma_{pp} \\ \end{bmatrix} \] Thus we have \[ Cov(\boldsymbol Z)=V^{-1/2}\Sigma V^{-1/2}=\boldsymbol \rho \]

Similarly the ith principal components of the standardized variables \(\boldsymbol Z'=[Z_1,Z_2,\dots,Z_p]\) with \(Cov(\boldsymbol Z)=\boldsymbol \rho\) is given by \[ Y_i=\boldsymbol e_i'\boldsymbol Z=e_{i1}Z_1+e_{i2}Z_2+\dots+e_{ip}Z_p \] where \(\{(\lambda_i,\boldsymbol e_i),i=1,\dots,p\}\) are the eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs of the population correlation matrix \(\boldsymbol \rho\).

Moreover, we similarly have the properties of the principal components \[ \sum_{i=1}^pVar(Y_i)=\sum_{i=1}^pVar(Z_i)=p \]

\[ \rho_{Y_i,Z_k}=e_{ik}\sqrt{\lambda_i} \]

Population Principal Components under Normality Assumption

Suppose \(\boldsymbol X\) is distributed as \(N_p(\boldsymbol \mu,\Sigma)\) and recall Geometry of Multivariate Normal Distribution.

Sample Principal Component

Suppose \(\boldsymbol X_1,\boldsymbol X_2,\dots,\boldsymbol X_n\) represent \(n\) independent drawings from some p-dimensional population with mean vector \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma\). And we have sample mean vector \(\bar{\boldsymbol X}\), sample covariance matrix \(S\), and sample correlation matrix \(R\).

Our objective is to identify linear combinations of the measured characteristics that - Account for much of the variation in the sample - Uncorrelated with each other

How to Find Sample Principal Components

Employ the same procedure as before but

  • Replace population distribution by empirical distribution
  • Replace population covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) by sample covariance matrix \(S\)
  • Replace population correlation matrix \(\boldsymbol \rho\) by sample correlation matrix \(R\)

Sample Principal Components and Properties

Let \(S\) be the \(p\times p\) sample covariance matrix with eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs \(\{(\hat \lambda_i,\hat{\boldsymbol e_i}),i=1,\dots,p\}\). Then the ith sample principal component (score) is given by \[ \hat y_i=\hat{\boldsymbol e_i}\boldsymbol x=\hat e_{i1}x_1+\hat e_{i2}x_2+\dots+\hat e_{ip}x_p\quad\text{for}\quad i=1,2,\dots,p \] Sample Variance and Sample Covariance \[ s^2(\hat y_k)=\hat \lambda_k\qquad s(\hat y_i,\hat y_j)=0 \] Total Sample Variance \[ \sum_{k=1}^ps^2(\hat y_k)=\hat \lambda_1+\hat \lambda_2+\dots+\hat \lambda_p=s_{11}+s_{22}+\dots+s_{pp}=\sum_{k=1}^ps^2(x_k) \] Sample Correlation \[ \rho_{\hat y_i,x_k}=\frac{\hat e_{ik}\sqrt{\hat \lambda_k}}{\sqrt{s_{kk}}} \]

Geometrical Interpretation of Sample Principal Components

  • \(\hat \lambda_1>\hat \lambda_2\) indicates unique directions for principal components
  • \(\hat \lambda_1\approx\hat \lambda_2\) indicates no unique directions for principal components

When variances in these two directions can be seen as having no significant differences, then taking the two directions as principal components simply doesn't make much sense.

PCA Based on Sample Correlation Matrix

If \(\boldsymbol z_1,\boldsymbol z_2,\dots,\boldsymbol z_p\) are standardized observations with covariance matrix \(R\), the ith sample principal component is given by \[ \hat y_i=\hat{\boldsymbol e_i}\boldsymbol z=\hat e_{i1}z_1+\hat e_{i2}z_2+\dots+\hat e_{ip}z_p\quad\text{for}\quad i=1,2,\dots,p \] where \(\{(\hat \lambda_i,\hat{\boldsymbol e_i}),i=1,\dots,p\}\) are the eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs of \(R\)

Lecture 7: Discussion and Extensions on PCA

Geometrical Interpretation of Dimension Reduction with PCA

If we choose approximation with only first \(r\) principle components, then it means we project the data onto the corresponding r-dimensional plane. Among the projections to all possible r-dimensional planes, the one above enjoys the minimal sum of squared distances \(\sum_{j=1}^nd_j^2\) between the observations and the plane.

Model

We project a random vector \(\boldsymbol X\) in \(\mathbb R^p\) with mean vector \(\boldsymbol \mu\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) onto a plane in a lower dimension \(\mathbb R^r\)

  • location – constant vector \(\boldsymbol c\)
  • direction – orthogonal basis \(U=[\boldsymbol u_1,\dots,\boldsymbol u_r]\)
  • approximation – coefficients \(\boldsymbol b'=[b_1,\dots,b_r]\)

The objective function (optimization problem) is \[ \min_{\boldsymbol c,U,\boldsymbol b}E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol c-U\boldsymbol b)'(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol c-U\boldsymbol b)) \] Since the motivation is to minimize \[ E(\boldsymbol d'\boldsymbol d)\quad\text{and}\quad \boldsymbol d=\boldsymbol X-\tilde{\boldsymbol X}=(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol c)-(\tilde{\boldsymbol X}-\boldsymbol c)=(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol c)-U\boldsymbol b \] where \(U\boldsymbol b\) stands for the projection of \(\boldsymbol X\) on the plane

Idea

Optimization order is \[ \boldsymbol b\to\boldsymbol c\to U \]

How to find the best approximation given a plane decided by \(\boldsymbol c\) and \(U\)

The intuition is that projection is the closest approximation \[ \hat{\boldsymbol b}=U'(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol c) \]

How to find the best location given the direction of the plane decided by \(U\)

The intuition is that the mean vector \(\boldsymbol \mu\) is the closest location \[ \hat{\boldsymbol c}=\boldsymbol \mu \]

Then optimize the simplified objective function directly to find the direction \(U\) \[ \min_{U}E(\boldsymbol d'\boldsymbol d) \] where \[ \boldsymbol d=(\boldsymbol X-\hat{\boldsymbol c})-U\hat{\boldsymbol b}=(I-UU')(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu) \] Thus the optimization problem becomes \[ \min_{U}E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'(I-UU')^2(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu))=\min_{U}E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'(I-UU')(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)) \] And we use some familiar tricks w.r.t. trace \[ \begin{aligned} \min_{U}E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'(I-UU')(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu))&=\min_{U}tr(E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'(I-UU')(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)))\\ &=\min_{U}tr((I-UU')E((\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)(\boldsymbol X-\boldsymbol \mu)'))\\ &=\min_{U}tr((I-UU')\Sigma)\\ &=tr(\Sigma)-\max_{U}tr(UU'\Sigma)\\ &=tr(\Sigma)-\max_{U}tr(U'\Sigma U) \end{aligned} \] And this gives the solution \[ U=[\boldsymbol e_1,\dots,\boldsymbol e_p] \] where \(\{(\lambda_i,\boldsymbol e_i),i=1,\dots,p\}\) are the eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs of \(\Sigma\) with \(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\dots\ge\lambda_p\ge0\)

Interpretation

The intuition is that \(U\) is not unique regarding the redution to the r-dimention.

However, if we increase the dimensionality from one to \(r\) dimensions dim-by-dim, and require that each dimension enjoys the minimal sum of squared distances, then the corresponding \(U\) is unique, which is consistent with the results obtained from principal component analysis.

Alternatively, among the projections to all possible r-dimensional planes, the one above also enjoys the largest spread among the shadows of the observations.
