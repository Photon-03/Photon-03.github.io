INTERMEDIATE MICROECONOMICS

Posted on 2023-04-15 Edited on 2023-04-16

My notes on the 2023 spring Intermediate Microeconomics course taught by Professor Xingye Wu from School of Economics and Management, Tsinghua University.

Lecture 2: Budget Constraint & Preferences

Budget Constraint

Consumption Choice Sets

A consumption choice set is the collection of all consumption choices available to the consumer.

Budget Constraints

Consumer plans to spend money on \(n\) goods with a bundle \((x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)\).

A bundle \((x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)\) is affordable at prices \((p_1,p_2,\dots,p_n)\) when \[ p_1x_1+p_2x_2+\dots p_nx_n\le m \] where \(m\) is the consumer's income.

The consumer's budget set is the set of all affordable bundles \[ B(p_1,\dots,p_n,m)=\{(x_1,\dots,x_n)|x_1\ge0,\dots,x_n\ge0\quad\text{and}\quad p_1x_1+p_2x_2+\dots+p_nx_n\le m\} \] The budget line is the upper boundary of the budget set.

Budget Set for Two Commodities

Budget line is \[ p_1x_1+p_2x_2=m \]

  • Not affordable – above the budget line
  • Just affordable – on the budget line
  • Affordable with some money left – below the budget line

\[ x_2=-\frac{p_1}{p_2}x_1+\frac{m}{p_2} \]

For \(n=2\) and \(x_1\) on the horizontal axis, the slope of the budget line is \(-\frac{p_1}{p_2}\).

  • Increasing \(x_1\) by 1 must reduce \(x_2\) by \(\frac{p_1}{p_2}\).
  • On the budget line, getting an extra unit of commodity 1 means forgoing \(\frac{p_1}{p_2}\) units of commodity 2.
  • Opportunity cost / Trade-off

Budget Set for Three Commodities

\[ \{(x_1,x_2,x_3)|x_1\ge0,x_2\ge0,x_3\ge0\quad\text{and}\quad p_1x_1+p_2x_2+p_3x_3\le m\} \]

Budget line becomes budget plane.

Notes on Units of Measure

Economists tend to be sloppy in terms of units of measure.

If that bothers you, you may follow the standard international system of units.

Income and Price Changes

The budget set depend upon prices and income.

Higher income gives more choice

Original and new budget lines are parallel.

How does the budget set change as \(p_1\) decreases from \(p_1\) to \(p_1'\)

Budget line rotates around the vertical intercept.

The slope is less negative (from \(-\frac{p_1}{p_2}\) to \(-\frac{p_1'}{p_2}\)).

Uniform Ad Valorem Sales Taxes

An ad valorem sales tax levied at a rate of \(p\) increases all prices by \(tp\) from \(p\) to \((1+t)p\).

A uniform sales tax is applied uniformly to all commodities, which changes the constraint from \[ p_1x_1+p_2x_2\le m \] to \[ (1+t)p_1x_1+(1+t)p_2x_2\le m \] i.e. \[ p_1x_1+p_2x_2\le\frac{m}{1+t} \]

The Food Stamp Program

Food stamps are coupons that can be legally exchanged only for food.

\(F+G=100\) without stamps.

We may normalize both prices to 1 by changing the unit of the goods:

Define the amount of food that can be bought with $1 as one basket. Then price of food is $1 per basket by definition.

Budget set after receiving 40 food stamps. Each food stamp can be used as $1 to buy food only. \[ G=\begin{cases} 100,&\quad0\le F\le40\\ 140-F,&\quad40\le F\le140 \end{cases} \]

What if food stamps can be traded on a black market at the price of $0.5

\[ G=\begin{cases} 120-\frac{1}{2}F,&\quad0\le F\le40\\ 140-F,&\quad40\le F\le140 \end{cases} \]

  • Budget line after 40 food stamps issued.
  • Black market trading makes the budget set larger again.

Relative Prices

Suppose prices and income are measured in dollars. Say \(p_1=2,\ p_2=3,\ m=12\). Then the constraint is \[ 2x_1+3x_2\le12 \] It is also \[ x_1+\frac{3}{2}x_2\le6 \] which is the constraint for \(p_1=1,\ p_2=\frac{3}{2},\ m=6\).

Setting \(p_1=1\) makes commodity 1 the numeraire and defines all prices in terms of commodity 1.

Numeraire

Any commodity can be chosen as the numeraire without changing the budget set.

  • Dividing the budget constraint by \(p_k\) will make commodity k the numeraire

Shapes of Budget Set

What if prices are not constants

  • bulk buying discounts, or the opposite, price penalties for buying too much
  • Then lines will be curved

Quantity Discounts / Quantity Penalty

Suppose \(p_2=1\) is constant but that \(p_1=2\) for \(0\le x_1\le20\) and \(p_1=1\) for \(x_1>20\).

Then the slope of the budget line is \[ -\frac{p_1}{p_2}=\begin{cases} -2&\quad0\le x_1\le20\\ -1&\quad x_1>20 \end{cases} \]

One Price Negative

Commodity 1 is stinky garbage. You are paid 2 per unit to accept it, i.e. \(p_1=-2,\ p_2=1\). Income, other than from accepting commodity 1, is \(m=10\).

Then the constraint is \[ -2x_1+x_2\le10 \] Budget set is all bundles for which \(x_1\ge0,\ x_2\ge0\ \text{and}\ x_2\le2x_1+10\).

More General Choice Sets

Choices are usually constrained by more than a budget. A bundle is available only if it meets every constraint.

  • time constraints and other resources constraints

The set of feasible bundles is the intersection.

Preferences

Preference Relations

Three possibilities when comparing two consumption bundles \(\boldsymbol x\) and \(\boldsymbol y\)

  • The consumer strictly prefers \(\boldsymbol x\) to \(\boldsymbol y\), denoted as \(\boldsymbol x\succ\boldsymbol y\).
  • The consumer is indifferent between \(\boldsymbol x\) and \(\boldsymbol y\), denoted as \(\boldsymbol x\sim\boldsymbol y\).
  • The consumer strictly prefers \(\boldsymbol y\) to \(\boldsymbol x\), denote as \(\boldsymbol y\succ\boldsymbol x\).
  • When we say the consumer weakly prefers \(\boldsymbol x\) to \(\boldsymbol y\), denote as \(\boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol y\), we mean Case 1 or 2 above.

Note that

  • \(\boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol y\) and \(\boldsymbol y\succsim\boldsymbol x\) imply \(\boldsymbol x\sim\boldsymbol y\)
  • \(\boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol y\) and (not \(\boldsymbol y\succsim\boldsymbol x\)) imply \(\boldsymbol x\succ\boldsymbol y\)

Assumptions about Preference Relations

Completeness

For any two different bundles \(\boldsymbol x\) and \(\boldsymbol y\) it is always possible to make the statement that either \[ \boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol y \] or \[ \boldsymbol y\succsim\boldsymbol x \]

Reflexivity

Any bundle \(\boldsymbol x\) is always at least as preferred as itself, i.e. \[ \boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol x \]

Transitivity

\(\boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol y\) and \(\boldsymbol y\succsim\boldsymbol z\) imply that \[ \boldsymbol x\succsim\boldsymbol z \] Transitivity of \(\succsim\) implies that \(\sim\) and \(\succ\) are also transitive.

Indifference Curves

Take a reference bundle \(\boldsymbol x\). The set of all bundles equally preferred to \(\boldsymbol x\) is the indifference curve containing \(\boldsymbol x\) – the set of all bundles \(\boldsymbol y\sim\boldsymbol x\).

Different indifference curves cannot intersect.

Since an indifference curve is not always a curve, a better name might be an indifference set.

Slopes of Indifference Curves

When more of a commodity is always preferred, the commodity is a good.

  • If every commodity is a good then indifference curves are negatively sloped.

When less of a commodity is always preferred, the commodity is a bad.

  • One good and one bad lead to a positively sloped indifference curve.

Special Case of Indifference Curves

Perfect Substitutes

If a consumer always regards units of commodities 1 and 2 as equivalent, then the commodities are perfect substitutes and only the sum of the two commodities matters.

  • Slopes are constant at \(-1\).

Perfect Complements

If a consumer always consumes commodities 1 and 2 in fixed proportion, then the commodities are perfect complements and only the number of pairs of units of the two commodities determines the preference rank-order of bundles.

  • L-shape indifference curves

Preferences Exhibiting Satiation

A bundle strictly preferred to any other is a satiation point or a bliss point.

Well-Behaved Preferences

A preference relation is well-behaved if it is monotonic and convex.

Monotonicity

More of any commodity is always preferred, i.e. no satiation and every commodity is a good.

Convexity

Mixtures of two indifferent bundles are weakly preferred to the bundles themselves.

  • \(\boldsymbol z=t\boldsymbol x+(1-t)\boldsymbol y\) is weakly preferred to \(\boldsymbol x\) and \(\boldsymbol y\) for all \(0<t<1\).

Preferences are strictly convex when all mixtures \(\boldsymbol z\) are strictly preferred to their component bundles \(\boldsymbol x\) and \(\boldsymbol y\).

Marginal Rate-of-Substitution

The slope of an indifference curve is its marginal rate-of-substitution (MRS). MRS is the rate at which the consumer is only just willing to exchange commodity 2 for a small amount of commodity 1.

MRS & Indifference Curve Properties

Moving along an indifference curve, MRS increases with \(x_1\) (become less negative) if and only if preferences are strictly convex.

  • MRS decreases (becomes more negative) as \(x_1\) increases indicates nonconvex preferences
  • MRS is not always increasing as \(x_1\) increases indicates nonconvex preferences

Lecture 3: Utility & Choice & Demand

Utility

Utility Functions

A utility function \(U(\boldsymbol x)\) represents a preference relation. \[ \boldsymbol x\succ\boldsymbol y \iff U(\boldsymbol x)>U(\boldsymbol y)\\ \boldsymbol x\prec\boldsymbol y \iff U(\boldsymbol x)<U(\boldsymbol y)\\ \boldsymbol x\sim\boldsymbol y \iff U(\boldsymbol x)=U(\boldsymbol y)\\ \]

Utility Functions & Indifference Curves

  • All bundles in an indifference curve have the same utility level.
  • \(U(x_1,x_2)\equiv k\) is the equation of an indifference curve where \(k\) is a constant.

Goods, Bads and Neutrals

  • A good is a commodity which increases utility (gives a more preferred bundle).
  • A bad is a commodity which decreases utility (gives a less preferred bundle).
  • A neutral is a commodity which does not change utility (gives an equally preferred bundle).

Some Utility Functions and Their Indifference Curves

Perfect Substitution Indifference Curves

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=ax_1+bx_2 \]

Perfect Complementarity Indifference Curves

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=\min \{ax_1,bx_2\} \]

Quasi-linear Indifference Curves

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=f(x_1)+x_2 \]

Cobb-Douglas Indifference Curves

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=x_1^ax_2^b \]

Marginal Utilities

The marginal utility of commodity \(i\) is the rate-of-change of total utility as the quantity of commodity \(i\) consumed changes. \[ MU_i=\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_i} \]

Marginal Utilities and MRS

The general equation for an indifference curve is \(U(x_1,x_2)=k\) where \(k\) is a constant.

Totally differentiating this identity gives \[ \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_1}dx_1+\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_2}dx_2=0 \] which is equivalent to \[ MRS=\frac{dx_2}{dx_1}=-\frac{\partial U/\partial x_1}{\partial U/\partial x_2} \]

MRS for Quasi-linear Utility Functions

A quasi-linear utility function is of the form \(U(x_1,x_2)=f(x_1)+x_2\). \[ \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_1}=f'(x_1)\qquad \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_2}=1 \] Thus \[ MRS=\frac{dx_2}{dx_1}=-\frac{\partial U/\partial x_1}{\partial U/\partial x_2}=-f'(x_1) \] which does not depend upon \(x_2\).

  • The slope of indifference curves for a quasi-linear utility function is constant along vertical lines.

Ordinal vs. Cardinal

Utility is usually an ordinal (i.e. ordering) concept.

If \(U(\boldsymbol x)=6\) and \(U(\boldsymbol y)=2\) then bundle \(\boldsymbol x\) is strictly preferred to bundle \(\boldsymbol x\). But \(\boldsymbol x\) is not preferred three times as much as is \(\boldsymbol y\).

Strictly Increasing Transformation

If \(U\) is a utility function that represents a preference relation and \(f\) is a strictly increasing function, then \(V=f(U)\) is also a utility function representing the same preference relation.

Moreover, note that \[ MRS=-\frac{\partial V/\partial x_1}{\partial V/\partial x_2}=-\frac{f'(U)\cdot\partial U/\partial x_1}{f'(U)\cdot\partial U/\partial x_2}=-\frac{\partial U/\partial x_1}{\partial U/\partial x_2} \] So MRS is unchanged by a strictly increasing transformation.

Choice

Utility Functions & Budget Constraint

The consumer makes her choice as if she is solving a constraint maximization problem.

  • Objective function: Utility Function
  • Constraint: Budget Constraint

Rational Constrained Choice

The most preferred affordable bundle is called the consumer's demand at the given prices and budget.

  • Demands will be denoted by \(x_1^*(p_1,p_2,m)\) and \(x_2^*(p_1,p_2,m)\)
  • \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) is the most preferred affordable bundle

When \(x_1^*>0\) and \(x_2^*>0\), the demanded bundle is said to be interior.

  • \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) is not on the axes

If \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) is on the budget line, we say that the budget constraint is binding.

  • \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) exhausts the budget

\((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) satisfies two conditions – two equations and two unknowns

  1. The budget is binding \[ p_1x_1^*+p_2x_2^*=m \]

  2. Slope of I.C. equals to Slope of B.L. \[ MRS=-\frac{p_1}{p_2} \]

Computing Demands: Cobb-Douglas Example

Suppose that the consumer has Cobb-Douglas preferences. \[ U(x_1,x_2)=x_1^ax_2^b \] Then \[ MU_1=\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_1}=ax_1^{a-1}x_2^b\qquad MU_2=\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_2}=bx_1^ax_2^{b-1} \] So the MRS is \[ MRS=\frac{dx_2}{dx_1}=-\frac{\partial U/\partial x_1}{\partial U/\partial x_2}=-\frac{ax_2}{bx_1} \] At the optimal point \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) \[ MRS=-\frac{p_1}{p_2} \] Thus \[ -\frac{ax_2^*}{bx_1^*}=-\frac{p_1}{p_2} \] \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) also exhausts the budget \[ p_1x_1^*+p_2x_2^*=m \] The two equations above gives the most preferred affordable bundle for a consumer with Cobb-Douglas preferences \[ (x_1^*,x_2^*)=(\frac{am}{(a+b)p_1},\frac{bm}{(a+b)p_2}) \]

Examples of Corner Solutions

The Perfect Substitutes Case

When \(U(x_1,x_2)=x_1+x_2\), the most preferred affordable bundle is \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) where \[ (x_1^*,x_2^*)=(\frac{y}{p_1},0)\quad \text{if}\quad p_1<p_2 \] and \[ (x_1^*,x_2^*)=(0,\frac{y}{p_2})\quad \text{if}\quad p_1>p_2 \] All the bundles on the budget line are equally the most preferred affordable bundle when \(p_1=p_2\).

The Non-Convex Preferences Case

Tangency condition gives us the worst bundle along the budget line when we face a non-convex indifference curve.

The Perfect Complements Case

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=\min \{ax_1,x_2\} \]

The most preferred affordable bundle \((x_1^*,x_2^*)\) satisfies \[ x_2^*=ax_1^*\\ p_1x_1^*+p_2x_2^*=m \]

Three Steps to Find the Optimal Choice of the Consumer

  1. Draw the budget set.
  2. Draw the indifference curves.
  3. Locate the point of optimal choice and calculate the solution.

If you know some more math, you may use Lagrangian.

Demand

Own-Price Changes

How does \(x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)\) change as \(p_1\) changes holding \(p_2\) and \(y\) constant

The curve containing all the utility-maximizing bundles traced out as \(p_1\) changes with \(p_2\) and \(y\) constant is the \(p_1\)-price offer curve.

The plot of the \(x_1\)-coordinate of the \(p_1\)-price offer curve against \(p_1\) is the demand curve for commodity 1.

The Case of Cobb-Douglas Utility Function

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=x_1^ax_2^b \]

Then the demand functions for commodities 1 and 2 are \[\begin{aligned} x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\frac{a}{a+b}\cdot\frac{y}{p_1}\\ x_2^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\frac{b}{a+b}\cdot\frac{y}{p_2} \end{aligned}\] Note that \(x_2^*\) does not vary with \(p_1\) so the \(p_1\)-price offer curve is flat and the demand curve for commodity 1 is a hyperbola.

The Case of Perfect-Complements Utility Function

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=\min \{x_1,x_2\} \]

Then the demand functions for commodities 1 and 2 are \[ x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)=x_2^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\frac{y}{p_1+p_2} \] With \(p_2\) and \(y\) fixed, higher \(p_1\) causes smaller \(x_1^*\) and \(x_2^*\).

The Case of Perfect-Substitutes Utility Function

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=x_1+x_2 \]

Then the demand functions for commodities 1 and 2 are \[\begin{aligned} x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\begin{cases} 0,\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1>p_2\\ \frac{y}{p_1},\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1<p_2 \end{cases}\\ x_2^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\begin{cases} 0,\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1<p_2\\ \frac{y}{p_2},\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1>p_2 \end{cases} \end{aligned}\]

Inverse Demand Function

Taking quantity demanded as given and then asking what must be price describes the inverse demand function of a commodity.

A Cobb-Douglas example

The demand function \[ x_1^*=\frac{ay}{(a+b)p_1} \]

The inverse demand function \[ p_1=\frac{ay}{(a+b)x_1^*} \]

Income Changes

How does \(x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)\) change as \(y\) changes holding \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) constant

The Engel Curve

A plot of quantity demanded against income is called an Engel curve.

The Case of Cobb-Douglas Utility Function

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=x_1^ax_2^b \]

The demand equations are \[\begin{aligned} x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\frac{a}{a+b}\cdot\frac{y}{p_1}\\ x_2^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\frac{b}{a+b}\cdot\frac{y}{p_2} \end{aligned}\] Rearranged to isolate \(y\) \[\begin{aligned} y=\frac{(a+b)p_1}{a}x_1^*\\ y=\frac{(a+b)p_1}{b}x_2^* \end{aligned}\] which are engel curves for good 1 and good 2.

The Case of Perfect-Complements Utility Function

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=\min \{x_1,x_2\} \]

The demand equations are \[ x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)=x_2^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\frac{y}{p_1+p_2} \] Rearranged to isolate \(y\) \[\begin{aligned} y=(p_1+p_2)x_1^*\\ y=(p_1+p_2)x_2^*\\ \end{aligned}\] which are engel curves for good 1 and good 2.

The Case of Perfect-Substitutes Utility Function

\[ U(x_1,x_2)=x_1+x_2 \]

Then the demand functions for commodities 1 and 2 are \[\begin{aligned} x_1^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\begin{cases} 0,\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1>p_2\\ \frac{y}{p_1},\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1<p_2 \end{cases}\\ x_2^*(p_1,p_2,y)=\begin{cases} 0,\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1<p_2\\ \frac{y}{p_2},\quad &\text{if}\quad p_1>p_2 \end{cases} \end{aligned}\] Suppose \(p_1<p_2\) then \(x_1^*=\frac{y}{p_1}\) and \(x_2^*=0\) \[ y=p_1x_1^*\qquad x_2^*=0 \]

Homotheticity

In the three examples above the Engel curves have been straight lines. But it is not true in general.

Engel curves are straight lines if the consumer's preferences are homothetic.

A consumer's preferences are homothetic if and only if \[ (x_1,x_2)\succ(y_1,y_2)\iff(kx_1,kx_2)\succ(ky_1,ky_2)\quad \text{for every }k>0 \] Note that homotheticity also means that the consumer's MRS is the same anywhere on a straight line drawn from the origin.

A Nonhomothetic Example

Quasi-linear preferences are not homothetic. \[ U(x_1,x_2)=f(x_1)+x_2 \]

Normal vs. Inferior & Ordinary vs. Giffen

A good for which quantity demanded rises with income is called normal.

  • Therefore a normal good's Engel curve is positively sloped.

A good for which quantity demanded falls as income increases is called inferior.

  • Therefore an income inferior good's Engel curve is negatively sloped.

A good is called ordinary if the quantity demanded of it always increases as its own price decreases.

  • Downward-sloping demand curve

If the quantity demanded of a good rises as its own-price increases then the good is called Giffen.

  • Demand curve has a positively sloped part

Cross-Price Effects

  • If an increase in \(p_2\) increases demand for commodity 1, then commodity 1 is a gross substitute for commodity 2.
  • If an increase in \(p_2\) reduces demand for commodity 1, then commodity 1 is a gross complement for commodity 2.

A perfect-complements example

\[ x_1^*=\frac{y}{p_1+p_2} \]

so \[ \frac{\partial x_1^*}{\partial p_2}=-\frac{y}{(p_1+p_2)^2}<0 \] Therefore commodity 2 is a gross complement for commodity 1.

A Cobb-Douglas example

\[ x_2^*=\frac{by}{(a+b)p_2} \]

so \[ \frac{\partial x_2^*}{\partial p_1}=0 \] Therefore commodity 1 is neither a gross complement nor a gross substitute for commodity 2.

Lecture 4: Slutsky Equation & Buying and Selling & Intertemporal Choice

Slutsky Equation

We decompose the changes in demand due to price changes into two effects

  • Substitution effect
  • Income effect

\[ \text{Total Effect}=\text{Substitution Effect}+\text{Income Effect} \] This is called Slutsky decomposition

Effects of a Price Change

Suppose consumer's budget is \(y\). Lower price for commodity 1 pivots the constraint outwards.

We add an intermediate step, with the new price and income \(y'\) under which the old choice is just affordable.

Substitution Effect

What is the change in demand when the consumer's income is adjusted so that, at the new prices, she can only just buy the original bundle

  • That is, nominal income is adjusted s.t. purchasing power remains unchanged.

Lower \(p_1\) makes good 1 cheaper and causes a substitution from good 2 to good 1. This is called the substitution effect. Pure Substitution Effect is always negative, which means the new point must be on the right-hand-side of the original one.

Slutsky's Effects for Normal Goods

If Good 1 is normal, substitution and income effects on \(x_1\) of a drop in \(p_1\) reinforce each other.

  • \(x_1\) must increase
  • total effect on \(x_2\) is ambiguous

Since both effects increase demand when own-price falls, a normal good's demand curve slopes down. The Law of Downward-Sloping Demand therefore always applies to normal goods.

  • a normal good must be ordinary & a Giffen good must be inferior

Slutsky's Effects for Inferior Goods

The substitution effect is as for a normal good. But the income effect works in the opposite direction. - total effect on \(x_1\) is ambiguous

In cases of extreme inferiority, the income effect may dominate the substitution effect, causing quantity demanded to fall as own-price rises. Such goods are Giffen goods.

Endowments

The list of resource units with which a consumer starts is his endowment.

A consumer's endowment will be denoted by the vector \(\boldsymbol\omega\).

  • \(\boldsymbol \omega=(\omega_1,\omega_2)\) states that the consumer is endowed with \(\omega_1\) units of good 1 and \(\omega_2\) units of good 2.

Budget Constraints Revisited

Given \(p_1\) and \(p_2\), the budget constraint for a consumer with an endowment \(\boldsymbol \omega=(\omega_1,\omega_2)\) is \[ p_1x_1+p_2x_2\le p_1\omega_1+p_2\omega_2 \]

And the budget set is \[ \{(x_1,x_2)|p_1x_1+p_2x_2\le p_1\omega_1+p_2\omega_2,x_1\ge0,x_2\ge0\} \]

The endowment point is always on the budget constraint.

  • So the price changes pivot the constraint about the endowment point.

The budget line \[ p_1x_1+p_2x_2=p_1\omega_1+p_2\omega_2 \] can be written as \[ p_1(x_1-\omega_1)+p_2(x_2-\omega_2)=0 \] That is, the sum of the values of a consumer's net demands is zero.

Price-offer curve contains all the utility-maximizing gross demands.

Income from Labor

A worker is endowed with \(m\) of nonlabor income and \(\bar R\) hours of time which can be used for labor or leisure. \[ \boldsymbol\omega=(\bar R,m) \]

We suppose the consumption good's price is \(p_c\) and wage rate is \(w\).

Labor Supply

The worker's budget constraint is \[ p_cC\le w(\bar R-R)+m \] where \(C\) and \(R\) denote gross demands for the consumption good and for leisure respectively

An rearranged form with expenditure and endowment value \[ p_cC+wR\le w\bar R+m \]

The buget line \[ C=-\frac{w}{p_c}R+\frac{w\bar R+m}{p_c} \] Note that the point \((\bar R,\frac{m}{p_c})\) is always on the budget constraint and \(\text{slope}=-\frac{w}{p_c}\) is the real wage rate.

Slutsky's Equation Revisited

Changes to demands caused by a price change are the sum of a substitution effect and an income effect.

This assumed that income \(y\) does NOT change as prices changed. But now \[ y=p_1\omega_1+p_2\omega_2 \] does change with prices.

Overall change in demand caused by a change in price is the sum of

  • Substitution effect
  • Ordinary income effect
  • Endowment income effect

where Ordinary income effect and Endowment income effect are together called Income Effect.

Effect on Real Income

  • A drop in \(p_1\) will decrease the consumer's real income if he/she is a seller of \(x_1\).
  • A drop in \(p_1\) will increase the consumer's real income if he/she is a buyer of \(x_1\).

Intertemporal Choice

We apply our method of consumer choices to consumption choices over time

  • Saving vs. borrowing

Present and Future Values

Let \(r\) denote the interest rate between two periods 1 and 2.

Every consumer can give the bank \(s\) in period 1. The bank gives back \(s(1+r)\) in period 2.

  • \(s>0\) indicates saving & \(s<0\) indicates borrowing

Future Value

The future value of \(m\) in period 1 is \(\text{FV}=m(1+r)\).

Present Value

The present value of \(m\) in period 2 is \(\text{PV}=\frac{m}{1+r}\).

The Intertemporal Choice Problem

Notations

  • \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) – incomes received in periods 1 and 2
  • \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) – aggregate consumption in periods 1 and 2
  • \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) – aggregate price in periods 1 and 2

Intertemporal Budget Constraint

The saving in period 1 is denoted as \(s\)

  • Budget in period 1 \[ p_1c_1+s\le m_1 \]

  • Budget in period 2 \[ p_2c_2\le m_2+(1+r)s \]

Combining these two budgets, we get \[ (1+r)p_1c_1+p_2c_2\le(1+r)m_1+m_2 \] which is the intertemporal budget constraint expressed in future value

Equivalently, we can write down the intertemporal budget constraint expressed in present value \[ p_1c_1+\frac{p_2}{1+r}c_2\le m_1+\frac{m_2}{1+r} \]

Intertemporal Budget Line

\[ (1+r)p_1c_1+p_2c_2=(1+r)m_1+m_2 \]

The slope of intertemporal budget line \[ \text{slope}=-(1+r)\frac{p_1}{p_2} \]

Note that the point \((\frac{m_1}{p_1},\frac{m_2}{p_2})\) is always on the budget constraint.

Inflation

Define the inflation rate \(\pi\) as \[ 1+\pi=\frac{p_2}{p_1} \]

  • E.g. \(\pi=0.05\) means 5% inflation

We can define the amount of consumption that costs $1 in period 1 as one basket. Then price of \(c_1\) is $1 per basket. Price of a basket of consumption in period 2 is \(p_2=1+\pi\).

Then the budget constraint can be written as \[ c_1+\frac{1+\pi}{1+r}c_2\le m_1+\frac{m_2}{1+r} \] The slope of the budget line is \[ \text{slope}=-\frac{1+r}{1+\pi} \]

Real Interest Rate

We define the real interest rate \[ \rho=\frac{r-\pi}{1+\pi} \] which satisfies the equation \[ 1+\rho=\frac{1+r}{1+\pi} \]

So the slope of the intertemporal budget line is \(-(1+\rho)\).

Comparative Statics

Saver with a decrease in \(r\)

  • Saving may increase or decrease. The consumer may even become a borrower.

Borrower with a decrease in \(r\)

  • Borrowing may increase or decrease. But the consumer remains a borrower.

Lecture 5: Consumer's Surplus & Market Demand & Equilibrium

Consumer's Surplus

Welfare Measure

We already have the notion of utility to measure consumer welfare, although ordinally.

However, we want to develop a measure of consumer welfare in the unit of money.

Monetary Measure of Welfare

Consider a consumer with quasi-linear utility function \[ u(x,y)=v(x)+y \] where

  • \(x\) denotes the amount of rice consumption (in kg)
  • \(y\) denotes the amount of money left for all other goods

For the purpose of illustration, let's consider the case where \(x\) is restricted to natural numbers. Then in general, the monetary equivalent of the nth kg of rice is \[ r_n\triangleq v(n)-v(n-1) \] because we have \(u(n,m)=u(n-1,m+r_n)\).

And we call \(r_n\) the consumer's reservation price of the nth kg of rice.

  • \(r_1+\dots+r_n=v(n)-v(0)\) is therefore the monetary equivalent of getting n kg of rice for free
  • \(r_1+\dots+r_n-p\cdot n\) is the monetary equivalent of getting n kg of rice at the price \(p\) per kg

Generally, we define the reservation price as \[ r(t)\triangleq v'(t) \] And we thus define the monetary equivalent as \[ \int_{0}^q(v'(x)-p)dx=(v(q)-v(0))-p\cdot q \]

Consumer Surplus for Quasi-linear Utility

We simply define consumer surplus (CS) as the area of the region surrounded by the vertical axis, quantity line, reservation price curve, and price line.

Under the assumption of quasi-linear utility function, the reservation price curve is the same as the demand curve, both are given by \(p=v'(q)\).

The demand curved is defined as follows \[ \max_{q,y}(v(q)+y)\qquad\text{s.t.}\qquad p\cdot q+y\le m \] And since the solution satisties the equation \[ p\cdot q+y=m \] the problem can be reformulated as \[ \max_q(v(q)+m-p\cdot q) \] which gives the solution under FOC \[ v'(q)=p \]

Consumer's Surplus

Without the quasi-linear assumption, the notion of reservation price is controversial, since the consumer's willingness to pay for a unit of rice usually depends on the consumer's income level due to income effect.

But since demand is always well-defined, even without the quasi-linear assumption, we can define consumer surplus as the area of the region surrounded by the vertical axis, quantity line, demand curve, and price line.

  • Under the quasi-linear assumption, CS is equivalent to the consumer's utility gain from purchasing the good.
  • In general, CS can be regarded as a monetary measure of welfare change that is reasonably accurate if the income effect is not that significant.

Compensating Variation

Another monetary measure of welfare change is known as Compensating Variation (CV).

CV is level of income increase under the new price that will make the consumer as well off as before.

Equivalent Variation

EV is level of income decrease under the old price that will make the consumer as well off as under the new price.

CS, CV, and EV

Under a quasi-linear utility function and interior solution, the three concepts are equivalent. \[ U(x_1,x_2)=v(x_1)+x_2 \]

Consider the change in Consumer's Surplus \[ \text{CS}(p_1)=v(x_1)-v(0)-p_1x_1 \] The monetary equivalent of welfare loss measured by CS is \[ -\Delta\text{CS}=(v(x_1')-v(0)-p_1'x_1')-(v(x_1'')-v(0)-p_1''x_1'')=v(x_1')-v(x_1'')-(p_1'x_1'-p_1''x_1'') \]

Now consider the monetary equivalent of welfare loss measured by CV. The consumer's utility for given \(p_1\) is \[ v(x_1^*(p_1))+m-p_1x_1^*(p_1) \] And CV is the extra income which, at the new prices, makes the consumer's utility the same as at the old prices. That is \[ v(x_1')+m-p_1'x_1'=v(x_1'')+m+\text{CV}-p_1''x_1'' \] thus \[ \text{CV}=v(x_1')-v(x_1'')-(p_1'x_1'-p_1''x_1'')=-\Delta\text{CS} \]

Now consider the monetary equivalent of welfare loss measured by EV. The consumer's utility for given \(p_1\) is \[ v(x_1^*(p_1))+m-p_1x_1^*(p_1) \] And EV is the reduction in income which, at the old prices, makes the consumer's utility the same as at the new prices. That is \[ v(x_1'')+m-p_1''x_1''=v(x_1')+m-\text{EV}-p_1'x_1' \] thus \[ \text{EV}=v(x_1')-v(x_1'')-(p_1'x_1'-p_1''x_1'')=-\Delta\text{CS} \]

To sum up, with quasi-linear utility, we have \[ \text{CV}=\text{EV}=-\Delta\text{CS} \]

Without quasi-linear utility, it can be shown that the welfare change measured in CS lies between CV and EV, and the difference is usually relatively small. The concept of Consumer's Surplus is generally designed for partial equilibrium model.

Producer's Surplus

Changes in a firm's welfare can be measured in the unit of money similarly to a consumer's welfare.

Producer's Surplus is defined as the difference between Revenue and Variable Cost, where VC is the sum (or integral) of the marginal costs.

  • Note that Fixed Cost is not considered in Producer's Surplus. And Profit is more natural to measure producers' welfare.

Market Demand

From Individual to Market Demand Functions

Think of an economy containing n consumers, denoted by \(i=1,\dots,n\). The demand function for Commodity \(j\) of Consumer \(i\) is denoted as \(x_j^{i}(p_1,p_2,m^i)\).

Thus the market demand function for commodity \(j\) is \[ X_j(p_1,p_2,m^1,\dots,m^n)=\sum_{i=1}^nx_j^{i}(p_1,p_2,m^i) \]

Note that it's the horizontal sum of the demand curves of individuals.

Elasticity

Elasticity measures the sensitivity of one variable with respect to another.

  • The elasticity of variable X with respect to variable Y is \[ \varepsilon_{x,y}=\frac{\Delta x/x}{\Delta y/y} \]

Own-Price Elasticity

\[ \varepsilon_{X_i^*,p_i}=\frac{\frac{dX_i^*}{X_i^*}}{\frac{dp_i}{p_i}}=\frac{p_i}{X_i^*}\cdot\frac{dX_i^*}{dp_i}=\frac{d\log X_i^*}{d\log p_i} \]

Suppose \(p_i=a-bX_i^*\), then \(X_i^*=\frac{a-p_i}{b}\) and \(\frac{dX_i^*}{dp_i}=-\frac{1}{b}\). Therefore \[ \varepsilon_{X_i^*,p_i}=\frac{p_i}{(a-p_i)/b}\cdot(-\frac{1}{b})=-\frac{p_i}{a-p_i} \]

Suppose \(X_i^*=k\cdot p_i^a\), then \(\frac{dX_i^*}{dp_i}=k\cdot a\cdot p_i^{a-1}\). Therefore \[ \varepsilon_{X_i^*,p_i}=\frac{p_i}{k\cdot p_i^a}\cdot k\cdot a\cdot p_i^{a-1}=a \] That is, the Own-Price Elasticity is a constant everywhere along the demand curve.

Revenue and Own-Price Elasticity of Demand

Monopoly's revenue is \[ R(p)=p\cdot X^*(p) \] And we have \[ \frac{dR}{dp}=X^*(p)+p\cdot \frac{dX^*}{dp}=X^*(p)\cdot(1+\frac{p}{X^*(p)}\frac{dX^*}{dp})=X^*(p)\cdot(1+\varepsilon) \]

  • If \(\varepsilon=-1\) then \(\frac{dR}{dp}=0\), which means a small change in price at a point where \(\varepsilon=-1\) does not change sellers' revenue.

  • If \(\varepsilon>-1\) then \(\frac{dR}{dp}>0\), which means a price increase raises sellers' revenue.

  • If \(\varepsilon<-1\) then \(\frac{dR}{dp}<0\), which means a price increase reduces sellers' revenue.

We may also express revenue as a function of the quantity sold using the inverse demand function \(p(q)\) \[ R(q)=p(q)\cdot q \] And a seller's marginal revenue is the rate at which revenue changes with \(q\) \[ MR(q)=\frac{dR(q)}{dq}=\frac{dp(q)}{dq}\cdot q+p(q)=p(q)\cdot(1+\frac{q}{p(q)}\frac{dp(q)}{dq})=p(q)\cdot(1+\frac{1}{\varepsilon}) \]

  • If \(\varepsilon=-1\) then \(MR(q)=0\)

  • If \(\varepsilon>-1\) then \(MR(q)<0\)

  • If \(\varepsilon<-1\) then \(MR(q)>0\)

Equilibrium

A market is in equilibrium when total quantity demanded by buyers equals total quantity supplied by sellers.

Quantity Taxes

A quantity tax levied at a rate of t is a tax of t paid on each unit traded.

  • If the tax is levied on sellers then it is an excise tax, i.e. price includes tax.
  • If the tax is levied on buyers then it is a sales tax, i.e. price does not include tax.

We care about the five questions below

  • What is the effect of a quantity tax on a market's equilibrium
  • How are prices affected
  • How is the quantity traded affected
  • Who pays the tax
  • How are gains-to-trade altered

A tax rate \(t\) makes the price paid by buyers \(p_b\) higher by \(t\) from the price received by sellers \(p_s\)

Even with a tax the market must clear, i.e. quantity demanded by buyers at price \(p_b\) must equal quantity supplied by sellers at price \(p_s\). \[ D(p_b)=S(p_s) \]

Then we know that \[ p_b-p_s=t\qquad\text{and}\qquad D(p_b)=S(p_s) \] describe the market's equilibrium. Note that these conditions apply no matter if the tax is levied on sellers or on buyers.

Therefore, a sales tax rate t has the same effect as an excise tax rate t.

Quantity Taxes & Market Equilibrium

  • An excise tax raises the market supply curve by $t, raises the buyers' price and reduces the quantity traded.
  • An sales tax lowers the market demand curve by $t, lowers the sellers' price and reduces the quantity traded.

Who actually Pays the Tax

The division of the $t between buyers and sellers is the incidence of the tax.

Suppose the market demand and supply curves are linear. \[ D(p_b)=a-b\cdot p_b\qquad S(p_s)=c+d\cdot p_s \] With the tax, the market equilibrium satisfies \[ p_b=p_s+t\qquad\text{and}\qquad a-b\cdot p_b=c+d\cdot p_s \] which gives the solution \[ p_s=\frac{a-c-bt}{b+d}\qquad p_b=\frac{a-c+dt}{b+d} \] and the quantity traded at equilibrium is \[ q^t=D(p_b)=S(p_s)=\frac{ad+bc-bdt}{b+d} \] And we can conclude that as \(t\) increases, \(p_s\) falls, \(p_b\) rises, and \(q^t\) falls.

  • The tax paid per unit by the buyer is \[ p_b-p^*=\frac{a-c+dt}{b+d}-\frac{a-c}{b+d}=\frac{dt}{b+d} \]

  • The tax paid per unit by the seller is \[ p^*-p_s=\frac{a-c}{b+d}-\frac{a-c-bt}{b+d}=\frac{bt}{b+d} \]

  • The total tax paid (by buyers and sellers combined) is \[ T=t\cdot q^t=t\cdot\frac{ad+bc-bdt}{b+d} \]

Tax Incidence and Own-Price Elasticities

We know the change to buyers' price is \(p_b-p^*>0\) and the change to quantity demanded is \(\Delta q=q^*-q^t\), and around \(p=p^*\) the own-price elasticity of demand is approximately \[ \varepsilon_D\approx\frac{\Delta q/q^*}{(p_b-p^*)/p^*} \] which shows that \[ p_b-p^*\approx\frac{\Delta q\cdot p^*}{\varepsilon_D\cdot q^*} \]

We know the change to sellers' price is \(p_s-p^*<0\) and the change to quantity demanded is \(\Delta q=q^*-q^t\), and around \(p=p^*\) the own-price elasticity of supply is approximately \[ \varepsilon_S\approx\frac{\Delta q/q^*}{(p_s-p^*)/p^*} \] which shows that \[ p_s-p^*\approx\frac{\Delta q\cdot p^*}{\varepsilon_S\cdot q^*} \]

Therefore we have the tax incidence \[ \text{Tax Incidence}=\frac{p_b-p^*}{p^*-p_s}\approx-\frac{\varepsilon_D}{\varepsilon_S} \] which means that the inelastic side will bear more tax

Deadweight Loss and Own-Price Elasticities

Deadweight loss due to a quantity tax rises as either market demand or market supply becomes more elastic. And if either \(\varepsilon_D=0\) or \(\varepsilon_S=0\) then the deadweight loss is zero.

Lecture 6: Technology & Profit-Maximization

Look at Producers

  1. We work on producer's constraint, which is determined by technology
  2. We study producer's objective function, which is the profit function
  3. We examine producer's choice, which is his optimal production plan

Technology

Technology possessed by the producer determines what the producer can do. Particularly, it determines how many units of output can be produced using some given amount of input.

Production Functions

The technology's production function states the maximum amount of output possible from an input bundle. \[ y\le f(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) \] where \(y\) denotes the output level and the input bundle is a vector of the input levels \((x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)\)

Technology Sets

A production plan is an input bundle and an output level \((x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n,y)\)

A production plan is feasible if \[ y\le f(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) \]

The collection of all feasible production plans is the technology set \[ T=\{(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n,y)|y\le f(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)\text{ and }x_1\ge0,x_2\ge0,\dots,x_n\ge0\} \]
